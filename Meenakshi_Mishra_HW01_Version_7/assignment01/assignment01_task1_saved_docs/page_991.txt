			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Performance per watt</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Computing" title="Computing">computing</a>, <b>performance per watt</b> is a measure of the <a href="/wiki/Energy_conversion_efficiency" title="Energy conversion efficiency">energy efficiency</a> of a particular <a href="/wiki/Computer_architecture" title="Computer architecture">computer architecture</a> or <a href="/wiki/Computer_hardware" title="Computer hardware">computer hardware</a>. Literally, it measures the <a href="/wiki/Computer_performance" title="Computer performance">rate of computation</a> that can be delivered by a computer for every <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watt</a> of power consumed. Performance (unlike watt) isn't an objective thing, it depends on what the computer is working on, but the <a href="/wiki/Green500" class="mw-redirect" title="Green500">Green500</a> list ranks the most efficient computers, according to one (possible) <a href="/wiki/Benchmark_(computing)" title="Benchmark (computing)">benchmark</a>.</p>
<p>System designers building <a href="/wiki/Parallel_computing" title="Parallel computing">parallel computers</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Google_search_technology#Production_hardware" class="mw-redirect" title="Google search technology">Google's hardware</a>, pick CPUs based on their (other than Green500) performance per watt of power, because the cost of powering the CPU outweighs the cost of the CPU itself.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The performance and power consumption metrics used depend on the definition; reasonable measures of performance are <a href="/wiki/FLOPS" title="FLOPS">FLOPS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Instructions_per_second" title="Instructions per second">MIPS</a>, or the score for any <a href="/wiki/Benchmark_(computing)" title="Benchmark (computing)">performance benchmark</a>. Several measures of power usage may be employed, depending on the purposes of the metric; for example, a metric might only consider the electrical power delivered to a machine directly, while another might include all power necessary to run a computer, such as cooling and monitoring systems. The power measurement is often the average power used while running the benchmark, but other measures of power usage may be employed (e.g. peak power, idle power).</p>
<p>For example, the early <a href="/wiki/UNIVAC_I" title="UNIVAC I">UNIVAC I</a> computer performed approximately 0.015 operations per watt-second (performing 1,905 operations per second (OPS), while consuming 125&#160;kW). The <a href="/wiki/Fujitsu" title="Fujitsu">Fujitsu</a> <a href="/wiki/FR-V" class="mw-redirect" title="FR-V">FR-V</a> <a href="/wiki/VLIW" class="mw-redirect" title="VLIW">VLIW</a>/<a href="/wiki/Vector_processor" title="Vector processor">vector processor</a> <a href="/wiki/System_on_a_chip" title="System on a chip">system on a chip</a> in the 4 FR550 core variant released 2005 performs 51 Giga-OPS with 3 watts of power consumption resulting in 17 billion operations per watt-second.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This is an improvement by over a trillion times in 54 years.</p>
<p>Most of the power a computer uses is converted into heat, so a system that takes fewer watts to do a job will require less cooling to maintain a given <a href="/wiki/Operating_temperature" title="Operating temperature">operating temperature</a>. Reduced cooling demands makes it easier to <a href="/wiki/Quiet_PC" title="Quiet PC">quiet a computer</a>. Lower energy consumption can also make it less costly to run, and reduce the environmental impact of powering the computer (see <a href="/wiki/Green_computing" title="Green computing">green computing</a>). If installed where there is limited <a href="/wiki/Climate_control" class="mw-redirect" title="Climate control">climate control</a>, a lower power computer will operate at a lower temperature, which may make it more reliable. In a climate controlled environment, reductions in direct power use may also create savings in climate control energy.</p>
<p>Computing energy consumption is sometimes also measured by reporting the energy required to run a particular benchmark, for instance <a href="/wiki/EEMBC" title="EEMBC">EEMBC</a> EnergyBench. Energy consumption figures for a standard workload may make it easier to judge the effect of an improvement in energy efficiency.</p>
<p>Performance (in operations/second) per watt can also be written as operations/watt-second, or operations/joule, since 1 watt = 1 joule/second.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="FLOPS_per_watt">FLOPS per watt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: FLOPS per watt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="MFLOPS_PER_WATT"></span><span id="GFLOPS_PER_WATT"></span></p>

<p><b><a href="/wiki/FLOPS" title="FLOPS">FLOPS</a></b> (<a href="/wiki/Floating_point" title="Floating point">Floating Point</a> Operations Per Second) <b>per watt</b> is a common measure. Like the FLOPS it is based on, the metric is usually applied to <a href="/wiki/Scientific_computing" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific computing">scientific computing</a> and simulations involving many <a href="/wiki/Floating_point" title="Floating point">floating point</a> calculations.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of June 2016<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the <a href="/wiki/Green500" class="mw-redirect" title="Green500">Green500</a> list rates the two most efficient supercomputers highest&#160;–  those are both based on the same <a href="/wiki/Manycore" class="mw-redirect" title="Manycore">manycore</a> accelerator <a href="/w/index.php?title=PEZY-SCnp&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="PEZY-SCnp (page does not exist)">PEZY-SCnp</a> Japanese technology in addition to Intel Xeon processors&#160;–  both at <a href="/wiki/RIKEN" class="mw-redirect" title="RIKEN">RIKEN</a>, the top one at 6673.8&#160;MFLOPS/watt; and the third ranked is the Chinese-technology <a href="/wiki/Sunway_TaihuLight" title="Sunway TaihuLight">Sunway TaihuLight</a> (a much bigger machine, that is the highest ranked on <a href="/wiki/TOP500" title="TOP500">TOP500</a>, the others are not on that list) at 6051.3&#160;MFLOPS/watt.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2012, the Green500 list rated <a href="/wiki/Blue_Gene/Q" class="mw-redirect" title="Blue Gene/Q">BlueGene/Q, Power BQC 16C</a> as the most efficient supercomputer on the TOP500 in terms of FLOPS per watt, running at 2,100.88&#160;MFLOPS/watt.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 9 June 2008, CNN reported that <a href="/wiki/IBM_Roadrunner" title="IBM Roadrunner">IBM's Roadrunner</a> supercomputer achieves 376&#160;MFLOPS/watt.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 2010, IBM machine, <a href="/wiki/IBM_Blue_Gene#Blue_Gene.2FQ" class="mw-redirect" title="IBM Blue Gene">Blue Gene/Q</a> achieves 1,684&#160;MFLOPS/watt.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>As part of <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Intel_Tera-Scale" title="Intel Tera-Scale">Tera-Scale</a> research project, the team produced an 80 core CPU that can achieve over 16,000&#160;MFLOPS/watt.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The future of that CPU is not certain.</p>
<p>Microwulf, a low cost desktop <a href="/wiki/Beowulf_(computing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Beowulf (computing)">Beowulf cluster</a> of 4 dual core Athlon 64 x2 3800+ computers, runs at 58&#160;MFLOPS/watt.<sup id="cite_ref-microwulf_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-microwulf-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kalray has developed a 256-core VLIW CPU that achieves 25,000&#160;MFLOPS/watt. Next generation is expected to achieve 75,000&#160;MFLOPS/watt.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Parallella has announced the Epiphany-V, a 1024-core 64-bit RISC processor intended to achieve 75&#160;GFLOPS/Watt.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Green500_List">Green500 List</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Green500 List">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Green500 list ranks computers from the <a href="/wiki/TOP500" title="TOP500">TOP500</a> list of supercomputers in terms of energy efficiency, typically measured as <a href="/wiki/LINPACK" title="LINPACK">LINPACK</a> FLOPS per watt.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of August 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the Shoubu supercomputer of <a href="/wiki/RIKEN" class="mw-redirect" title="RIKEN">RIKEN</a> outside <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> Japan tops the Green500 list with 7032 MFLOPS/W. The top three supercomputers of the list use PEZY-SC accelerators (<a href="/wiki/General-purpose_computing_on_graphics_processing_units" title="General-purpose computing on graphics processing units">GPU</a>-like that use <a href="/wiki/OpenCL" title="OpenCL">OpenCL</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> by <a href="/w/index.php?title=PEZY_Computing&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="PEZY Computing (page does not exist)">PEZY Computing</a> with 1024 cores each and 6-7 GFLOPS/W efficiency.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of November 2014<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the L-CSC supercomputer of the <a href="/wiki/Helmholtz_Association_of_German_Research_Centres" title="Helmholtz Association of German Research Centres">Helmholtz Association</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Gesellschaft_f%C3%BCr_Schwerionenforschung" class="mw-redirect" title="Gesellschaft für Schwerionenforschung">GSI</a> in <a href="/wiki/Darmstadt" title="Darmstadt">Darmstadt</a> Germany topped the Green500 list with 5271 MFLOPS/W and was the first cluster to surpass an efficiency of 5 GFLOPS/W. It runs on <a href="/wiki/List_of_Intel_Xeon_microprocessors#Xeon_E5-26xx_v2_.28dual-processor.29.5B32.5D" title="List of Intel Xeon microprocessors">Intel Xeon E5-2690</a> Processors with the <a href="/wiki/Ivy_Bridge_(microarchitecture)" title="Ivy Bridge (microarchitecture)">Intel Ivy Bridge</a> Architecture and <a href="/wiki/List_of_AMD_graphics_processing_units#FirePro_Server_Series" title="List of AMD graphics processing units">AMD FirePro™ S9150</a> GPU Accelerators. It uses in rack watercooling and <a href="/wiki/Cooling_Tower" class="mw-redirect" title="Cooling Tower">Cooling Towers</a> to reduce the energy required for cooling.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Green500_List_-_November_2014_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Green500_List_-_November_2014-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of June 2013<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the <a href="/wiki/Eurotech_(company)" title="Eurotech (company)">Eurotech</a> supercomputer Eurora at <a href="/wiki/Cineca" class="mw-redirect" title="Cineca">Cineca</a> topped the Green500 list with 3208 LINPACK MFLOPS/W.<sup id="cite_ref-Eurotech_Eurora_tops_green_500_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eurotech_Eurora_tops_green_500-22">[22]</a></sup> The Cineca Eurora supercomputer is equipped with two Intel Xeon E5-2687W CPUs and two PCI-e connected NVIDIA Tesla K20 accelerators per node. Water cooling and electronics design allows for very high densities to be reached with a peak performance of 350 TFlop/s per rack.<sup id="cite_ref-top_green500Aurora_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-top_green500Aurora-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of November 2012<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, an <a href="/wiki/Appro_International,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Appro International, Inc.">Appro International, Inc.</a> Xtreme-X supercomputer (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beacon_(supercomputer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Beacon (supercomputer) (page does not exist)">Beacon</a></i>) topped the Green500 list with 2499 LINPACK MFLOPS/W.<sup id="cite_ref-nics_green500_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nics_green500-24">[24]</a></sup> Beacon is deployed by NICS of the University of Tennessee and is a GreenBlade GB824M, <a href="/wiki/Xeon#E5-16xx.2F24xx.2F26xx.2F46xx-series_.22Sandy_Bridge-EP.22" title="Xeon">Xeon E5-2670</a> based, eight cores (8C), 2.6&#160;GHz, Infiniband FDR, Intel Xeon Phi 5110P computer.<sup id="cite_ref-top_green500_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-top_green500-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="GPU_efficiency">GPU efficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: GPU efficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Graphics_processing_unit" title="Graphics processing unit">Graphics processing units</a> (GPU) have continued to increase in energy usage, while CPUs designers have recently focused on improving performance per watt. High performance GPUs may draw large amount of power and hence, intelligent techniques are required to manage GPU power consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Measures like <a href="/wiki/3DMark" title="3DMark">3DMark2006 score</a> per watt can help identify more efficient GPUs.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> However that may not adequately incorporate efficiency in typical use, where much time is spent doing less demanding tasks.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>With modern GPUs, energy usage is an important constraint on the maximum computational capabilities that can be achieved. GPU designs are usually highly scalable, allowing the manufacturer to put multiple chips on the same video card, or to use multiple video cards that work in parallel. Peak performance of any system is essentially limited by the amount of power it can draw and the amount of heat it can dissipate. Consequently, performance per watt of a GPU design translates directly into peak performance of a system that uses that design.</p>
<p>Since GPUs may also be used for some <a href="/wiki/GPGPU" class="mw-redirect" title="GPGPU">general purpose computation</a>, sometimes their performance is measured in terms also applied to CPUs, such as FLOPS per watt.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges">Challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While performance per watt is useful, absolute power requirements are also important. Claims of improved performance per watt may be used to mask increasing power demands. For instance, though newer generation GPU architectures may provide better performance per watt, continued performance increases can negate the gains in efficiency, and the GPUs continue to consume large amounts of power.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Benchmarks that measure power under heavy load may not adequately reflect typical efficiency. For instance, 3DMark stresses the 3D performance of a GPU, but many computers spend most of their time doing less intense display tasks (idle, 2D tasks, displaying video). So the 2D or idle efficiency of the graphics system may be at least as significant for overall energy efficiency. Likewise, systems that spend much of their time in standby or <a href="/wiki/Standby_power" title="Standby power">soft off</a> are not adequately characterized by just efficiency under load. To help address this some benchmarks, like <a href="/wiki/SPECpower" title="SPECpower">SPECpower</a>, include measurements at a series of load levels.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The efficiency of some electrical components, such as <a href="/wiki/Voltage_regulator" title="Voltage regulator">voltage regulators</a>, decreases with increasing temperature, so the power used may increase with temperature. Power supplies, motherboards, and some video cards are some of the subsystems affected by this. So their power draw may depend on temperature, and the temperature or temperature dependence should be noted when measuring.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SPCRNewLevels_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SPCRNewLevels-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Performance per watt also typically does not include full <a href="/wiki/Life_cycle_assessment" class="mw-redirect" title="Life cycle assessment">life-cycle costs</a>. Since computer manufacturing is energy intensive, and computers often have a relatively short lifespan, energy and materials involved in production, distribution, <a href="/wiki/Electronic_waste" title="Electronic waste">disposal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computer_recycling" title="Computer recycling">recycling</a> often make up significant portions of their cost, energy use, and environmental impact.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Energy required for climate control of the computer's surroundings is often not counted in the wattage calculation, but it can be significant.<sup id="cite_ref-impLowPower_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-impLowPower-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_energy_efficiency_measures">Other energy efficiency measures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other energy efficiency measures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>SWaP (space, wattage and performance) is a <a href="/wiki/Sun_Microsystems" title="Sun Microsystems">Sun Microsystems</a> metric for <a href="/wiki/Data_center" title="Data center">data centers</a>, incorporating energy and space.</p>
<p>SWaP = Performance / (Space × Power)</p>
<p>Where performance is measured by any appropriate benchmark, and space is size of the computer.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
