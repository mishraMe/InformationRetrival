A 25-foot (7.6&#160;m) wall of coal <a href="/wiki/Fly_ash" title="Fly ash">fly ash</a> contaminated with toxic heavy metals, resulting from the <a href="/wiki/Kingston_Fossil_Plant_coal_fly_ash_slurry_spill" title="Kingston Fossil Plant coal fly ash slurry spill">release of 5.4 million cubic yards of coal fly ash</a> slurry into the <a href="/wiki/Emory_River" title="Emory River">Emory River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a>, and nearby land and water features, in December 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Testing showed significantly elevated levels of arsenic, copper, <a href="/wiki/Barium" title="Barium">barium</a>, cadmium, chromium, lead, mercury, nickel, and thallium in samples of slurry and river water.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Cleanup costs may exceed $1.2 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></div>

<p>A <b>toxic heavy metal</b> is any relatively dense <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Metalloid" title="Metalloid">metalloid</a> that is noted for its potential toxicity, especially in environmental contexts.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The term has particular application to <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> all of which appear in the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="World Health Organisation">World Health Organisation</a>'s list of 10 chemicals of major public concern. Other examples include <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thallium" title="Thallium">thallium</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_(chemical_element)" class="mw-redirect" title="Heavy metal (chemical element)">Heavy metals</a> are found naturally in the earth. They become concentrated as a result of human caused activities and can enter plant, animal, and human tissues via inhalation, diet, and manual handling. Then, they can bind to and interfere with the functioning of vital cellular components. The toxic effects of arsenic, mercury, and lead were known to the ancients, but methodical studies of the toxicity of some heavy metals appear to date from only 1868. In humans, heavy metal poisoning is generally treated by the administration of <a href="/wiki/Chelation" title="Chelation">chelating agents</a>. Some elements otherwise regarded as toxic heavy metals are essential, in small quantities, for human health.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contamination_sources">Contamination sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toxic_heavy_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Contamination sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tetraethyllead-3D-balls.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Tetraethyllead-3D-balls.png/220px-Tetraethyllead-3D-balls.png" width="220" height="217" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Tetraethyllead-3D-balls.png/330px-Tetraethyllead-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Tetraethyllead-3D-balls.png/440px-Tetraethyllead-3D-balls.png 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="1970" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tetraethyllead-3D-balls.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Tetraethyl_lead" class="mw-redirect" title="Tetraethyl lead">Tetraethyl lead</a> is one of the most significant heavy metal contaminants in recent use.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Heavy metals are found naturally in the earth, and become concentrated as a result of human caused activities. Common sources are from mining and industrial wastes; vehicle emissions; <a href="/wiki/Lead%E2%80%93acid_battery" title="Lead–acid battery">lead-acid batteries</a>; fertilisers; paints; treated woods; aging water supply infrastructure;<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Microplastics" title="Microplastics">microplastics</a> floating in the world's oceans.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Arsenic, cadmium and lead may be present in children's toys at levels that exceed regulatory standards. Lead can be used in toys as a <a href="/wiki/Stabilizer_(chemistry)" title="Stabilizer (chemistry)">stabilizer</a>, color enhancer, or anti-corrosive agent. Cadmium is sometimes employed as a stabilizer, or to increase the mass and luster of toy jewelry. Arsenic is thought to be used in connection with coloring dyes.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Regular imbibers of <a href="/wiki/Moonshine" title="Moonshine">illegally distilled alcohol</a> may be exposed to arsenic or lead poisoning the source of which is arsenic-contaminated lead used to <a href="/wiki/Solder" title="Solder">solder</a> the distilling apparatus. <a href="/wiki/Rat_poison" class="mw-redirect" title="Rat poison">Rat poison</a> used in grain and <a href="/wiki/Mash_ingredients" title="Mash ingredients">mash</a> stores may be another source of the arsenic.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lead is the most prevalent heavy metal contaminant.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> As a component of <a href="/wiki/Tetraethyl_lead" class="mw-redirect" title="Tetraethyl lead">tetraethyl lead</a>, <span class="chemf nowrap">(CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
3</span>CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span>)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
4</span>Pb</span>, it was used extensively in <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> during the 1930s–1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Lead levels in the aquatic environments of industrialised societies have been estimated to be two to three times those of pre-industrial levels.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Although the use of leaded gasoline was largely phased out in North America by 1996, soils next to roads built before this time retain high lead concentrations. Lead (from <a href="/wiki/Lead_azide" class="mw-redirect" title="Lead azide">lead azide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lead_styphnate" title="Lead styphnate">lead styphnate</a> used in firearms) gradually accumulates at firearms training grounds, contaminating the local environment and exposing range employees to a risk of <a href="/wiki/Lead_poisoning" title="Lead poisoning">lead poisoning</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Entry_routes">Entry routes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toxic_heavy_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Entry routes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Heavy metals enter plant, animal and human tissues via air inhalation, diet and manual handling. Motor vehicle emissions are a major source of airborne contaminants including arsenic, cadmium, cobalt, nickel, lead, antimony, vanadium, zinc, platinum, palladium and rhodium.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Water sources (groundwater, lakes, streams and rivers) can be polluted by heavy metals leaching from industrial and consumer waste; <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a> can exacerbate this process by releasing heavy metals trapped in soils.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Plants are exposed to heavy metals through the uptake of water; animals eat these plants; ingestion of plant- and animal-based foods are the largest sources of heavy metals in humans.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Absorption through skin contact, for example from contact with soil, is another potential source of heavy metal contamination.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Toxic heavy metals can <a href="/wiki/Bioaccumulation" title="Bioaccumulation">bioaccumulate</a> in organisms as they are hard to <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolize</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Detrimental_effects">Detrimental effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toxic_heavy_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Detrimental effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Broadly, long-term exposure to toxic heavy metals can have carcinogenic, central and peripheral nervous system and circulatory effects. For humans, typical presentations associated with exposure to any of the "classical"<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> toxic heavy metals, or chromium (another toxic heavy metal) or arsenic (a metalloid), are shown in the table.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toxic_heavy_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The toxic effects of arsenic, mercury and lead were known to the ancients but methodical studies of the overall toxicity of heavy metals appear to date from only 1868. In that year, Wanklyn and Chapman speculated on the adverse effects of the heavy metals "arsenic, lead, copper, zinc, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>" in <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a>. They noted an "absence of investigation" and were reduced to "the necessity of pleading for the collection of data."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In 1884, Blake described an apparent connection between toxicity and the atomic weight of an element.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The following sections provide historical thumbnails for the "classical" toxic heavy metals (arsenic, mercury and lead) and some more recent examples (chromium and cadmium).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arsenic">Arsenic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toxic_heavy_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Arsenic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">Arsenic</a>, as <a href="/wiki/Realgar" title="Realgar">realgar</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">As<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
4</span>S<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
4</span></span>) and <a href="/wiki/Orpiment" title="Orpiment">orpiment</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">As<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span>S<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
3</span></span>), was known in ancient times. <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> (64–50 BCE – c. AD 24?), a Greek geographer and historian,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> wrote that only slaves were employed in realgar and orpiment mines since they would inevitably die from the toxic effects of the fumes given off from the ores. Arsenic-contaminated beer poisoned over 6,000 people in the Manchester area of England in 1900, and is thought to have killed at least 70 victims.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Clare_Luce" class="mw-redirect" title="Clare Luce">Clare Luce</a>, American ambassador to Italy from 1953 to 1956, suffered from <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_poisoning" title="Arsenic poisoning">arsenic poisoning</a>. Its source was traced to flaking arsenic-laden paint on the ceiling of her bedroom. She may also have eaten food contaminated by arsenic in flaking ceiling paint in the embassy dining room.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_contamination_of_groundwater" title="Arsenic contamination of groundwater">Ground water contaminated by arsenic</a>, as of 2014, "is still poisoning millions of people in Asia."<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mercury">Mercury</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toxic_heavy_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mercury">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The first emperor of unified China, <a href="/wiki/Qin_Shi_Huang" title="Qin Shi Huang">Qin Shi Huang</a>, it is reported, died of ingesting <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> pills that were intended to give him eternal life.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The phrase "mad as a hatter" is likely a reference to <a href="/wiki/Mercury_poisoning" title="Mercury poisoning">mercury poisoning</a> among <a href="/wiki/Milliner" class="mw-redirect" title="Milliner">milliners</a> (so-called "<a href="/wiki/Mad_hatter_disease" title="Mad hatter disease">mad hatter disease</a>"), as mercury-based compounds were once used in the manufacture of felt hats in the 18th and 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Historically, gold amalgam (an alloy with mercury) was widely used in <a href="/wiki/Gilding" title="Gilding">gilding</a>, leading to numerous casualties among the workers. It is estimated that during the construction of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Isaac%27s_Cathedral" title="Saint Isaac's Cathedral">Saint Isaac's Cathedral</a> alone, 60 workers died from the gilding of the main dome.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Outbreaks of <a href="/wiki/Methylmercury" title="Methylmercury">methylmercury</a> poisoning occurred in several places in Japan during the 1950s due to industrial discharges of mercury into rivers and coastal waters. The best-known instances were in <a href="/wiki/Minamata" class="mw-redirect" title="Minamata">Minamata</a> and <a href="/wiki/Niigata_Prefecture" title="Niigata Prefecture">Niigata</a>. In Minamata alone, more than 600 people died due to what became known as <a href="/wiki/Minamata_disease" title="Minamata disease">Minamata disease</a>. More than 21,000 people filed claims with the Japanese government, of which almost 3000 became certified as having the disease. In 22 documented cases, pregnant women who consumed contaminated fish showed mild or no symptoms but gave birth to infants with severe developmental disabilities.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Since the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">industrial Revolution</a>, mercury levels have tripled in many near-surface <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawaters</a>, especially around <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lead">Lead</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toxic_heavy_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lead">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The adverse effects of <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> were known to the ancients. In the 2nd century BC the Greek botanist <a href="/wiki/Nicander" title="Nicander">Nicander</a> described the <a href="/wiki/Colic" title="Colic">colic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paralysis" title="Paralysis">paralysis</a> seen in lead-poisoned people.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pedanius_Dioscorides" title="Pedanius Dioscorides">Dioscorides</a>, a Greek <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physician</a> who is thought to have lived in the 1st century CE,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> wrote that lead "makes the mind give way". Lead was used extensively in <a href="/wiki/Roman_aqueducts" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman aqueducts">Roman aqueducts</a> from about 500 BC to 300 AD.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>'s engineer, <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Vitruvius</a>, reported, "water is much more wholesome from earthenware pipes than from lead pipes. For it seems to be made injurious by lead, because <a href="/wiki/White_lead" title="White lead">white lead</a> is produced by it, and this is said to be harmful to the human body."<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Mongol" class="mw-redirect" title="Mongol">Mongol</a> period in China (1271−1368 AD), lead pollution due to silver smelting in the <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a> region exceeded contamination levels from modern mining activities by nearly four times.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[n 1]</a></sup> In the 17th and 18th centuries, people in <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a> were afflicted by a condition referred to as <a href="/wiki/Devon_colic" title="Devon colic">Devon colic</a>; this was discovered to be due to the imbibing of <a href="/wiki/Lead_poisoning" title="Lead poisoning">lead-contaminated</a> <a href="/wiki/Cider" title="Cider">cider</a>. In 2013, the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> estimated that lead poisoning resulted in 143,000 deaths, and "contribute[d] to 600,000 new cases of children with intellectual disabilities", each year.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup> In the U.S. city of <a href="/wiki/Flint,_Michigan" title="Flint, Michigan">Flint, Michigan</a>, lead contamination in drinking water has been <a href="/wiki/Flint_water_crisis" title="Flint water crisis">an issue since 2014</a>. The source of the contamination has been attributed to "corrosion in the lead and iron pipes that distribute water to city residents".<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup> In 2015, drinking water lead levels in north-eastern <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>, Australia, were reported to reach over 50 times national drinking water guidelines. The source of the contamination was attributed to “a combination of dilapidated drinking water infrastructure, including lead jointed pipelines, end-of-life polyvinyl chloride pipes and household plumbing.”<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chromium">Chromium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toxic_heavy_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Chromium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Chromium(III)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chromium(III)">Chromium(III)</a> compounds and <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a> metal are not considered a health hazard, while the toxicity and carcinogenic properties of <a href="/wiki/Chromium(VI)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chromium(VI)">chromium(VI)</a> have been known since at least the late 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup> In 1890, Newman described the elevated cancer risk of workers in a <a href="/wiki/Chromate" class="mw-redirect" title="Chromate">chromate</a> dye company.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup> Chromate-induced dermatitis was reported in aircraft workers during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> In 1963, an outbreak of dermatitis, ranging from <a href="/wiki/Erythema" title="Erythema">erythema</a> to exudative <a href="/wiki/Eczema" class="mw-redirect" title="Eczema">eczema</a>, occurred amongst 60 automobile factory workers in England. The workers had been wet-sanding chromate-based primer paint that had been applied to car bodies.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup> In Australia, chromium was released from the <a href="/wiki/Newcastle,_New_South_Wales" title="Newcastle, New South Wales">Newcastle</a> <a href="/wiki/Orica" title="Orica">Orica</a> explosives plant on August 8, 2011. Up to 20 workers at the plant were exposed as were 70 nearby homes in <a href="/wiki/Stockton,_New_South_Wales" title="Stockton, New South Wales">Stockton</a>. The town was only notified three days after the release and the accident sparked a major public controversy, with Orica criticised for playing down the extent and possible risks of the leak, and the state Government attacked for their slow response to the incident.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cadmium">Cadmium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toxic_heavy_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cadmium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">Cadmium</a> exposure is a phenomenon of the early 20th century, and onwards. In Japan in 1910, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mitsui_Mining_and_Smelting_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mitsui Mining and Smelting Company (page does not exist)">Mitsui Mining and Smelting Company</a> began discharging cadmium into the Jinzugawa river, as a byproduct of mining operations. Residents in the surrounding area subsequently consumed rice grown in cadmium-contaminated irrigation water. They experienced softening of the bones and kidney failure. The origin of these symptoms was not clear; possibilities raised at the time included "a regional or bacterial disease or lead poisoning."<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup> In 1955, cadmium was identified as the likely cause and in 1961 the source was directly linked to mining operations in the area.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup> In February 2010, cadmium was found in <a href="/wiki/Walmart" title="Walmart">Walmart</a> exclusive <a href="/wiki/Miley_Cyrus" title="Miley Cyrus">Miley Cyrus</a> jewelry. Wal-Mart continued to sell the jewelry until May, when covert testing organised by <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">Associated Press</a> confirmed the original results.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup> In June 2010 cadmium was detected in the paint used on promotional drinking glasses for the movie <a href="/wiki/Shrek_Forever_After" title="Shrek Forever After">Shrek Forever After</a>, sold by <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s_Restaurants" class="mw-redirect" title="McDonald's Restaurants">McDonald's Restaurants</a>, triggering a recall of 12 million glasses.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Remediation">Remediation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toxic_heavy_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Remediation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Metal-EDTA.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Metal-EDTA.svg/170px-Metal-EDTA.svg.png" width="170" height="240" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Metal-EDTA.svg/255px-Metal-EDTA.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Metal-EDTA.svg/340px-Metal-EDTA.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="744" data-file-height="1052" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Metal-EDTA.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A metal EDTA anion. Pb displaces Ca in <span class="chemf nowrap">Na<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span>[CaEDTA]</span> to give <span class="chemf nowrap">Na<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span>[PbEDTA]</span>, which is passed out of the body in <a href="/wiki/Urine" title="Urine">urine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In humans, heavy metal poisoning is generally treated by the administration of <a href="/wiki/Chelation" title="Chelation">chelating agents</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup> These are chemical compounds, such as <span class="chemf nowrap"><a href="/wiki/EDTA" class="mw-redirect" title="EDTA">CaNa2 EDTA</a></span> (calcium disodium ethylenediaminetetraacetate) that convert heavy metals to chemically inert forms that can be excreted without further interaction with the body. Chelates are not without side effects and can also remove beneficial metals from the body. Vitamin and mineral supplements are sometimes co-administered for this reason.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>Soils contaminated by heavy metals can be remediated by one or more of the following technologies: isolation; immobilization; toxicity reduction; physical separation; or extraction. <i>Isolation</i> involves the use of caps, membranes or below-ground barriers in an attempt to quarantine the contaminated soil. <i>Immobilization</i> aims to alter the properties of the soil so as to hinder the mobility of the heavy contaminants. <i>Toxicity reduction</i> attempts to oxidise or reduce the toxic heavy metal ions, via chemical or <a href="/wiki/Phytoremediation" title="Phytoremediation">biological means</a> into less toxic or mobile forms. <i>Physical separation</i> involves the removal of the contaminated soil and the separation of the metal contaminants by mechanical means. <i>Extraction</i> is an on or off-site process that uses chemicals, high-temperature volatization, or electrolysis to extract contaminants from soils. The process or processes used will vary according to contaminant and the characteristics of the site.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">Benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toxic_heavy_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some elements otherwise regarded as toxic heavy metals are <a href="/wiki/Dietary_element" class="mw-redirect" title="Dietary element">essential</a>, in small quantities, for human health. These elements include vanadium, manganese, iron, cobalt, copper, zinc, selenium, strontium and molybdenum.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[56]</a></sup> A deficiency of these essential metals may increase susceptibility to heavy metal poisoning.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[57]</a></sup></p>
