			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Vanadium redox battery</h1>

<p>The <b>vanadium redox battery</b> (VRB) (or <i>Vanadium flow battery</i>) is a type of rechargeable <a href="/wiki/Flow_battery" title="Flow battery">flow battery</a> that employs <a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">vanadium</a> ions in different oxidation states to store chemical potential energy.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The vanadium redox battery exploits the ability of <a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">vanadium</a> to exist in solution in four different <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation states</a>, and uses this property to make a battery that has just one electroactive element instead of two.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> For several reasons, including their relatively bulky size, most vanadium batteries are currently used for <a href="/wiki/Grid_energy_storage" title="Grid energy storage">grid energy storage</a>, such as being attached to power plants or electrical grids.</p>
<p>The first successful demonstration of the all-vanadium redox flow battery which employed vanadium in a solution of sulfuric acid in each half was by Maria Skyllas-Kazacos at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_New_South_Wales" title="University of New South Wales">University of New South Wales</a> in the 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-Rychik_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rychik-5">[5]</a></sup> Her design used sulfuric acid electrolytes, and was patented by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_New_South_Wales" title="University of New South Wales">University of New South Wales</a> in Australia in 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-Skyllas_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Skyllas-2">[2]</a></sup> The possibility of creating a vanadium flow battery was explored variously by Pissoort in 1930's,<sup id="cite_ref-Pissoort_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pissoort-6">[6]</a></sup> NASA researchers in the 1970s, and Pellegri and Spaziante in the 1970s,<sup id="cite_ref-Pelligri_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pelligri-7">[7]</a></sup> but none of them were successful in demonstrating the technology.</p>
<p>Numerous companies and organizations involved in funding and developing vanadium redox batteries include Imergy (formerly Deeya), Vionx (formerly Premium Power), <a href="/wiki/UniEnergy_Technologies" title="UniEnergy Technologies">UniEnergy Technologies</a><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and Ashlawn Energy<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> in the United States; Renewable Energy Dynamics Technology<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> in Ireland; <a href="/wiki/Gildemeister_AG" class="mw-redirect" title="Gildemeister AG">Gildemeister AG</a> (formerly Cellstrom GmbH in Austria) in Germany;<sup id="cite_ref-Cellstrom_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cellstrom-11">[11]</a></sup> Cellennium in Thailand <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.rongkepower.com/index.php/article/show/id/140/language/en">Rongke Power</a>; Prudent Energy in China;<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sumitomo" class="mw-redirect" title="Sumitomo">Sumitomo</a> in Japan; H2, Inc. in South Korea;<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and redT in Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The main advantages of the vanadium redox battery are:</p>
<ul>
<li>It can offer almost unlimited energy capacity simply by using larger electrolyte storage tanks.</li>
<li>It can be left completely discharged for long periods with no ill effects.</li>
<li>If the electrolytes are accidentally mixed, the battery suffers no permanent damage.</li>
<li>A single state of charge between the two electrolytes avoids the capacity degradation due to a single cell in non-flow batteries.</li>
<li>The electrolyte is aqueous and inherently safe and non-flammable.<sup id="cite_ref-UETproducts_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UETproducts-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>The generation 3 formulation using a mixed acid solution developed by the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://energy.gov/sites/prod/files/VRB.pdf">Pacific Northwest National Laboratory</a> operates at a high temperature allowing for passive cooling <sup id="cite_ref-miller2_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-miller2-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The main disadvantages with vanadium redox technology are a relatively poor energy-to-volume ratio in comparison with standard <a href="/wiki/Storage_battery" class="mw-redirect" title="Storage battery">storage batteries</a> (although the Generation 3 formulation has doubled the energy density <sup id="cite_ref-miller2_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-miller2-16">[16]</a></sup> of the system), and the aqueous electrolyte makes the battery heavy and therefore only useful for stationary applications.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation">Operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium_redox_battery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vanadium_battery.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Vanadium_battery.svg/220px-Vanadium_battery.svg.png" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Vanadium_battery.svg/330px-Vanadium_battery.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Vanadium_battery.svg/440px-Vanadium_battery.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="523" data-file-height="380" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vanadium_battery.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Diagram of a Vanadium Flow Battery</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A vanadium redox battery consists of an assembly of power <a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_cell" title="Electrochemical cell">cells</a> in which the two electrolytes are separated by a <a href="/wiki/Proton_exchange_membrane" title="Proton exchange membrane">proton exchange membrane</a>. Both electrolytes are <a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">vanadium</a>-based, the electrolyte in the positive half-cells contains VO<sub>2</sub><sup>+</sup> and VO<sup>2+</sup> ions, the electrolyte in the negative half-cells, V<sup>3+</sup> and V<sup>2+</sup> ions. The electrolytes may be prepared by any of several processes, including electrolytically dissolving <a href="/wiki/Vanadium_pentoxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Vanadium pentoxide">vanadium pentoxide</a> (V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>) in <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> (H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>). The solution remains strongly acidic in use.</p>
<p>In vanadium flow batteries, both half-cells are additionally connected to storage tanks and pumps so that very large volumes of the electrolytes can be circulated through the cell. This circulation of liquid electrolytes is somewhat cumbersome and does restrict the use of vanadium flow batteries in mobile applications, effectively confining them to large fixed installations.</p>
<p>When the vanadium battery is being charged, the VO<sup>2+</sup> ions in the positive half-cell are converted to VO<sub>2</sub><sup>+</sup> ions when electrons are removed from the positive terminal of the battery. Similarly in the negative half-cell, electrons are introduced converting the V<sup>3+</sup> ions into V<sup>2+</sup>. During discharge this process is reversed and results in a typical <a href="/wiki/Open_circuit_voltage" class="mw-redirect" title="Open circuit voltage">open-circuit voltage</a> of 1.41 V at 25&#160;°C.</p>
<p>Other useful properties of vanadium flow batteries are their very fast response to changing loads and their extremely large overload capacities. Studies by the University of New South Wales have shown that they can achieve a response time of under half a millisecond for a 100% load change, and allowed overloads of as much as 400% for 10 seconds. The response time is mostly limited by the electrical equipment. Sulfuric acid-based vanadium batteries only work between about 10 to 40&#160;°C. Below that temperature range, the ion-infused sulfuric acid crystallizes.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Round trip efficiency in practical applications is around 65–75&#160;%.<sup id="cite_ref-vrbpower_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vrbpower-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_improvements">Proposed improvements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium_redox_battery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Proposed improvements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.vrb.unsw.edu.au/about-us/history-of-vanadium-redox-battery.html">Second generation</a> vanadium redox batteries (<a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">vanadium</a>/<a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a>) may approximately double the energy density and increase the temperature range in which the battery can operate.</p>
<p>Despite the traditional pumping requirements, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nanoflowcell.com">nanoFlowcell AG</a> has developed a proprietary system of energy storage for <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric vehicle</a> applications showcased through a number of <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nanoflowcell.com/solutions/">Quant</a> vehicle prototypes, using rapid replacement of electrolyte to refuel the battery.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_density">Energy density</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium_redox_battery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Energy density">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Current production vanadium redox batteries achieve an energy density of about 20&#160;Wh/kg (90 kJ/kg) of electrolyte. More recent research at UNSW indicates that the use of precipitation inhibitors can increase the density to about 35&#160;Wh/kg (126 kJ/kg), with even higher densities made possible by controlling the electrolyte temperature. This <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a> is quite low as compared to other <a href="/wiki/Rechargeable_battery" title="Rechargeable battery">rechargeable battery</a> types (e.g., lead–acid, 30–40&#160;Wh/kg (108–144 kJ/kg); and lithium ion, 80–200&#160;Wh/kg (288–720 kJ/kg)).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium_redox_battery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The extremely large capacities possible from vanadium redox batteries make them well suited to use in large power storage applications such as helping to average out the production of highly variable generation sources such as wind or solar power, helping generators cope with large surges in demand or leveling out supply/demand at a transmission constrained region.</p>
<p>The limited self-discharge characteristics of vanadium redox batteries make them useful in applications where the batteries must be stored for long periods of time with little maintenance while maintaining a ready state. This has led to their adoption in some military electronics, such as the sensor components of the <a href="/wiki/GATOR_mine_system" title="GATOR mine system">GATOR mine system</a>. Their ability to fully cycle and stay at 0% state of charge makes them suitable for solar + storage applications where the battery must start each day empty and fill up depending upon the load and weather. Lithium Ion batteries, for example, are typically damaged when they are allowed to discharge below 20% state of charge, so they typically only operate between about 20% and 100%, meaning they are only using 80% of their nameplate capacity.<sup id="cite_ref-BattpowerOnline_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BattpowerOnline-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Their extremely rapid response times also make them superbly well suited to <a href="/wiki/Uninterruptible_power_supply" title="Uninterruptible power supply">UPS</a> type applications, where they can be used to replace <a href="/wiki/Lead%E2%80%93acid_batteries" class="mw-redirect" title="Lead–acid batteries">lead–acid batteries</a> and even diesel generators. Also the fast response time makes them well-suited for frequency regulation. Economically neither the UPS or frequency regulation applications of the battery are currently sustainable alone, but rather the battery is able to layer these applications with other uses to capitalize on various sources of revenue. Also, these capabilities make Vanadium redox batteries an effective "all-in-one" solution for microgrids that depend on reliable operations, frequency regulation and have a need for load shifting (from either high renewable penetration, a highly variable load or desire to optimize generator efficiency through time-shifting dispatch).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Installations">Installations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium_redox_battery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Installations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are several notable currently installed vanadium batteries:</p>
<ul>
<li>A 1 MW, 4&#160;MW·h (14&#160;GJ) <a href="/wiki/UniEnergy_Technologies" title="UniEnergy Technologies">UniEnergy Technologies</a> Energy Storage system (Uni.System) owned by <a href="/wiki/Avista" title="Avista">Avista</a> Utilities and installed in Pullman Washington in April 2015. The system is used for load shifting, distribution deferral and frequency regulation for Avista Utilities, and for backup power for critical loads at Schweitzer Engineering Laboratories.<sup id="cite_ref-wesoff_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wesoff-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>A 1.5&#160;MW UPS system in a semiconductor fabrication plant in Japan.</li>
<li>A 600&#160;kW, six-hour system, installed by Prudent Energy in Oxnard, California, USA.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li>A 275&#160;kW output balancer in use on a wind power project in the Tomari Wind Hills of Hokkaido.</li>
<li>A 200&#160;kW, 800&#160;kW·h (2.9&#160;GJ) output leveler in use at the <a href="/wiki/Huxley_Hill_Wind_Farm" title="Huxley Hill Wind Farm">Huxley Hill Wind Farm</a> on <a href="/wiki/King_Island_(Tasmania)" title="King Island (Tasmania)">King Island</a>, Tasmania.</li>
<li>A 250&#160;kW, 2&#160;MW·h (7.2&#160;GJ) load leveler in use at Castle Valley, Utah.</li>
<li>Two 5-kW units installed in St. Petersburg, Florida, under the auspices of USF's Power Center for Utility Explorations.</li>
<li>A 100 kWh (360 MJ) unit supplied with 18&#160;kW stacks manufactured by Cellstrom (Austria) has been installed in Vierakker (Gelderland, The Netherlands) as part of an integrated energy concept called 'FotonenBoer'/'PhotonFarmer' (InnovationNetwork/Foundation Courage).</li>
<li>A 400&#160;kW, 500&#160;kWh (1.8 GJ) output balancer in use on a solar power project in the Bilacenge Village in Sumba Island, Indonesia.</li>
<li>A 50&#160;kW, 100&#160;kWh (360 MJ) peak shaving for manufacturing facility in Gongju, South Korea.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>A 5&#160;kW, 60 kWh unit integrated with photovoltaic generation at University of Évora, Portugal.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li>A 100&#160;kW, 1.26 MWh installation is planned for the island of Gigha, Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>A 5 MW, 10&#160;MW·h (36&#160;GJ) system at the Woniushi wind power farm was connected to the Liaoning Power Grid, China, on 23 May 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li>1 MW, 5&#160;MW·h (18&#160;GJ) installed by Sumitomo Electric Industries in Yokohama.</li>
<li>100&#160;kW, 600 kWh unit, installed by Prudent Energy &amp; ABB in Kolarovo, Slovakia.</li>
<li>200&#160;kW 800kWh unit, planned by Solibra System Montage in Koblenz, Germany.</li>
</ul>
