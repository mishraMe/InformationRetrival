<p><b>Granada</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <small>Spanish:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Spanish" title="Help:IPA for Spanish">[ɡɾaˈnaða]</a></span>, <small>locally:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Spanish" title="Help:IPA for Spanish">[ɡɾaˈnaː, -ˈnaða]</a></span>)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is the <a href="/wiki/Capital_city" title="Capital city">capital city</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Granada" title="Province of Granada">province of Granada</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_communities_of_Spain" title="Autonomous communities of Spain">autonomous community</a> of <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusia</a>, Spain. Granada is located at the foot of the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(Spain)" title="Sierra Nevada (Spain)">Sierra Nevada</a> mountains, at the confluence of four rivers, the Beiro, the <a href="/wiki/Darro_(river)" title="Darro (river)">Darro</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Genil" title="Genil">Genil</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Monachil" title="Monachil">Monachil</a>. It sits at an average elevation of 738 metres <a href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" class="mw-redirect" title="Above mean sea level">above sea level</a>, yet is only one hour by car from the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> coast, the <a href="/wiki/Costa_Tropical" title="Costa Tropical">Costa Tropical</a>. Nearby is the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_Ski_Station" title="Sierra Nevada Ski Station">Sierra Nevada Ski Station</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/FIS_Alpine_World_Ski_Championships_1996" title="FIS Alpine World Ski Championships 1996">FIS Alpine World Ski Championships 1996</a> were held.</p>
<p>In the 2005 national <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a>, the population of the city of Granada proper was 236,982, and the population of the entire urban area was estimated to be 472,638, ranking as the 13th-largest urban area of Spain. About 3.3% of the population did not hold Spanish citizenship, the largest number of these people (31%; or 1% of the total population) coming from <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. Its nearest airport is <a href="/wiki/Federico_Garc%C3%ADa_Lorca_Airport" title="Federico García Lorca Airport">Federico García Lorca Granada-Jaén Airport</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Alhambra" title="Alhambra">Alhambra</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moorish</a> citadel and palace, is in Granada. It is the most renowned building of the Andalusian <a href="/wiki/Islamic" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic">Islamic</a> historical legacy with its many cultural attractions that make Granada a popular destination among the touristic cities of Spain. The <a href="/wiki/Almohad" class="mw-redirect" title="Almohad">Almohad</a> influence on architecture is also preserved in the Granada neighborhood called the <a href="/wiki/Albaic%C3%ADn" class="mw-redirect" title="Albaicín">Albaicín</a> with its fine examples of Moorish and <a href="/wiki/Morisco" title="Morisco">Morisco</a> construction. Granada is also well-known within Spain for the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Granada" title="University of Granada">University of Granada</a> which has about 80,000 students spread over five different campuses in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Pomegranate" title="Pomegranate">pomegranate</a> (in Spanish, <i>granada</i>) is the <a href="/wiki/Heraldry" title="Heraldry">heraldic</a> device of Granada.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">Hispania</a></div>
<p>The region surrounding what today is Granada has been populated since at least 5500 B.C. and experienced <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Visigoth" class="mw-redirect" title="Visigoth">Visigothic</a> influences. The most ancient ruins found in the city belongs to an <a href="/wiki/Iberians" title="Iberians">Iberian</a> oppidum called Ilturir, in the region known as <a href="/wiki/Bastetania" class="mw-redirect" title="Bastetania">Bastetania</a>.This oppidum eventually changed its name to Iliberri, and after the Roman conquest, to Municipium Florentinum Iliberitanum.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERingSalkinLa_Boda1995296_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERingSalkinLa_Boda1995296-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding">Founding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Founding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">Al-Andalus</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_conquest_of_Hispania" title="Umayyad conquest of Hispania">Umayyad conquest of Hispania</a>, starting in 711 A.D., brought large parts of the Iberian Peninsula under Moorish control and established Al-Andalus. In the early 11th century, after a civil war that ended the Caliphate, the Berber, <a href="/wiki/Zawi_ben_Ziri" title="Zawi ben Ziri">Zawi ben Ziri</a>, established an independent kingdom for himself, the <a href="/wiki/Taifa_of_Granada" title="Taifa of Granada">Taifa of Granada</a>, with Elvira/Illiberis as its capital. <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jewish people</a> were established in another area close to Illiberis, called <i>Gárnata</i> or <i>Gárnata al-yahūd</i> ("Granada of the Jews"). Granada's historical name in the <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic language</a> was <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">غرناطة</span> (<i><span title="DIN 31635 Arabic" class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none">Ġarnāṭah </span></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERingSalkinLa_Boda1995296_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERingSalkinLa_Boda1995296-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoom2006149_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoom2006149-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDale1882_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDale1882-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEl_Hareir2011454_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEl_Hareir2011454-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The word <i>Gárnata</i> (or <i>Karnatah</i>) possibly means "Hill of Strangers". Because the city was situated on a low plain and, as a result, difficult to protect from attacks, the ruler decided to transfer his residence to the higher situated area of Gárnata. In a short time this town was transformed into one of the most important cities of Al-Andalus.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERingSalkinLa_Boda1995296_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERingSalkinLa_Boda1995296-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDale1882_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDale1882-4">[4]</a></sup> By the end of the 11th century, the city had spread across the Darro to reach the hill of the future Alhambra, and included the <i><a href="/wiki/Albayz%C3%ADn" title="Albayzín">Albayzín</a></i> neighborhood (also called "Albaicín" or "El Albaicín", now a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_site" class="mw-redirect" title="World Heritage site">World Heritage site</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-unesco2008_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unesco2008-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Almoravids" class="mw-redirect" title="Almoravids">Almoravids</a> ruled Granada from 1090 and the <a href="/wiki/Almohad_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Almohad dynasty">Almohad dynasty</a> from 1166.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEColum_Hourihane2012_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEColum_Hourihane2012-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nasrid_dynasty.E2.80.94Emirate_of_Granada">Nasrid dynasty—Emirate of Granada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Nasrid dynasty—Emirate of Granada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Nasrid_dynasty" title="Nasrid dynasty">Nasrid dynasty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_Granada" title="Emirate of Granada">Emirate of Granada</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:132px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Escudo_de_la_dinast%C3%ADa_nazar%C3%AD_(Palacio_de_la_Alhambra,_Spain).JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Escudo_de_la_dinast%C3%ADa_nazar%C3%AD_%28Palacio_de_la_Alhambra%2C_Spain%29.JPG/130px-Escudo_de_la_dinast%C3%ADa_nazar%C3%AD_%28Palacio_de_la_Alhambra%2C_Spain%29.JPG" width="130" height="98" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Escudo_de_la_dinast%C3%ADa_nazar%C3%AD_%28Palacio_de_la_Alhambra%2C_Spain%29.JPG/195px-Escudo_de_la_dinast%C3%ADa_nazar%C3%AD_%28Palacio_de_la_Alhambra%2C_Spain%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Escudo_de_la_dinast%C3%ADa_nazar%C3%AD_%28Palacio_de_la_Alhambra%2C_Spain%29.JPG/260px-Escudo_de_la_dinast%C3%ADa_nazar%C3%AD_%28Palacio_de_la_Alhambra%2C_Spain%29.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Escudo_de_la_dinast%C3%ADa_nazar%C3%AD_(Palacio_de_la_Alhambra,_Spain).JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Coat of arms of the <a href="/wiki/Nasrid_Kingdom_of_Granada" class="mw-redirect" title="Nasrid Kingdom of Granada">Nasrid Kingdom of Granada</a> in Palacio de Comares room in the Alhambra.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1228, with the departure of the Almohad prince, Idris, who left Iberia to take the Almohad leadership, the ambitious Ibn al-Ahmar established the longest lasting <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> dynasty on the Iberian peninsula - the <a href="/wiki/Nasrid_dynasty" title="Nasrid dynasty">Nasrids</a>. With the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a> in full swing after the conquest of Cordoba in 1236, the Nasrids aligned themselves with <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_III_of_Castile" title="Ferdinand III of Castile">Fernando III of Castile</a>, officially becoming the Emirate of Granada in 1238.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEColum_Hourihane2012_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEColum_Hourihane2012-7">[7]</a></sup> According to some historians, Granada was a <a href="/wiki/Tributary_state" title="Tributary state">tributary state</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Castile" title="Kingdom of Castile">Kingdom of Castile</a> since that year. It provided connections with the Muslim and Arab trade centers, particularly for <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> from <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">Maghreb</a>, and exported silk and dried fruits produced in the area.<sup id="cite_ref-KennedyKennedy2014_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KennedyKennedy2014-8">[8]</a></sup> The Nasrids also supplied troops from the Emirate and mercenaries from <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> for service to Castile.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ibn_Battuta" title="Ibn Battuta">Ibn Battuta</a>, a famous traveler and an authentic historian, visited the kingdom of Granada in 1350. He described it as a powerful and self-sufficient kingdom in its own right, although frequently embroiled in skirmishes with the kingdom of Castile. If it was really a vassal state, it was contrary to the policy of the Reconquista to allow it to flourish for almost two centuries and a half after the fall of Sevilla in 1248.</p>
<p>During the Moor rule, Granada was a city with adherents to many religions and ethnicities (Arabs, Berbers, Christians, Jews) who lived in separate quarters.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reconquista_by_the_Christian_Spanish_against_Muslims_and_the_16th_century">Reconquista by the Christian Spanish against Muslims and the 16th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reconquista by the Christian Spanish against Muslims and the 16th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:La_Rendici%C3%B3n_de_Granada_-_Pradilla.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/La_Rendici%C3%B3n_de_Granada_-_Pradilla.jpg/250px-La_Rendici%C3%B3n_de_Granada_-_Pradilla.jpg" width="250" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/La_Rendici%C3%B3n_de_Granada_-_Pradilla.jpg/375px-La_Rendici%C3%B3n_de_Granada_-_Pradilla.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/La_Rendici%C3%B3n_de_Granada_-_Pradilla.jpg/500px-La_Rendici%C3%B3n_de_Granada_-_Pradilla.jpg 2x" data-file-width="8133" data-file-height="5210" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:La_Rendici%C3%B3n_de_Granada_-_Pradilla.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>The Capitulation of Granada</i> by F. Padilla: Muhammad XII before Ferdinand and Isabella (circa 1882).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On January 2, 1492, the last Muslim ruler in Iberia, <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_XII_of_Granada" title="Muhammad XII of Granada">Emir Muhammad XII</a>, known as Boabdil to the Spanish, surrendered complete control of the <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_Granada" title="Emirate of Granada">Emirate of Granada</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_V_of_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferdinand V of Spain">Fernando V</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isabella_I_of_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="Isabella I of Spain">Isabella I</a>, <i>Los Reyes Católicos</i> ('the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">Catholic Monarchs</a>'), after the last battle of the <a href="/wiki/Granada_War" title="Granada War">Granada War</a>.</p>
<p>The 1492 surrender of the Islamic Emirate of Granada to the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">Catholic Monarchs</a> is one of the most significant events in Granada's history as it marks the completion of the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a> of <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">Al-Andalus</a>. The terms of the surrender, expressed in the <a href="/wiki/Alhambra_Decree" title="Alhambra Decree">Alhambra Decree</a> treaty, explicitly allowed the city's Muslim inhabitants to continue unmolested in the practice of their faith and customs, known as <a href="/wiki/Mud%C3%A9jar" title="Mudéjar">Mudéjar</a>. By 1499, however, Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Jim%C3%A9nez_de_Cisneros" title="Francisco Jiménez de Cisneros">Francisco Jiménez de Cisneros</a> grew frustrated with the slow pace of the efforts of <a href="/wiki/Archbishopric_of_Granada" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishopric of Granada">Granada</a>’s first Archbishop, Fernando de Talavera, to convert non-Christians to Christianity and undertook a program of forced Christian baptisms, creating the <a href="/wiki/Converso" title="Converso">Converso</a> (convert) class for Muslims and Jews. Cisneros's new tactics, which were a direct violation of the terms of the treaty, provoked <a href="/wiki/Morisco_rebellions_in_Granada" class="mw-redirect" title="Morisco rebellions in Granada">an armed Muslim revolt</a> centered in the rural <a href="/wiki/Alpujarras" title="Alpujarras">Alpujarras</a> region southwest of the city.</p>
<p>Responding to the rebellion of 1501, the Castilian Crown rescinded the Alhambra Decree treaty, and mandated that Granada's Muslims must convert or emigrate. Under the 1492 Alhambra Decree, Spain's Jewish population, unlike the Muslims, had already been forced to convert under threat of expulsion or even execution, becoming <a href="/wiki/Marrano" title="Marrano">Marranos</a> (meaning "pigs" in Spanish), or Catholics of Jewish descent. Many of the elite Muslim class subsequently emigrated to <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>. The majority of the Granada's Mudéjar Muslims stayed to convert, however, becoming <a href="/wiki/Morisco" title="Morisco">Moriscos</a>, or Catholics of <a href="/wiki/Moorish" class="mw-redirect" title="Moorish">Moorish</a> descent ("Moor" being equivalent to Muslim). Both populations of <i>conversos</i> were subject to persecution, execution, or exile, and each had cells that practiced their original religion in secrecy.</p>
<p>Over the course of the 16th century, Granada took on an ever more Catholic and Castilian character, as immigrants came to the city from other parts of the Iberian Peninsula. The city's <a href="/wiki/Mosque" title="Mosque">mosques</a> were converted to Christian churches or completely destroyed. New structures, such as the cathedral and the Chancillería, or Royal Court of Appeals, transformed the urban landscape. After the 1492 Alhambra decree, which resulted in the majority of Granada's Jewish population being expelled, the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_quarter_(diaspora)" title="Jewish quarter (diaspora)">Jewish quarter</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ghetto" title="Ghetto">ghetto</a>) was demolished to make way for new Catholic and Castilian institutions and uses.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish Empire</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Granada" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Granada">fall of Granada</a> has a significant place among the important events that mark the latter half of the Spanish 15th century. It completed the so-called <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a> (or Christian reconquest) of the eight-hundred-year-long Islamic rule in the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>. Spain, now without any major internal territorial conflict, embarked on a great phase of exploration and colonization around the globe. In the same year the sailing expedition of <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> resulted in what is usually claimed to be the first European sighting of the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>, although <a href="/wiki/Leif_Ericson" class="mw-redirect" title="Leif Ericson">Leif Ericson</a> is often regarded as the first European to land in the New World, 500 years before Christopher Columbus. The resources of the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> enriched the crown and the country, allowing <a href="/wiki/Isabella_I_of_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="Isabella I of Spain">Isabella I</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">Ferrando II</a> to consolidate their rule as Catholic Monarchs of the united kingdoms. Subsequent conquests, and the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">Spanish colonization of the Americas</a> by the maritime expeditions they commissioned, created the vast <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish Empire</a>: for a time the largest in the world.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Heritage_and_monuments">Heritage and monuments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Heritage and monuments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The greatest artistic wealth of Granada is its Spanish-Muslim art — in particular, the compound of the <a href="/wiki/Alhambra" title="Alhambra">Alhambra</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Generalife" title="Generalife">Generalife</a>. The Generalife is a pleasure <a href="/wiki/Palace" title="Palace">palace</a> with attached romantic <a href="/wiki/Gardens" class="mw-redirect" title="Gardens">gardens</a>, remarkable both for its location and layout, as well as for the diversity of its <a href="/wiki/Flowers" class="mw-redirect" title="Flowers">flowers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fountain" title="Fountain">fountains</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Alhambra" title="Alhambra">Alhambra</a> is the architectural culmination of the works of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nasrid_art&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nasrid art (page does not exist)">Nasrid art</a> that were undertaken in the 13th and 14th centuries, with most of the Alhambra having been built at the time of <a href="/wiki/Yusuf_I,_Sultan_of_Granada" title="Yusuf I, Sultan of Granada">Yusuf I</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muhammed_V,_Sultan_of_Granada" class="mw-redirect" title="Muhammed V, Sultan of Granada">Mohammed V</a>, between 1333 and 1354.</p>
<p>At present, the buildings of Granada are typically <a href="/wiki/Bourgeois" class="mw-redirect" title="Bourgeois">bourgeois</a> in appearance, with much of the architecture dating from the 19th Century, together with numerous <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> buildings.</p>
<dl>
<dt>The Alhambra</dt>
</dl>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Alhambra" title="Alhambra">Alhambra</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alhambra_de_Granada_-_Espa%C3%B1a.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Alhambra" title="Alhambra">Alhambra</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Alhambra is a <a href="/wiki/Nasrid_dynasty" title="Nasrid dynasty">Nasrid</a> "palace city". It was declared a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> in 1984. It is certainly Granada's most emblematic monument and one of the most visited in Spain. It consists of a defensive zone, the <a href="/wiki/Alcazaba" title="Alcazaba">Alcazaba</a>, together with others of a residential and formal state character, the Nasrid Palaces and, lastly, the palace, gardens and orchards of El <a href="/wiki/Generalife" title="Generalife">Generalife</a>.</p>
<p>The Alhambra occupies a small plateau on the southeastern border of the city in the foothills of the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(Spain)" title="Sierra Nevada (Spain)">Sierra Nevada</a> above the Assabica valley. Some of the buildings may have existed before the arrival of the Moors. The Alhambra as a whole is completely walled, bordered to the north by the valley of the <a href="/wiki/Darro_(river)" title="Darro (river)">Darro</a>, to the south by the <i>al-Sabika</i>, and to the east of the <i>Cuesta del Rey Chico</i>, which in turn is separated from the <a href="/wiki/Albayz%C3%ADn" title="Albayzín">Albayzín</a> and Generalife, located in the <i>Cerro del Sol</i>.</p>
<p>In the 11th century the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Castle_of_the_Alhambra&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Castle of the Alhambra (page does not exist)">Castle of the Alhambra</a> was developed as a walled town which became a military stronghold that dominated the whole city. But it was in the 13th century, with the arrival of the first <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarch</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Nasrid_dynasty" title="Nasrid dynasty">Nasrid dynasty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mohammed_I_ibn_Nasr" title="Mohammed I ibn Nasr">Mohammed I ibn Nasr</a> (Mohammed I, 1238–1273), that the royal residence was established in the Alhambra. This marked the beginning of its heyday. The Alhambra became palace, citadel and fortress, and was the residence of the Nasrid sultans and their senior officials, including servants of the court and elite soldiers (13th-14th centuries).</p>
<p>In 1492 the Catholic Monarchs, <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">Ferdinand and Isabela</a>, expelled the final Moors from the city of Granada. They established permanent residency in The Alhambra, and it was here that <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> requested royal endorsement for his westward expedition.</p>
<p>In 1527 <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor</a> demolished part of the architectural complex to build the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Charles_V" title="Palace of Charles V">Palace</a> which bears his name. Although the Catholic Monarchs had already altered some rooms of the Alhambra after the conquest of the city in 1492, Charles V wanted to construct a permanent residence befitting an emperor. Around 1537 he ordered the construction of the <i>Peinador de la Reina</i>, or Queen's dressing room, where his wife Isabel lived, over the Tower of Abu l-Hayyay.</p>
<p>There was a pause in the ongoing maintenance of the Alhambra from the 18th century for almost a hundred years, and during the <a href="/wiki/First_French_Empire" title="First French Empire">French domination</a> substantial portions of the fortress were blown apart. The repair, restoration and conservation that continues to this day did not begin until the 19th century. The complex currently includes the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Museum_of_the_Alhambra&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Museum of the Alhambra (page does not exist)">Museum of the Alhambra</a>, with objects mainly from the site of the monument itself and the Museum of Fine Arts.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<dt>The Generalife</dt>
</dl>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Generalife" title="Generalife">Generalife</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Spain_Andalusia_Granada_BW_2015-10-25_15-39-55.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Spain_Andalusia_Granada_BW_2015-10-25_15-39-55.jpg/200px-Spain_Andalusia_Granada_BW_2015-10-25_15-39-55.jpg" width="200" height="298" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Spain_Andalusia_Granada_BW_2015-10-25_15-39-55.jpg/300px-Spain_Andalusia_Granada_BW_2015-10-25_15-39-55.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Spain_Andalusia_Granada_BW_2015-10-25_15-39-55.jpg/400px-Spain_Andalusia_Granada_BW_2015-10-25_15-39-55.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2474" data-file-height="3692" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Spain_Andalusia_Granada_BW_2015-10-25_15-39-55.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Generalife Fountains</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <b>Generalife</b> is a garden area attached to the Alhambra which became a place of recreation and rest for the <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_Granada" title="Emirate of Granada">Granadan Muslim kings</a> when they wanted to flee the tedium of official life in the Palace. It occupies the slopes of the hill <i>Cerro del Sol</i> above the ravines of the <a href="/wiki/Genil" title="Genil">Genil</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Darro_(river)" title="Darro (river)">Darro</a> and is visible from vantage points throughout the city. It was conceived as a rural village, consisting of landscaping, gardens and architecture. The palace and gardens were built during the reign of Muhammad III (1302–1309) and redecorated shortly after by Abu I-Walid Isma'il (1313–1324). It is of the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_architecture" title="Islamic architecture">Islamic</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nasrid_art&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nasrid art (page does not exist)">Nasrid</a> style, and is today one of the biggest attractions in the city of Granada. The Generalife was declared a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> in 1984.</p>
<p>It is difficult to know the original appearance of the Generalife, as it has been subject to modifications and reconstructions throughout the Christian period which disfigured many of its former aspects. All buildings of the Generalife are of solid construction, and the overall decor is austere and simple. There is little variety to the Alhambra's decorative <a href="/wiki/Plaster" title="Plaster">plaster</a>, but the aesthetic is tasteful and extremely delicate. In the last third of the 20th century, a part of the garden was destroyed to build an <a href="/wiki/Auditorium" title="Auditorium">auditorium</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Cathedral</dt>
</dl>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_Granada" class="mw-redirect" title="Cathedral of Granada">Cathedral of Granada</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Spain_Andalusia_Granada_BW_2015-10-25_13-20-43.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Spain_Andalusia_Granada_BW_2015-10-25_13-20-43.jpg/200px-Spain_Andalusia_Granada_BW_2015-10-25_13-20-43.jpg" width="200" height="219" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Spain_Andalusia_Granada_BW_2015-10-25_13-20-43.jpg/300px-Spain_Andalusia_Granada_BW_2015-10-25_13-20-43.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Spain_Andalusia_Granada_BW_2015-10-25_13-20-43.jpg/400px-Spain_Andalusia_Granada_BW_2015-10-25_13-20-43.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3859" data-file-height="4218" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Spain_Andalusia_Granada_BW_2015-10-25_13-20-43.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_Granada" class="mw-redirect" title="Cathedral of Granada">Cathedral of Granada</a> - south portal</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">cathedral</a> of Granada is built over the Nasrid Great <a href="/wiki/Mosque" title="Mosque">Mosque</a> of Granada, in the center of the city. Its construction began during the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Renaissance" title="Spanish Renaissance">Spanish Renaissance</a> in the early 16th century, shortly after the <a href="/wiki/Granada_War" title="Granada War">conquest of Granada</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">Catholic Monarchs</a>, who commissioned the works to <a href="/wiki/Juan_Gil_de_Honta%C3%B1%C3%B3n" title="Juan Gil de Hontañón">Juan Gil de Hontañón</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Enrique_Egas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Enrique Egas (page does not exist)">Enrique Egas</a>. Numerous grand buildings were built in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor</a>, so that the cathedral is contemporary to the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Charles_V" title="Palace of Charles V">Christian palace of the Alhambra</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Granada" title="University of Granada">University</a> and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Real_Chanciller%C3%ADa_de_Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Real Chancillería de Granada (page does not exist)">Real Chancillería</a> (supreme court).</p>
<p>The church was conceived on the model of the <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_Toledo" class="mw-redirect" title="Cathedral of Toledo">Cathedral of Toledo</a>, for what initially was a <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> architectural project, as was customary in Spain in the early decades of the 16th century. However, Egas was relieved by the Catholic hierarchy in 1529, and the continuation of the work was assigned to <a href="/wiki/Diego_Siloe" title="Diego Siloe">Diego Siloe</a>, who built upon the example of his predecessor, but changed the approach towards a fully <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> aesthetic.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The architect drew new Renaissance lines for the whole building over the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> foundations, with an ambulatory and five naves instead of the usual three. Over time, the bishopric continued to commission new architectural projects of importance, such as the redesign of the main façade, undertaken in 1664 by <a href="/wiki/Alonso_Cano" class="mw-redirect" title="Alonso Cano">Alonso Cano</a> (1601–1667) to introduce Baroque elements. In 1706 Francisco de Hurtado Izquierdo and later his collaborator José Bada built the current tabernacle of the cathedral.</p>
<p>Highlights of the church's components include the Main <a href="/wiki/Chapel" title="Chapel">chapel</a>, where may be found the praying statues of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">Catholic Monarchs</a>, which consists of a series of <a href="/wiki/Corinthian_order" title="Corinthian order">Corinthian columns</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Entablature" title="Entablature">entablature</a> resting on their <a href="/wiki/Capital_(architecture)" title="Capital (architecture)">capitals</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Vault_(architecture)" title="Vault (architecture)">vault</a> over all. The spaces of the walls between the columns are perforated by a series of windows. The design of the <a href="/wiki/Church_tabernacle" title="Church tabernacle">tabernacle</a> of 1706 preserves the classic proportions of the church, with its multiple columns crossing the forms of Diego de Siloé.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<dt>Royal Chapel</dt>
</dl>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Royal_Chapel_of_Granada" title="Royal Chapel of Granada">Royal Chapel of Granada</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">Catholic Monarchs</a> chose the city of Granada as their burial site by a royal decree dated September 13, 1504. The Royal Chapel of Granada, built over the former terrace of the Great Mosque, ranks with other important Granadan buildings such as the Lonja and the Catedral e Iglesia del Sagrario. In it are buried the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">Catholic Monarchs</a>, their daughter <a href="/wiki/Joanna_of_Castile" title="Joanna of Castile">Joanna of Castile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philip_I_of_Castile" title="Philip I of Castile">Felipe I</a>. Construction of the Chapel started in 1505, directed by its designer, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Enrique_Egas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Enrique Egas (page does not exist)">Enrique Egas</a>. Built in several stages, the continuing evolution of its design joined <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> construction and decoration with <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> ideals, as seen in the tombs and the 17th and 18th century Granadan art in the Chapel of Santa Cruz. Over the years the church acquired a treasury of works of art, liturgical objects and <a href="/wiki/Relic" title="Relic">relics</a>.</p>
<p>The Royal Chapel was declared a <a href="/wiki/Bien_de_Inter%C3%A9s_Cultural" title="Bien de Interés Cultural">Historic Artistic Monument</a> on May 19, 1884, taking consideration of B.I.C. (<a href="/wiki/Bien_de_Inter%C3%A9s_Cultural" title="Bien de Interés Cultural">Bien de Interés Cultural</a>) in the current legislation of the Spanish Historical Heritage (Law 16/1985 of 25 June). The most important parts of the chapel are its main <a href="/wiki/Retable" title="Retable">retable</a>, grid and <a href="/wiki/Vault_(architecture)" title="Vault (architecture)">vault</a>. In the Sacristy-Museum is the legacy of the Catholic Monarchs. Its art gallery is highlighted by works of the Flemish, Italian and Spanish schools.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<dl>
<dt>Albayzín</dt>
</dl>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Albayz%C3%ADn" title="Albayzín">Albayzín</a></div>
<p>The Albayzín (or Albaicín) is a neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">Al-Andalus</a> origin, much visited by tourists who flock to the city because of its historical associations, architecture, and landscape.</p>
<p>The archeological findings in the area show that it has been inhabited since ancient times. It became more relevant with the arrival of the <a href="/wiki/Zirid_dynasty" title="Zirid dynasty">Zirid dynasty</a>, in 1013, when it was surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Defensive_walls" class="mw-redirect" title="Defensive walls">defensive walls</a>. It is one of the ancient centers of Granada, like the Alhambra, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Realejo-San_Mat%C3%ADas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Realejo-San Matías (page does not exist)">Realejo</a> and the <i>Arrabal de Bib-Rambla</i>, in the flat part of the city. Its current extension runs from the walls of the <i><a href="/wiki/Alcazaba" title="Alcazaba">Alcazaba</a></i> to the <i>cerro of San Miguel</i> and on the other hand, from the <i>Puerta de Guadix</i> to the <i>Alcazaba</i>.</p>
<p>This neighborhood had its greatest development in the <a href="/wiki/Nasrid_dynasty" title="Nasrid dynasty">Nasrid</a> era, and therefore largely maintains the urban fabric of this period, with narrow streets arranged in an intricate network that extends from the upper area, called <i>San Nicolás</i>, to the river <a href="/wiki/Darro_(river)" title="Darro (river)">Darro</a> and <i>Calle Elvira</i>, located in <i>Plaza Nueva</i>. The traditional type of housing is the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Carmen_granadino&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Carmen granadino (page does not exist)">Carmen granadino</a>, consisting of a free house surrounded by a high wall that separates it from the street and includes a small orchard or garden.</p>
<p>In the Muslim era the Albaicín was characterized as the locus of many revolts against the caliphate. At that time it was the residence of <a href="/wiki/Artisan" title="Artisan">craftsmen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Industrialist" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrialist">industrialists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy_(class)" title="Aristocracy (class)">aristocrats</a>. With the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Christian reconquest</a>, it would progressively lose its splendor. The Christians built churches and settled there the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Real_Chanciller%C3%ADa_de_Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Real Chancillería de Granada (page does not exist)">Real Chancillería</a>. During the rule of <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Felipe II of Spain</a>, after the rebellion and subsequent expulsion of the <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moors</a>, the district was depopulated. In 1994 it was declared a <a href="/wiki/Unesco" class="mw-redirect" title="Unesco">Unesco</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Of its architectural wealth among others include the <i>Ziri walls</i> of the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alcazaba_Cadima&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Alcazaba Cadima (page does not exist)">Alcazaba Cadima</a></i>, the <i>Nasrid walls</i>, the <i>towers of the Alcazaba</i>, the churches of <i>El Salvador</i> (former main <a href="/wiki/Mosque" title="Mosque">mosque</a>), <i>San Cristóbal</i>, <i>San Miguel Alto</i> and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Real_Chanciller%C3%ADa_de_Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Real Chancillería de Granada (page does not exist)">Real Chancillería</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Sacromonte</dt>
</dl>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sacromonte" title="Sacromonte">Sacromonte</a></div>
<p>The <i>Sacromonte</i> neighborhood is located on the <i>Valparaíso</i> hill, one of several hills that make up Granada. This neighborhood is known as the old neighborhood of the <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Romani</a>, who settled in Granada after the conquest of the city. It is one of the most picturesque neighborhoods, full of whitewashed caves cut into the rock and used as residences. The sound of strumming guitars may still be heard there in the performance of flamenco <a href="/wiki/Flamenco#Cante_.28song.29" title="Flamenco">cantes</a> and "quejíos", so that over time it has become one of the most popular tourist attractions in Granada.</p>
<p>At the top of this hill is the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Abbey_of_Sacromonte&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Abbey of Sacromonte (page does not exist)">Abbey of Sacromonte</a> and the <i>College of Sacromonte</i>, founded in the 17th century by the then Archbishop of Granada <a href="/wiki/Pedro_de_Castro" class="mw-redirect" title="Pedro de Castro">Pedro de Castro</a>. The Abbey of Sacromonte was built to monitor and guard the alleged relics of the evangelists of Baetica. Those are of questionable authenticity, but since their finding the area has been a religious pilgrimage destination.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The abbey complex consists of <i>the Catacombs</i>, <i>The Abbey</i> (17th-18th centuries), the <i>Colegio Viejo de San Dionisio Areopagita</i> (17th century) and the Colegio Nuevo (19th century). The interior of the church is simple and small but has numerous excellent works of art, which accentuate the size and rich carving of the <i>Crucificado de Risueño</i>, an object of devotion for the gypsy people, who sing and dance in the procession of Holy Week. The facilities also include a museum, which houses the works acquired by the Foundation.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Charterhouse</dt>
</dl>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Granada_Charterhouse" title="Granada Charterhouse">Granada Charterhouse</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cartuja_de_Granada_Cupula_del_Sagrario.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Cartuja_de_Granada_Cupula_del_Sagrario.jpg/220px-Cartuja_de_Granada_Cupula_del_Sagrario.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Cartuja_de_Granada_Cupula_del_Sagrario.jpg/330px-Cartuja_de_Granada_Cupula_del_Sagrario.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Cartuja_de_Granada_Cupula_del_Sagrario.jpg/440px-Cartuja_de_Granada_Cupula_del_Sagrario.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="681" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cartuja_de_Granada_Cupula_del_Sagrario.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tabernacle Dome, <a href="/wiki/Granada_Charterhouse" title="Granada Charterhouse">Granada Charterhouse</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Charterhouse of Granada is a monastery of cloistered monks, located in what was a farm or Muslim almunia called <i>Aynadamar</i> ('Fountain of the Tears') that had an abundance of water and fruit trees. The initiative to build the monastery in that place was begun by <a href="/wiki/Gonzalo_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Gonzalo Fernández de Córdoba">Gonzalo Fernández de Córdoba</a>, known as <a href="/wiki/Gonzalo_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Gonzalo Fernández de Córdoba">El Gran Capitán</a>. The charterhouse was founded in 1506; construction started ten years later, and continued for the following 300 years.</p>
<p>The Monastery suffered heavy damage during the <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_War" title="Peninsular War">Peninsular War</a> and lost considerable property in 1837 as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_Confiscations_of_Mendiz%C3%A1bal" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecclesiastical Confiscations of Mendizábal">confiscations of Mendizábal</a>. Currently, the monastery belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Carthusian_Order" class="mw-redirect" title="Carthusian Order">Carthusians</a>, reporting directly to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Granada" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Granada">Archdiocese of Granada</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The street entrance to the complex is an ornate arch of <a href="/wiki/Plateresque" title="Plateresque">Plateresque</a> style. Through it one reaches a large courtyard, at the end which is a wide staircase leading to the entrance of the church. The church, of early 16th century style and plan, has three entrances, one for the faithful and the other two for monks and clergy. Its plan has a single nave divided into four sections, highlighting the <a href="/wiki/Retable" title="Retable">retables</a> of <a href="/wiki/Juan_S%C3%A1nchez_Cot%C3%A1n" title="Juan Sánchez Cotán">Juan Sánchez Cotán</a> and the chancel's glass doors, adorned with mother-of-pearl, silver, precious woods and ivory. The <a href="/wiki/Presbytery_(architecture)" class="mw-redirect" title="Presbytery (architecture)">presbytery</a> is covered by <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">elliptical</a> <a href="/wiki/Vault_(architecture)" title="Vault (architecture)">vaulting</a>. The main altar, between the chancel arch and the <a href="/wiki/Church_tabernacle" title="Church tabernacle">church tabernacle</a>, is gilded wood.</p>
<p>The Church tabernacle and <i>Sancta Santorum</i> is considered a masterpiece of baroque Spanish art in its blend of architecture, painting and sculpture. The dome that covers this area is decorated with frescoes by the Cordovan artist <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Palomino" title="Antonio Palomino">Antonio Palomino</a> (18th century) representing the triumph of the Church Militant, the faith, and religious life.</p>
<p>The courtyard, with galleries of arches on <a href="/wiki/Doric_order" title="Doric order">Doric order</a> columns opening on it, is centered by a fountain. The Chapter House of Legos is the oldest building of the monastery (1517). It is rectangular and covered with groin vaulting.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Realejo">The Realejo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Realejo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Realejo was the Jewish district at the time of the Nasride Granada. The Jewish population was so important that Granada was known from the Al-Andalus Country under the name of <i>Granada de los judios</i> (in Arabic, غرناطة اليهود <i>gharnāṭah al-yahūd</i>). It is today a district made up of many Andalusian villas, with gardens opening onto the streets, called <i>Los Cármenes</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Cartuja">The Cartuja</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The Cartuja">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This district contains the Carthusian monastery of the same name: <i>Cartuja</i>. This is an old monastery started in a late Gothic style with Baroque exuberant interior decorations. In this district also, many buildings were created with the extension of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Granada" title="University of Granada">University of Granada</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bib-Rambla">Bib-Rambla</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Bib-Rambla">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The toponym existed at the time of the Arabs. Nowadays, Bib-Rambla is a high point for gastronomy, especially in its terraces of restaurants, open on beautiful days. The Arab bazaar (<i>Alcaicería</i>) is made up of several narrow streets, which start from this place and continue as far as the cathedral</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sacromonte">Sacromonte</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sacromonte">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sacromonte" title="Sacromonte">Sacromonte</a></div>
<p>The Sacromonte neighborhood is located on the extension of the hill of Albaicín, along the Darro River. This area, which became famous by the nineteenth century for its predominantly <a href="/wiki/Romani_people_in_Spain" title="Romani people in Spain">Gitano</a> inhabitants, is characterized by cave houses, which are dug into the hillside. The area has a reputation as a major center of flamenco song and dance, including the <i>Zambra Gitana</i>, Andalusian dance originating in the Middle East. The zone is a protected cultural environment under the auspices of the <i>Centro de Interpretación del Sacromonte</i>, a cultural center dedicated to the preservation of Gitano cultural forms.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Albayz.C3.ADn">Albayzín</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Albayzín">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Albayz%C3%ADn" title="Albayzín">Albayzín</a></div>
<p>Albayzín (also written as Albaicín), located on a hill on the right bank of the river <a href="/wiki/Darro_(river)" title="Darro (river)">Darro</a>, is the ancient Moorish quarter of the city and transports the visitor to a unique world: the site of the ancient city of Elvira, so-called before the <a href="/wiki/Zirid" class="mw-redirect" title="Zirid">Zirid</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moors</a></i> renamed it Granada. It housed the artists who went up to build the palaces of Alhambra on the hill facing it. Time allowed its embellishment. Of particular note is the Plaza de San Nicolas (Plaza of St Nicholas) from where a stunning view of the Alhambra can be seen. The artist George Owen Wynne Apperley RA RI (1884–1960) owned houses on both sides of the Placeta de San Nicolás, also known as El Mirador.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zaid.C3.ADn">Zaidín</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Zaidín">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This formerly blue collar but now upmarket neighborhood houses 100,000 residents of Granada, making it the largest neighborhood or 'barrio'. Traditionally populated by <a href="/wiki/Romani_people_in_Spain" title="Romani people in Spain">Romani</a> people, now many residents are from North and West Africa, China, and many South American countries. Every Saturday morning it hosts a large outdoor market or "mercadillo", where many people come and sell their wares of fruits and vegetables, clothes and shoes, and other odds and ends.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to its distance from the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, Granada has a borderline <a href="/wiki/Cold_semi-arid_climate" class="mw-redirect" title="Cold semi-arid climate">cold semi-arid climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a>: <i>BSk</i>). The city lies on the eastern limit of the Mediterranean climate zone of southwestern Spain; thus its climate borders the <a href="/wiki/Hot-summer_Mediterranean_climate" class="mw-redirect" title="Hot-summer Mediterranean climate">hot-summer Mediterranean climate</a> (<i>Csa</i>), receiving enough rainfall as well as having a precipitation regime typical of the Mediterranean climate. Summers are hot and dry with daily temperatures averaging 34&#160;°C (93&#160;°F) in the hottest month (July); however, temperatures reaching over 40&#160;°C (104&#160;°F) are not uncommon in the summer months. Winters are cool and wet, and most of the rainfall is concentrated from November through to January. The coldest month is January with daytime temperatures hovering at 13&#160;°C (55&#160;°F) and dropping to around 1&#160;°C (34&#160;°F) during the night. Frost is quite common as temperatures usually reach below-freezing in the early morning. Spring and autumn are unpredictable, with temperatures ranging from mild to warm.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Construction of a <a href="/wiki/Light_metro" class="mw-redirect" title="Light metro">light metro</a> network, the <a href="/wiki/Granada_metro" title="Granada metro">Granada metro</a>, began in 2007. It will cross Granada and cover the towns of <a href="/wiki/Albolote" title="Albolote">Albolote</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maracena" title="Maracena">Maracena</a> and <a href="/wiki/Armilla,_Granada" title="Armilla, Granada">Armilla</a>, The metro was scheduled to open in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other transportation options in the city of Granada are trains, taxis or buses.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bus">Bus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Bus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The main company operating bus transport in Granada is Transportes Rober. There is also bus transportation to and from the airport with the company Alsa.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Train">Train</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Train">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Granada has rail connections with many cities in Spain. There are several types of train service to and from Granada<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Short distance trains</li>
<li>Medium distance trains</li>
<li>Long distance trains</li>
<li>AVE (High speed long distance) The closest AVE connection is in <a href="/wiki/Antequera" title="Antequera">Antequera</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxi">Taxi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Taxi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Granada has a wide network of taxis to help the traveller reach their destinations. Official Granada taxis are white with a green stripe.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Airport">Airport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Airport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The nearest airport is <a href="/wiki/Federico_Garc%C3%ADa_Lorca_Airport" title="Federico García Lorca Airport">Federico_García_Lorca_Airport</a>, about 9.4 miles (15 km) west of Granada.</p>
