			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Energy in South Korea</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>South Korea is a major energy importer, importing nearly all of its oil needs and the second-largest importer of <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_natural_gas" title="Liquefied natural gas">liquefied natural gas</a> in the world. Electricity generation in the country mainly comes from conventional thermal power, which accounts for more than two thirds of production, and from <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-eia_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eia-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Energy producers were dominated by government enterprises, although privately operated coal mines and oil refineries also existed. The <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_(South_Korea)" title="National Assembly (South Korea)">National Assembly</a> enacted a broad electricity sector restructuring program in 2000, but the restructuring process was halted amid political controversy in 2004 and remains a topic of intense political debate.<sup id="cite_ref-Pittman_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pittman-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>South Korea has no proven oil reserves.<sup id="cite_ref-eia_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eia-1">[1]</a></sup> Exploration until the 1980s in the Yellow Sea and on the continental shelf between Korea and Japan did not find any offshore oil. Coal supply in the country is insufficient and of low quality. The potential for hydroelectric power is limited because of high seasonal variations in the weather and the concentration of most of the rainfall in the summer. Accordingly, the government is increasingly focused on developing <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_South_Korea" title="Nuclear power in South Korea">nuclear power generation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_power">Electric power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Electric power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Korea_Electric_Power_Corporation" title="Korea Electric Power Corporation">Korea Electric Power Corporation</a> (KEPCO) provided electricity in the country. When KEPCO's predecessor, KECO, was founded in 1961, annual power production was 1,770 GWh. Production reached 73,992 GWh in 1987. In that year, residential customers used 17.9% of total production, public and service businesses used 16.2%, and the industrial sector used 65.9%. Sources of power generation were primarily nuclear power, coal, oil, and liquefied natural gas. Of the 54,885 GWh of electricity generated in 1985, 22% came from nuclear plants then in operation, 74% from non-nuclear thermal plants (oil and coal), and 4% from hydroelectric sites. It was predicted in 1988 that the generation structure by the year 2000 would be 10.2% hydroelectric, 12.2% oil, 22.9% coal, 10.2% LNG, and 44.5% nuclear.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_Power">Nuclear Power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nuclear Power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_South_Korea" title="Nuclear power in South Korea">Nuclear power in South Korea</a></div>
<p>South Korea placed a heavy emphasis on <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> generation. The country's first nuclear power plant, the <a href="/wiki/Kori_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Kori Nuclear Power Plant">Kori Number One</a> located near Pusan, which opened in 1977. Eight plants operated in 1987 when atomic power generation was an estimated 71,158 million kilowatts, or 53.1% of total electric power.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewable_energy">Renewable energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Renewable energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>The government decision in July 2008 to increase investment in <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> to reduce <a href="/wiki/Energy_security" title="Energy security">reliance</a> on foreign oil imports may provide an incentive for conglomerates' solar plans.<sup id="cite_ref-greentechmedia.com_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greentechmedia.com-7">[7]</a></sup> The Ministry of Knowledge and Economy said the country intends to spend 194.4 billion won ($193 million) on technologies and projects, including solar, wind and <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a>, in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-greentechmedia.com_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greentechmedia.com-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>South Korea is fast-growing gigawatt-market for <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">photovoltaics</a> (PV). In 2014, the country ranked among the world-leading <a href="/wiki/Growth_of_photovoltaics#Top_10_ranking_of_worldwide_photovoltaic_installation" title="Growth of photovoltaics">top-ten installers</a> of <a href="/wiki/PV_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="PV systems">PV systems</a>.</p>
<p>Hydro also comes under <a href="/wiki/Korea_Hydro_%26_Nuclear_Power" title="Korea Hydro &amp; Nuclear Power">Korea Hydro &amp; Nuclear Power</a> Ltd. (한국수력원자력㈜)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_warming">Global warming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_in_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Global warming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Climate_change_by_country" title="Category:Climate change by country">Category:Climate change by country</a></div>
<p>According to the Carbon Dioxide Information Analysis Center <a href="/wiki/CDIAC" class="mw-redirect" title="CDIAC">CDIAC</a> South Korea is among the top ten, namely ninth, highest country in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> emissions in the period 1950-2005. The United States (25%), China (10%) and Russia (8%) are the countries with the highest carbon dioxide emissions from 1950-2005.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
