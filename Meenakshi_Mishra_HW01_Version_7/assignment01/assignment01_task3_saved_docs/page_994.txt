<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <b>hydrophobicity</b> is the physical property of a <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a> (known as a <b>hydrophobe</b>) that is seemingly <a href="/wiki/Intermolecular_force" title="Intermolecular force">repelled</a> from a mass of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (Strictly speaking, there is no repulsive force involved; it is an absence of attraction.) In contrast, <a href="/wiki/Hydrophile" title="Hydrophile">hydrophiles</a> are attracted to water.</p>
<p>Hydrophobic molecules tend to be <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity#Nonpolar_molecules" title="Chemical polarity">nonpolar</a> and, thus, prefer other neutral molecules and nonpolar <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvents</a>. Because water molecules are polar, hydrophobes do not <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_(chemistry)" title="Dissolution (chemistry)">dissolve</a> well among them. Hydrophobic molecules in water often cluster together, forming <a href="/wiki/Micelle" title="Micelle">micelles</a>. Water on hydrophobic surfaces will exhibit a high <a href="/wiki/Contact_angle" title="Contact angle">contact angle</a>.</p>
<p>Examples of hydrophobic <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> include the <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkanes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">oils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fats</a>, and greasy substances in general. Hydrophobic materials are used for oil removal from water, the management of oil spills, and chemical separation processes to remove non-polar substances from polar compounds.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hydrophobic is often used interchangeably with <a href="/wiki/Lipophilic" class="mw-redirect" title="Lipophilic">lipophilic</a>, "fat-loving". However, the two terms are not synonymous. While hydrophobic substances are usually lipophilic, there are exceptions—such as the <a href="/wiki/Silicones" class="mw-redirect" title="Silicones">silicones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fluorocarbon" title="Fluorocarbon">fluorocarbons</a>.</p>
<p>The term <i>hydrophobe</i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> ὑδρόφοβος, "having a horror of water", constructed from ὕδωρ, "water", and φόβος, "fear".<sup id="cite_ref-Liddell_.26_Scott_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Liddell_.26_Scott-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_background">Chemical background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrophobe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Chemical background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hydrophobic_force" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrophobic force">Hydrophobic force</a></div>
<p>The hydrophobic interaction is mostly an <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropic</a> effect originating from the disruption of the highly dynamic <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bond" title="Hydrogen bond">hydrogen bonds</a> between molecules of liquid water by the nonpolar solute forming a <a href="/wiki/Clathrate" class="mw-redirect" title="Clathrate">clathrate</a>-like structure around the non-polar molecules. This structure formed is more highly ordered than free water molecules due to the water molecules arranging themselves to interact as much as possible with themselves, and thus results in a higher entropic state which causes non-polar molecules to clump together to reduce the <a href="/wiki/Accessible_surface_area" title="Accessible surface area">surface area exposed to water</a> and decrease the entropy of the system.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Thus, the two immiscible phases (hydrophilic vs. hydrophobic) will change so that their corresponding interfacial area will be minimal. This effect can be visualized in the phenomenon called <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">phase</a> separation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Superhydrophobicity">Superhydrophobicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrophobe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Superhydrophobicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Superhydrophobe" class="mw-redirect" title="Superhydrophobe">Superhydrophobe</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:DropConnectionAngel.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/DropConnectionAngel.jpg/220px-DropConnectionAngel.jpg" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/DropConnectionAngel.jpg/330px-DropConnectionAngel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/DropConnectionAngel.jpg/440px-DropConnectionAngel.jpg 2x" data-file-width="952" data-file-height="708" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:DropConnectionAngel.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A water drop on a Lotus plant leaf.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Superhydrophobic</b> surfaces, such as the leaves of the lotus plant, are those that are extremely difficult to wet. The <a href="/wiki/Contact_angle" title="Contact angle">contact angles</a> of a water droplet exceeds 150° and the roll-off angle is less than 10°.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This is referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Lotus_effect" title="Lotus effect">Lotus effect</a>, and is primarily a physical property related to <a href="/wiki/Interfacial_tension" class="mw-redirect" title="Interfacial tension">interfacial tension</a>, rather than a chemical property.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theory">Theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrophobe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1805, Thomas Young defined the contact angle <i>θ</i> by analyzing the forces acting on a fluid droplet resting on a solid surface surrounded by a gas.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">

A liquid droplet rests on a solid surface and is surrounded by gas. The contact angle, <i>θ</i><sub>C</sub>, is the angle formed by a liquid at the three-phase boundary where the liquid, gas, and solid intersect.</div>

A droplet resting on a solid surface and surrounded by a gas forms a characteristic contact angle&#160;<i>θ</i>. If the solid surface is rough, and the liquid is in intimate contact with the solid asperities, the droplet is in the Wenzel state. If the liquid rests on the tops of the asperities, it is in the Cassie–Baxter state.</div>

<p>A recent alternative criterion for the Cassie–Baxter state asserts that the Cassie–Baxter state exists when the following 2 criteria are met: 1) Contact line forces overcome body forces of unsupported droplet weight and 2) The microstructures are tall enough to prevent the liquid that bridges microstructures from touching the base of the microstructures.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>A new criterion for the switch between Wenzel and Cassie-Baxter states has been developed recently based on surface roughness and surface energy.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The criterion focuses on the air-trapping capability under liquid droplets on rough surfaces, which could tell whether Wenzel's model or Cassie-Baxter's model should be used for certain combination of surface roughness and energy.</p>
<p>Contact angle is a measure of static hydrophobicity, and contact angle hysteresis and slide angle are dynamic measures. Contact angle hysteresis is a phenomenon that characterizes surface heterogeneity.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> When a pipette injects a liquid onto a solid, the liquid will form some contact angle. As the pipette injects more liquid, the droplet will increase in volume, the contact angle will increase, but its three-phase boundary will remain stationary until it suddenly advances outward. The contact angle the droplet had immediately before advancing outward is termed the advancing contact angle. The receding contact angle is now measured by pumping the liquid back out of the droplet. The droplet will decrease in volume, the contact angle will decrease, but its three-phase boundary will remain stationary until it suddenly recedes inward. The contact angle the droplet had immediately before receding inward is termed the receding contact angle. The difference between advancing and receding contact angles is termed contact angle hysteresis and can be used to characterize surface heterogeneity, roughness, and mobility.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Please clarify the following statement or statements with a good explanation from a reliable source. (January 2015)">how?</span></a></i>]</sup> Surfaces that are not homogeneous will have domains that impede motion of the contact line.The slide angle is another dynamic measure of hydrophobicity and is measured by depositing a droplet on a surface and tilting the surface until the droplet begins to slide. In general, liquids in the Cassie–Baxter state exhibit lower slide angles and contact angle hysteresis than those in the Wenzel state.</p>

<p>Dettre and Johnson discovered in 1964 that the superhydrophobic, <a href="/wiki/Lotus_effect" title="Lotus effect">Lotus effect</a> phenomenon was related to rough hydrophobic surfaces, and they developed a theoretical model based on experiments with glass beads coated with paraffin or TFE telomer. The self-cleaning property of superhydrophobic micro-<a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">nanostructured</a> surfaces was reported in 1977.<sup id="cite_ref-Barthlott1977_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barthlott1977-15">[15]</a></sup> Perfluoroalkyl, perfluoropolyether and RF plasma formed superhydrophobic materials were developed, used for <a href="/wiki/Electrowetting" title="Electrowetting">electrowetting</a> and commercialized for bio-medical applications between 1986 and 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Other technology and applications have emerged since the mid 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-Barthlott1997_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barthlott1997-20">[20]</a></sup> A durable superhydrophobic hierarchical composition, applied in one or two steps, was disclosed in 2002 comprising nano-sized particles ≤ 100 nanometers overlaying a surface having micrometer-sized features or particles ≤ 100 micrometers. The larger particles were observed to protect the smaller particles from mechanical abrasion.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In recent research, superhydrophobicity has been reported by allowing alkylketene <a href="/wiki/Dimer_(chemistry)" title="Dimer (chemistry)">dimer</a> (AKD) to solidify into a nanostructured fractal surface.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Many papers have since presented fabrication methods for producing superhydrophobic surfaces including particle deposition,<sup id="cite_ref-Miwa_2000_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miwa_2000-23">[23]</a></sup> sol-gel techniques,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> plasma treatments,<sup id="cite_ref-TeareSpanos2002_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TeareSpanos2002-25">[25]</a></sup> vapor deposition,<sup id="cite_ref-Miwa_2000_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miwa_2000-23">[23]</a></sup> and casting techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Current opportunity for research impact lies mainly in fundamental research and practical manufacturing.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Debates have recently emerged concerning the applicability of the Wenzel and Cassie–Baxter models. In an experiment designed to challenge the surface energy perspective of the Wenzel and Cassie–Baxter model and promote a contact line perspective, water drops were placed on a smooth hydrophobic spot in a rough hydrophobic field, a rough hydrophobic spot in a smooth hydrophobic field, and a hydrophilic spot in a hydrophobic field.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Experiments showed that the surface chemistry and geometry at the contact line affected the contact angle and contact angle hysteresis, but the surface area inside the contact line had no effect. An argument that increased jaggedness in the contact line enhances droplet mobility has also been proposed.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many hydrophobic materials found in nature rely on <a href="/wiki/Cassie%27s_law" title="Cassie's law">Cassie's law</a> and are <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">biphasic</a> on the submicrometer level with one component air. The Lotus effect is based on this principle. Inspired by it, a lot of functional superhydrophobic surfaces were prepared.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>An example of a <a href="/wiki/Bionics" title="Bionics">biomimetic</a> superhydrophobic material in <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">nanotechnology</a> is <a href="/wiki/Nanopin_film" title="Nanopin film">nanopin film</a>. In one study, a <a href="/wiki/Vanadium_pentoxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Vanadium pentoxide">vanadium pentoxide</a> surface that can switch reversibly between superhydrophobicity and <a href="/wiki/Superhydrophilicity" title="Superhydrophilicity">superhydrophilicity</a> under the influence of UV radiation is presented.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> According to the study, any surface can be modified to this effect by application of a <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(chemistry)" title="Suspension (chemistry)">suspension</a> of rose-like V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> particles, for instance with an <a href="/wiki/Inkjet_printer" class="mw-redirect" title="Inkjet printer">inkjet printer</a>. Once again hydrophobicity is induced by interlaminar air pockets (separated by 2.1 <a href="/wiki/Nanometer" class="mw-redirect" title="Nanometer">nm</a> distances). The UV effect is also explained. UV light creates <a href="/wiki/Electron-hole_pair" class="mw-redirect" title="Electron-hole pair">electron-hole pairs</a>, with the holes reacting with lattice oxygen, creating surface oxygen vacancies, while the electrons reduce V<sup>5+</sup> to V<sup>3+</sup>. The oxygen vacancies are met by water, and it is this water absorbency by the vanadium surface that makes it hydrophilic. By extended storage in the dark, water is replaced by oxygen and <a href="/wiki/Hydrophilicity" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrophilicity">hydrophilicity</a> is once again lost.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_applications">Potential applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrophobe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Potential applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Active recent research on superhydrophobic materials might eventually lead to industrial applications. For example, a simple routine of coating cotton fabric with <a href="/wiki/Silica" class="mw-redirect" title="Silica">silica</a><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Titanium_dioxide" title="Titanium dioxide">titania</a><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> particles by <a href="/wiki/Sol-gel" title="Sol-gel">sol-gel</a> technique has been reported, which protects the fabric from UV light and makes it superhydrophobic. Also, an efficient routine has been reported for making <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a> superhydrophobic and thus self-cleaning.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>—99% of dirt absorbed on such surface is easily washed away. Patterned superhydrophobic surfaces also have promise for lab-on-a-chip microfluidic devices and can drastically improve surface-based bioanalysis.<sup id="cite_ref-Ressine2007_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ressine2007-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
