<p>The <b>triple-alpha process</b> is a set of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> reactions by which three <a href="/wiki/Helium-4" title="Helium-4">helium-4</a> nuclei (<a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">alpha particles</a>) are transformed into <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Appenzeller_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Appenzeller-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ostlie_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ostlie-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Triple-Alpha_process_in_stars">Triple-Alpha process in stars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triple-alpha_process&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Triple-Alpha process in stars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">Helium</a> accumulates in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_core" title="Solar core">core</a> of stars as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Proton%E2%80%93proton_chain_reaction" title="Proton–proton chain reaction">proton–proton chain reaction</a> and the <a href="/wiki/CNO_cycle" title="CNO cycle">carbon–nitrogen–oxygen cycle</a>. Further nuclear fusion reactions of helium with hydrogen or another alpha particle produce <a href="/wiki/Lithium-5" class="mw-redirect" title="Lithium-5">lithium-5</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beryllium-8" class="mw-redirect" title="Beryllium-8">beryllium-8</a> respectively. Both products are highly unstable and decay, almost instantly, back into smaller nuclei, unless a third alpha particle fuses with a beryllium before that time to produce a stable <a href="/wiki/Carbon-12" title="Carbon-12">carbon-12</a> nucleus.<sup id="cite_ref-Audi_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>When a star runs out of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> to fuse in its <a href="/wiki/Stellar_core" class="mw-redirect" title="Stellar core">core</a>, it begins to collapse until the central temperature rises to 10<sup>8</sup> K,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> six times hotter than the sun's core. At this temperature and density, alpha particles are able to fuse rapidly enough (the half-life of <sup>5</sup>Li is <span class="nowrap"><span style="display:none" class="sortkey">6978370000000000000♠</span>3.7<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−22</sup>&#160;s</span> and that of <sup>8</sup>Be is <span class="nowrap"><span style="display:none" class="sortkey">6983670000000000000♠</span>6.7<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−17</sup>&#160;s</span>) to produce significant amounts of carbon and restore <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium" title="Thermodynamic equilibrium">thermodynamic equilibrium</a> in the core</p>

<p>The net energy release of the process is 7.273 MeV (<span class="nowrap"><span style="display:none" class="sortkey">6988116600000000000♠</span>1.166&#160;<a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">pJ</a></span>).</p>
<p>As a side effect of the process, some carbon nuclei fuse with additional helium to produce a stable isotope of oxygen and energy:</p>

<p>See <a href="/wiki/Alpha_process" title="Alpha process">alpha process</a> for more details about this reaction and further steps in the chain of <a href="/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis" title="Stellar nucleosynthesis">stellar nucleosynthesis</a>.</p>
<p>This creates a situation in which stellar nucleosynthesis produces large amounts of carbon and oxygen but only a small fraction of those elements are converted into neon and heavier elements. Both oxygen and carbon make up the 'ash' of helium-4 burning.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Primordial_carbon">Primordial carbon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triple-alpha_process&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Primordial carbon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because the triple-alpha process is unlikely, it normally needs a long time to produce much carbon. One consequence of this is that no significant amount of carbon was <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang_nucleosynthesis" title="Big Bang nucleosynthesis">produced in the Big Bang</a> because within minutes after the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a>, the temperature fell below the critical point for nuclear fusion.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Resonances">Resonances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triple-alpha_process&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Resonances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ordinarily, the probability of the triple alpha process is extremely small. However, the beryllium-8 ground state has almost exactly the energy of two alpha particles. In the second step, <sup>8</sup>Be + <sup>4</sup>He has almost exactly the energy of an <a href="/wiki/Excited_state" title="Excited state">excited state</a> of <sup>12</sup>C. This "<a href="/wiki/Resonance_(particle_physics)" title="Resonance (particle physics)">resonance</a>" greatly increases the probability that an incoming alpha particle will combine with beryllium-8 to form carbon. The existence of this resonance was predicted by <a href="/wiki/Fred_Hoyle" title="Fred Hoyle">Fred Hoyle</a> before its actual observation, based on the physical necessity for it to exist, in order for carbon to be formed in stars. The prediction and then discovery of this energy resonance and process gave very significant support to Hoyle's hypothesis of <a href="/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis" title="Stellar nucleosynthesis">stellar nucleosynthesis</a>, which posited that all chemical elements had originally been formed from hydrogen, the true primordial substance. The <a href="/wiki/Anthropic_principle" title="Anthropic principle">anthropic principle</a> has been controversially cited to explain the fact that nuclear resonances are sensitively arranged to create large amounts of carbon and oxygen in the Universe.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nucleosynthesis_of_heavy_elements">Nucleosynthesis of heavy elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triple-alpha_process&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Nucleosynthesis of heavy elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With further increases of temperature and density, fusion processes produce <a href="/wiki/Nuclide" title="Nuclide">nuclides</a> only up to <a href="/wiki/Nickel-56" class="mw-redirect" title="Nickel-56">nickel-56</a> (which decays later to <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>); heavier <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">elements</a> (those beyond Ni) are created mainly by neutron capture. The slow capture of neutrons, the <a href="/wiki/S-process" title="S-process">s-process</a>, produces about half of elements beyond iron. The other half are produced by rapid neutron capture, the <a href="/wiki/R-process" title="R-process">r-process</a>, which probably occurs in a <a href="/wiki/Core-collapse_supernova" class="mw-redirect" title="Core-collapse supernova">core-collapse supernova</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_rate_and_stellar_evolution">Reaction rate and stellar evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triple-alpha_process&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reaction rate and stellar evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The triple-alpha steps are strongly dependent on the temperature and density of the stellar material. The power released by the reaction is approximately proportional to the temperature to the 40th power, and the density squared.<sup id="cite_ref-Carroll_and_Motliest_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll_and_Motliest-6">[6]</a></sup> In contrast, the <a href="/wiki/Proton%E2%80%93proton_chain_reaction" title="Proton–proton chain reaction">PP chain</a> produces energy at a rate proportional to the fourth power of temperature, the <a href="/wiki/CNO_cycle" title="CNO cycle">CNO cycle</a> at about the 17th power of the temperature, and both are linearly proportional to the density. This strong temperature dependence has consequences for the late stage of stellar evolution, the <a href="/wiki/Red_giant" title="Red giant">red giant</a> stage.</p>
<p>For lower mass stars, the helium accumulating in the core is prevented from further collapse only by <a href="/wiki/Degenerate_matter" title="Degenerate matter">electron degeneracy</a> pressure. As the temperature rises, increased pressure in the core would normally result in an expansion, reduction of density, and thus reduction in reaction rate. However, due to the high pressure at the center of the star this does not occur and energy production continues unmoderated. As a consequence, the temperature increases, causing the reaction rate further increase in a positive feedback cycle that becomes a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_runaway" title="Thermal runaway">runaway</a> reaction. This process, known as the <a href="/wiki/Helium_flash" title="Helium flash">helium flash</a>, lasts a matter of seconds but burns 60–80% of the helium in the core. During the core flash, the star's <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">energy production</a> can reach approximately 10<sup>11</sup> <a href="/wiki/Solar_luminosity" title="Solar luminosity">solar luminosities</a> which is comparable to the <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminosity</a> of a whole <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxy</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Carroll_and_Ostlie_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll_and_Ostlie-7">[7]</a></sup> although no effects will be immediately observed at the surface, as it is hidden by the star's overlying layers.</p>
<p>For higher mass stars, carbon collects in the core, displacing the helium to a surrounding shell where helium burning occurs. In this helium shell, the pressures are lower and the mass is not supported by electron degeneracy. Thus, as opposed to the center of the star, the shell is able to expand in response to increased thermal pressure in the helium shell. Expansion cools this layer and slows the reaction, causing the star to contract again. This process continues cyclically, and stars undergoing this process will have periodically variable radius and power production. These stars will also lose material from their outer layers as they expand and contract.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triple-alpha_process&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The triple alpha process is highly dependent on <a href="/wiki/Carbon-12" title="Carbon-12">carbon-12</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beryllium-8" class="mw-redirect" title="Beryllium-8">beryllium-8</a> having resonances with slightly more energy than <a href="/wiki/Helium-4" title="Helium-4">helium-4</a>, and before 1952, no such energy levels were known for carbon. The astrophysicist <a href="/wiki/Fred_Hoyle" title="Fred Hoyle">Fred Hoyle</a> used the fact that carbon-12 is abundant in the universe as evidence for the existence of a carbon-12 resonance. The only way Hoyle could find that would produce an abundance of both carbon and oxygen is through a triple alpha process with a carbon-12 resonance near 7.68&#160;MeV.<sup id="cite_ref-Kragh_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kragh-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hoyle went to nuclear physicist <a href="/wiki/William_Alfred_Fowler" title="William Alfred Fowler">William Alfred Fowler</a>'s lab at <a href="/wiki/Caltech" class="mw-redirect" title="Caltech">Caltech</a> and said that there had to be a resonance of 7.68 MeV in the carbon-12 nucleus. (There had been reports of an excited state at about 7.5&#160;MeV.<sup id="cite_ref-Kragh_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kragh-8">[8]</a></sup>) Fred Hoyle's audacity in doing this is remarkable, and initially the nuclear physicists in the lab were skeptical. Finally, a junior physicist, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ward_Whaling&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ward Whaling (page does not exist)">Ward Whaling</a>, fresh from <a href="/wiki/Rice_University" title="Rice University">Rice University</a>, who was looking for a project decided to look for the resonance. Fowler gave Ward permission to use an old <a href="/wiki/Van_de_Graaff_generator" title="Van de Graaff generator">Van de Graaff generator</a> that was not being used. Hoyle was back in Cambridge when his prediction was verified a few months later. The nuclear physicists put Hoyle as first author on a paper delivered by Ward Whaling at the Summer meeting of the <a href="/wiki/American_Physical_Society" title="American Physical Society">American Physical Society</a>. A long and fruitful collaboration between Hoyle and Fowler soon followed, with Fowler even coming to Cambridge.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> By 1952, Fowler had noted the beryllium-8 resonance, and <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Salpeter" class="mw-redirect" title="Edwin Salpeter">Edwin Salpeter</a> calculated the reaction rate taking this resonance into account.<sup id="cite_ref-Salpeter_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salpeter-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>This helped to explain the rate of the process, but the rate calculated by Salpeter seemed too low at the temperatures expected in supernovas.<sup id="cite_ref-Kragh_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kragh-8">[8]</a></sup> When Fowler's lab discovered a carbon-12 resonance near 7.65 MeV it eliminated the discrepancy between the nuclear theory and the theory of stellar evolution.</p>
<p>The final reaction product lies in a 0<sup>+</sup> state (0 spin and positive parity). Since the Hoyle State was predicted to be either a 0<sup>+</sup> or a 2<sup>+</sup> state, electron–positron pairs or <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma rays</a> were expected to be seen. However, when experiments were carried out, the gamma emission reaction channel was not observed, and this meant the state must be a 0+ state. This state completely suppresses single gamma emission, since single gamma emission must carry away at least 1 <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum_operator" title="Angular momentum operator">unit of angular momentum</a>. <a href="/wiki/Pair_production" title="Pair production">Pair production</a> from an excited 0+ state is possible because their combined spins (0) can couple to a reaction that has a change in angular momentum of 0.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Improbability_and_fine-tuning">Improbability and fine-tuning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triple-alpha_process&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Improbability and fine-tuning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fine-tuned_universe" class="mw-redirect" title="Fine-tuned universe">Fine-tuned universe</a></div>
<p>Carbon is a necessary component of all life that we know. <sup>12</sup>C, a stable isotope of carbon, is abundantly produced in stars due to three factors:</p>
<ol>
<li>The decay lifetime of a <sup>8</sup>Be nucleus is four orders of magnitude larger than the time for two <sup>4</sup>He nuclei (alpha particles) to scatter.<sup id="cite_ref-uzan_2003_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uzan_2003-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>An excited state of the <sup>12</sup>C nucleus exists just above the energy level <sup>8</sup>Be + <sup>4</sup>He. This is necessary because the ground state of <sup>12</sup>C is 7.3367 MeV below the energy of <sup>8</sup>Be + <sup>4</sup>He. Therefore, a <sup>8</sup>Be nucleus and a <sup>4</sup>He nucleus cannot reasonably fuse directly into a ground-state <sup>12</sup>C nucleus. The excited Hoyle state of <sup>12</sup>C is 7.656 MeV above the ground state of <sup>12</sup>C. This allows <sup>8</sup>Be and <sup>4</sup>He to use the kinetic energy of their collision to fuse into the excited <sup>12</sup>C, which can then transition to its stable ground state. According to one calculation, the energy level of this excited state must be between about 7.3 and 7.9 MeV to produce sufficient carbon for life to exist, and must be further "fine-tuned" to between 7.596 MeV and 7.716 MeV in order to produce the abundant level of <sup>12</sup>C observed in nature.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the reaction <sup>12</sup>C + <sup>4</sup>He going to <sup>16</sup>O there is an excited state of oxygen which, if it were slightly higher, would provide a resonance and speed up the reaction. In that case insufficient carbon would exist in nature; it would almost all have converted to oxygen.<sup id="cite_ref-uzan_2003_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uzan_2003-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>Some scholars argue the 7.656 MeV Hoyle resonance, in particular, is unlikely to be the product of mere chance. <a href="/wiki/Fred_Hoyle" title="Fred Hoyle">Fred Hoyle</a> famously argued in 1982 the Hoyle resonance was evidence of a "superintellect";<sup id="cite_ref-Kragh_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kragh-8">[8]</a></sup> the scientific community rejects Hoyle's <a href="/wiki/Intelligent_design" title="Intelligent design">intelligent design</a> argument as unparsimonious.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Instead, some scientists believe different regions of a vast "<a href="/wiki/Multiverse" title="Multiverse">multiverse</a>" have different fundamental constants:<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> according to this controversial <a href="/wiki/Fine-tuned_universe" class="mw-redirect" title="Fine-tuned universe">fine-tuning</a> hypothesis, life can only evolve in rare patches of the multiverse where the fundamental constants are fine-tuned to support the existence of life. Other scientists reject a multiverse, arguing instead that the resonance is just an overblown coincidence.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
