Solar neutrinos (<a href="/wiki/Proton-proton_chain_reaction" class="mw-redirect" title="Proton-proton chain reaction">proton-proton chain</a>) in the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Solar_Model" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard Solar Model">Standard Solar Model</a></div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Electron_neutrino" title="Electron neutrino">Electron neutrinos</a> are produced in the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> as a product of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a>. Solar neutrinos constitute by far the largest flux of neutrinos from natural sources observed on Earth, as compared with e.g. <a href="/wiki/Neutrino#Atmospheric" title="Neutrino">atmospheric neutrinos</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Diffuse_supernova_neutrino_background" title="Diffuse supernova neutrino background">diffuse supernova neutrino background</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observed_data">Observed data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_neutrino&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Observed data">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The highest flux of solar neutrinos come directly from the proton-proton interaction, and have a low energy, up to 400 keV. There are also several other significant production mechanisms, with energies up to 18 MeV.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> From the Earth, the amount of neutrino flux at Earth is around 7·10<sup>10</sup>&#160;particles/cm<sup>2</sup>/s.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The number of neutrinos can be predicted by the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Solar_Model" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard Solar Model">Standard Solar Model</a>. The detected number of electron neutrinos was only 1/3 of the predicted number, and this was known as the <a href="/wiki/Solar_neutrino_problem" title="Solar neutrino problem">solar neutrino problem</a>. It led to the idea of <a href="/wiki/Neutrino_oscillation" title="Neutrino oscillation">neutrino oscillation</a> and the fact that neutrinos can change flavour. This was confirmed when the total flux of solar neutrinos of all types was measured and it agreed with the earlier predictions of expected electron neutrino flux, as seen by <a href="/wiki/Sudbury_Neutrino_Observatory" title="Sudbury Neutrino Observatory">Sudbury Neutrino Observatory</a>, and thus confirmed that neutrinos have mass. Solar models additionally predict the location within the Sun's core where solar neutrinos should originate from, depending on the nuclear fusion reaction which leads to their production. Future neutrino detectors will be able to detect the incoming direction of these neutrinos with enough precision to measure this effect.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The energy spectrum of solar neutrinos is also predicted by solar models.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It is essential to know this energy spectrum because different neutrino detection experiments are sensitive to different neutrino energy ranges. The <a href="/wiki/Homestake_Experiment" class="mw-redirect" title="Homestake Experiment">Homestake Experiment</a> used <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> and was most sensitive to solar neutrinos produced by the decay of the <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_beryllium" title="Isotopes of beryllium">beryllium isotope</a> <sup>7</sup>Be. The <a href="/wiki/Sudbury_Neutrino_Observatory" title="Sudbury Neutrino Observatory">Sudbury Neutrino Observatory</a> is most sensitive to solar neutrinos produced by <sup>8</sup>B. The detectors that use <a href="/wiki/Gallium" title="Gallium">gallium</a> are most sensitive to the solar neutrinos produced by the proton-proton chain reaction process. In 2012 the collaboration known as <a href="/wiki/Borexino" title="Borexino">Borexino</a> reported detecting low-energy neutrinos for the proton-electron-proton (<a href="/wiki/Proton-proton_chain_reaction#The_pep_reaction" class="mw-redirect" title="Proton-proton chain reaction">pep reaction</a>) that produces 1 in 400 deuterium nuclei in the sun.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The detector contained 100 metric tons of liquid and saw on average 3 events each day (due to <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_carbon#Carbon-11" title="Isotopes of carbon">carbon 11 production</a>) from this relatively uncommon <a href="/wiki/Thermonuclear" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermonuclear">thermonuclear</a> reaction.</p>
<p>Neutrinos can trigger nuclear reactions. By looking at ancient ores of various ages that have been exposed to solar neutrinos over geologic time, it may be possible to interrogate the luminosity of the Sun over time,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> which, according to the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Solar_Model" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard Solar Model">Standard Solar Model</a>, has changed with time.</p>
