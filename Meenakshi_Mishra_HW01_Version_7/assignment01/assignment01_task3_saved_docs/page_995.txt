<p>A <b>hydrophile</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Molecular_entity" title="Molecular entity">molecular entity</a> that is <a href="/wiki/Intermolecular_force" title="Intermolecular force">attracted</a> to <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> molecules and tends to be <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_(chemistry)" title="Dissolution (chemistry)">dissolved</a> by water.<sup id="cite_ref-Liddell_.26_Scott_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Liddell_.26_Scott-1">[1]</a></sup> In contrast, <a href="/wiki/Hydrophobe" title="Hydrophobe">hydrophobes</a> are not attracted to water and may seem to be repelled by it.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecules">Molecules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Molecules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A hydrophilic <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a> or portion of a molecule is one whose interactions with water and other polar substances are more <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamically</a> favorable than their interactions with oil or other <a href="/wiki/Hydrophobic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrophobic">hydrophobic</a> solvents.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> They are typically charge-polarized and capable of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bond" title="Hydrogen bond">hydrogen bonding</a>. This makes these molecules soluble not only in water but also in other <a href="/wiki/Solvent#Solvent_classifications" title="Solvent">polar solvents</a>.</p>
<p>Hydrophilic molecules (and portions of molecules) can be contrasted with hydrophobic molecules (and portions of molecules). In some cases, both hydrophilic and hydrophobic properties occur in a single molecule. An example of these <a href="/wiki/Amphiphile" title="Amphiphile">amphiphilic molecules</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a> that comprise the <a href="/wiki/Cell_membrane" title="Cell membrane">cell membrane</a>. Another example is <a href="/wiki/Soap" title="Soap">soap</a>, which has a hydrophilic head and a hydrophobic tail, allowing it to dissolve in both water and oil.</p>
<p>Hydrophilic and hydrophobic molecules are also known as <a href="/wiki/Polar_molecules" class="mw-redirect" title="Polar molecules">polar molecules</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nonpolar_molecule" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonpolar molecule">nonpolar molecules</a>, respectively. Some hydrophilic substances do not dissolve. This type of mixture is called a <a href="/wiki/Colloid" title="Colloid">colloid</a>.</p>
<p>An approximate rule of thumb for hydrophilicity of <a href="/wiki/Organic_compounds" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic compounds">organic compounds</a> is that <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">solubility</a> of a molecule in water is more than 1 <a href="/wiki/Concentration#Mass_percentage_.28fraction.29" title="Concentration">mass %</a> if there is at least one neutral hydrophile group per 5 carbons, or at least one electrically charged hydrophile group per 7 carbons.<sup id="cite_ref-Pedersen_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pedersen-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hydrophilic substances (ex: salts) can seem to attract water out of the air. Sugar is also hydrophilic, and like salt is sometimes used to draw water out of foods. Sugar sprinkled on cut fruit will "draw out the water" through hydrophilia, making the fruit mushy and wet, as in a common strawberry <a href="/wiki/Compote" title="Compote">compote</a> recipe.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemicals">Chemicals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chemicals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Liquid hydrophilic chemicals complexed with solid chemicals can be used to optimize solubility of hydrophobic chemicals.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liquid_chemicals">Liquid chemicals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Liquid chemicals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Alcohols">Alcohols</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Alcohols">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Alcohol#Solvents" title="Alcohol">Alcohol §&#160;Solvents</a></div>
<p>Hydroxyl groups (-OH), found in alcohols, are <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity" title="Chemical polarity">polar</a> and therefore <a href="/wiki/Hydrophilic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrophilic">hydrophilic</a> (water loving) but their carbon chain portion is <a href="/wiki/Non-polar" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-polar">non-polar</a> which make them hydrophobic. The molecule increasingly becomes overall more nonpolar and therefore less soluble in the polar water as the carbon chain becomes longer.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Methanol has the shortest carbon chain of all alcohols (one carbon atom) followed by ethanol (two carbon atoms), and <a href="/wiki/1-Propanol" title="1-Propanol">1-propanol</a> along with its isomer <a href="/wiki/Isopropyl_alcohol" title="Isopropyl alcohol">2-propanol</a>, all being <a href="/wiki/Miscibility" title="Miscibility">miscible</a> with water. <a href="/wiki/Tert-Butyl_alcohol" title="Tert-Butyl alcohol">Tert-Butyl alcohol</a>, with four carbon atoms, is the only one among its isomers to be miscible with water.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solid_chemicals">Solid chemicals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Solid chemicals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cyclodextrins">Cyclodextrins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cyclodextrins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cyclodextrin#Uses" title="Cyclodextrin">Cyclodextrin §&#160;Uses</a></div>
<p>Cyclodextrins are used to make pharmaceutical solutions by capturing hydrophobic molecules as guest hosts. Because inclusion compounds of cyclodextrins with hydrophobic molecules are able to penetrate body tissues, these can be used to release biologically active compounds under specific conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> For example, the Joseph Pitha study showed that when testosterone was complexed with hydroxy-propyl-beta-cyclodextrin (HPBCD), that 95% absorption of testosterone was achieved in 20 minutes via the sublingual route but HPBCD was not absorbed, whereas normally hydrophobic testosterone is absorbed less than around 40% normally via sublingual route.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Membrane_filtration">Membrane filtration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Membrane filtration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hydrophilic <a href="/wiki/Membrane_technology" title="Membrane technology">membrane filtration</a> is used in several industries to filter various liquids. These hydrophilic filters are used in the medical, industrial, and biochemical fields to filter such elements as bacteria, viruses, proteins, particulates, drugs, and other contaminates. Common hydrophilic molecules include colloids, cotton, and cellulose (which cotton consists of).</p>
<p>Unlike other membranes, hydrophilic membranes do not require pre-wetting: they can filter liquids in their dry state. Although most are used in low-heat filtration processes, many new hydrophilic membrane fabrics are used to filter hot liquids and fluids.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
