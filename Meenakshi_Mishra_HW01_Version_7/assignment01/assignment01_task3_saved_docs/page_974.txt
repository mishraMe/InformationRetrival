<p>The <b>Sun Belt</b> is a region of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> generally considered to stretch across the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">Southeast</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southwest_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Southwest United States">Southwest</a>. Another rough definition of the region is the area south of the <a href="/wiki/36th_parallel_north" title="36th parallel north">36th parallel</a>.</p>
<p>The main defining feature of the Sun Belt is its warm climate, with extended summers and brief, relatively mild winters. Within the region, <a href="/wiki/Desert_climate" title="Desert climate">desert</a>/<a href="/wiki/Semi-arid_climate" title="Semi-arid climate">semi-desert</a> (<a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>), <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">Mediterranean</a> (California), <a href="/wiki/Humid_subtropical" class="mw-redirect" title="Humid subtropical">humid subtropical</a> (Oklahoma, Texas, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, and <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_climate" title="Tropical climate">tropical</a> (Florida) climates can be found.</p>
<p>The Sun Belt has seen substantial population growth since the 1960s from an influx of people seeking a warm and sunny climate, a surge in retiring <a href="/wiki/Baby_boomers" title="Baby boomers">baby boomers</a>, and growing economic opportunities. The advent of <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">air conditioning</a> made it easier for people to deal with the summertime heat in the <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_United_States" title="Southwestern United States">Desert Southwest</a>, where triple-digit temperatures in <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">Fahrenheit</a> (higher than 37.7 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Celsius</a>) are common. In recent years, <a href="/wiki/Water_shortage" class="mw-redirect" title="Water shortage">water shortages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Droughts" class="mw-redirect" title="Droughts">droughts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Illegal_drug_trade" title="Illegal drug trade">drug trafficking</a> near the Mexican border have become problems in the Southwest.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sun_Belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Sun Belt comprises the southern tier of the United States, including the states of Alabama, Arizona, Florida, Georgia, Louisiana, Mississippi, New Mexico, South Carolina, Texas, roughly two-thirds of California (up to <a href="/wiki/Greater_Sacramento" class="mw-redirect" title="Greater Sacramento">Greater Sacramento</a>), and parts of <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a>, North Carolina, Nevada, and Oklahoma. Five of the states—Arizona, California, Florida, Nevada, and Texas—are sometimes collectively called the <b>Sand States</b> because of their abundance of beaches or deserts.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>First employed by political analyst <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Phillips_(political_commentator)" title="Kevin Phillips (political commentator)">Kevin Phillips</a> in his 1969 book <i>The Emerging Republican Majority</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> the term "Sun Belt" became synonymous with the southern third of the nation in the early 1970s. In this period, economic and political prominence shifted from the <a href="/wiki/Midwest_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Midwest United States">Midwest</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northeast_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Northeast United States">Northeast</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">South</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_United_States" title="Western United States">West</a>. Factors such as the warmer climate, the migration of workers from <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, and a boom in the agriculture industry allowed the southern third of the United States to grow economically. The climate spurred not only agricultural growth, but also the migration of many retirees to retirement communities in the region, especially in Florida and Arizona.</p>
<p>Industries such as <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_industry#Aerospace_manufacturing" class="mw-redirect" title="Aerospace industry">aerospace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Defense_industry" class="mw-redirect" title="Defense industry">defense</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_industry" title="Petroleum industry">oil</a> boomed in the Sun Belt as companies took advantage of the low involvement of <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">labor unions</a> in the region (due to more recent industrialization, 1930s–1950s) and the proximity of <a href="/wiki/Military_base" title="Military base">military installations</a> that were major consumers of their products. The oil industry helped propel states such as Texas and Louisiana forward, and tourism grew in Florida and <a href="/wiki/Southern_California" title="Southern California">Southern California</a>. More recently, <a href="/wiki/High_tech" title="High tech">high tech</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_economy" title="New economy">new economy</a> industries have been major drivers of growth in California, Florida, Texas, and other parts of the Sun Belt. Texas and California rank among the top five states in the nation with the most <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune&#160;500</a> companies.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Projections">Projections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sun_Belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Projections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2005, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Census_Bureau" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Census Bureau">U.S.&#160;Census Bureau</a> projected that approximately 88% of the nation's&#160;population growth between 2000 and 2030 would occur in the Sun Belt.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> California, Texas, and Florida were each expected to add more than 12&#160;million people during that time, which would make them by far the most populous states in America. Nevada, Arizona, Florida, and Texas were expected to be the fastest-growing states.</p>
<p>Events leading up to and including the <a href="/wiki/Great_Recession_in_the_United_States" title="Great Recession in the United States">2008–2009 recession</a> led some to question whether growth projections for the Sun Belt had been overstated.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The economic bubble that led to the recession appeared, to some observers, to have been more acute in the Sun Belt than other parts of the country. Additionally, the traditional lure of cheaper labor markets in the region compared with America's older industrial centers has been eroded by overseas <a href="/wiki/Outsourcing" title="Outsourcing">outsourcing</a> trends.</p>
<p>One of the greatest threats facing the belt in the coming decades is water shortages.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Communities in California are making plans to build multiple <a href="/wiki/Desalination" title="Desalination">desalination</a> plants to supply fresh water and avert near-term crises.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Texas, Georgia, and Florida also face increasingly serious shortages because of their rapidly expanding populations.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lingering effects from the Great Recession slowed down, and in some places even stopped, the migration from the <a href="/wiki/Frost_Belt" title="Frost Belt">Frost Belt</a> to the Sun Belt, according to data tracking people's movements over the year from July 2012–2013. Americans remained cautious about moving to a different state over this period.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> However, migration to the Sun Belt from the Frost Belt resumed again, according to 2015 Census data estimates, with growing migration to the Sun Belt and out of the Frost Belt and California.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environment">Environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sun_Belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The environment in the belt is extremely valuable, not only to local and state governments, but to the federal government. Eight of the ten states have extremely high <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> (ranging from 3,800 to 6,700 species, not including marine life).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The Sun Belt also has the highest number of distinct ecosystems: <a href="/wiki/Chaparral" title="Chaparral">chaparral</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a>, <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grasslands" class="mw-redirect" title="Grasslands">grasslands</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest" title="Tropical rainforest">tropical rainforest</a>.</p>

<p>The five largest metropolitan statistical areas are <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_metropolitan_area" title="Los Angeles metropolitan area">Los Angeles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dallas%E2%80%93Fort_Worth_metroplex" title="Dallas–Fort Worth metroplex">Dallas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greater_Houston" title="Greater Houston">Houston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miami_Metropolitan_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Miami Metropolitan Area">Miami</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Metro_Atlanta" class="mw-redirect" title="Metro Atlanta">Atlanta</a>. The Los Angeles area is by far the largest, with over 13&#160;million inhabitants as of 2012<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sun_Belt&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>. The ten largest metropolitan statistical areas are found in California, Texas, Georgia, North Carolina, Florida, and Arizona.<sup id="cite_ref-US_Mayors_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Mayors-20">[20]</a></sup> Additionally, the cross-border metropolitan areas of <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_%E2%80%93_Tijuana_metropolitan_area" class="mw-redirect" title="San Diego – Tijuana metropolitan area">San Diego-Tijuana</a> and <a href="/wiki/El_Paso%E2%80%93Ju%C3%A1rez" title="El Paso–Juárez">El Paso–Juárez</a> lie partially within the Sun Belt. Seven of the ten largest cities in the United States are located in the Sun Belt: <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> (2), <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a> (4), <a href="/wiki/Phoenix,_Arizona" title="Phoenix, Arizona">Phoenix</a> (6), <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio" title="San Antonio">San Antonio</a> (7), <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a> (8), <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a> (9), and <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose</a> (10).</p>
