<p><b>Cadmium sulfide</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the formula CdS. Cadmium sulfide is a yellow solid.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiberg.26Holleman_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg.26Holleman-4">[4]</a></sup> It occurs in nature with two different crystal structures as the rare minerals <a href="/wiki/Greenockite" title="Greenockite">greenockite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hawleyite" title="Hawleyite">hawleyite</a>, but is more prevalent as an impurity substituent in the similarly structured <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> ores <a href="/wiki/Sphalerite" title="Sphalerite">sphalerite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wurtzite" title="Wurtzite">wurtzite</a>, which are the major economic sources of cadmium. As a compound that is easy to isolate and purify, it is the principal source of <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a> for all commercial applications.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiberg.26Holleman_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg.26Holleman-4">[4]</a></sup> Its vivid yellow color led to its adoption as a pigment for the yellow paint "cadmium yellow" in the 18th century.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium_sulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cadmium sulfide can be prepared by the precipitation from soluble cadmium(II) salts with sulfide ion. This reaction has been used for gravimetric analysis and <a href="/wiki/Qualitative_inorganic_analysis" title="Qualitative inorganic analysis">qualitative inorganic analysis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><br />
The preparative route and the subsequent treatment of the product, affects the <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphic</a> form that is produced (i.e., cubic vs hexagonal). It has been asserted that chemical precipitation methods result in the <a href="/wiki/Zincblende_(crystal_structure)" class="mw-redirect" title="Zincblende (crystal structure)">cubic zincblende form</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pigment production usually involves the precipitation of CdS, the washing of the solid precipitate to remove soluble cadmium salts followed by calcination (roasting) to convert it to the hexagonal form followed by milling to produce a powder.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-7">[7]</a></sup> When cadmium sulfide selenides are required the CdSe is co-precipitated with CdS and the cadmium sulfoselenide is created during the calcination step.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cadmium sulfide is sometimes associated with sulfate reducing bacteria.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Routes_to_thin_films_of_CdS">Routes to thin films of CdS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium_sulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Routes to thin films of CdS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Special methods are used to produce films of CdS as components in some photoresistors and solar cells. In the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bath_deposition" title="Chemical bath deposition">chemical bath deposition</a> method, thin films of CdS have been prepared using <a href="/wiki/Thiourea" title="Thiourea">thiourea</a> as the source of sulfide anions and an <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a> <a href="/wiki/Buffer_solution" title="Buffer solution">buffer solution</a> to control pH:<sup id="cite_ref-Oladeji_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oladeji-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>Cd<sup>2+</sup> + H<sub>2</sub>O + (NH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub>CS + 2 NH<sub>3</sub> → CdS + (NH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub>CO + 2 NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Cadmium sulfide can be produced using <a href="/wiki/Metalorganic_vapour_phase_epitaxy" title="Metalorganic vapour phase epitaxy">metalorganic vapour phase epitaxy</a> and <a href="/wiki/MOCVD" class="mw-redirect" title="MOCVD">MOCVD</a> techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-Uda_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Uda-11">[11]</a></sup> This process requies volatile cadmium and sulfur precursors. A common example is the reaction of <a href="/wiki/Dimethylcadmium" class="mw-redirect" title="Dimethylcadmium">dimethylcadmium</a> with <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_sulfide" title="Diethyl sulfide">diethyl sulfide</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-Uda_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Uda-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>Cd(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub> + Et<sub>2</sub>S → CdS + CH<sub>3</sub>CH<sub>3</sub> + C<sub>4</sub>H<sub>10</sub></dd>
</dl>
<p>Many other methods have been reported.</p>
<p>Other methods to produce films of CdS include</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sol_gel" class="mw-redirect" title="Sol gel">Sol gel</a> techniques<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sputtering" title="Sputtering">Sputtering</a><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>Electrochemical deposition<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>Spraying with precursor cadmium salt, sulfur compound and dopant<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Screen_printing" title="Screen printing">Screen printing</a> using a slurry containing dispersed CdS<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium_sulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cadmium sulfide is soluble in (actually degraded by) acids, and this conversion has been investigated as a method of extracting the pigment from waste polymers e.g. <a href="/wiki/HDPE" class="mw-redirect" title="HDPE">HDPE</a> pipes:<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>CdS + 2 HCl → CdCl<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>S</dd>
</dl>
<p>When solutions of sulfide solutions containing dispersed CdS particles are irradiated with light hydrogen gas is generated:<sup id="cite_ref-Schiavello_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schiavello-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>H<sub>2</sub>S → H<sub>2</sub> + S ΔH<sub>f</sub> = +9.4 kcal/mol</dd>
</dl>
<p>The proposed mechanism involves the electron/hole pairs created when incident light is absorbed by the cadmium sulfide<sup id="cite_ref-Kittel_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kittel-19">[19]</a></sup> followed by these reacting with water and sulfide:<sup id="cite_ref-Schiavello_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schiavello-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>Production of an electron hole pair
<dl>
<dd>CdS&#160;+ <i>hν</i>&#160;→ e<sup>−</sup>&#160;+ hole<sup>+</sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
<dd>Reaction of electron
<dl>
<dd>2e<sup>−</sup>&#160;+ 2H<sub>2</sub>O&#160;→ H<sub>2</sub>&#160;+ 2OH<sup>−</sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
<dd>Reaction of hole
<dl>
<dd>2hole<sup>+</sup>&#160;+ S<sup>2−</sup> → S</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_physical_properties">Structure and physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium_sulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Structure and physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cadmium sulfide has, like <a href="/wiki/Zinc_sulfide" title="Zinc sulfide">zinc sulfide</a>, two crystal forms. The more stable hexagonal <a href="/wiki/Wurtzite_(crystal_structure)" class="mw-redirect" title="Wurtzite (crystal structure)">wurtzite</a> structure (found in the mineral <a href="/wiki/Greenockite" title="Greenockite">Greenockite</a>) and the cubic <a href="/wiki/Zincblende_(crystal_structure)" class="mw-redirect" title="Zincblende (crystal structure)">zinc blende structure</a> (found in the mineral <a href="/wiki/Hawleyite" title="Hawleyite">Hawleyite</a>). In both of these forms the cadmium and sulfur atoms are four coordinate.<sup id="cite_ref-Wells_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wells-20">[20]</a></sup> There is also a high pressure form with the NaCl rock salt structure.<sup id="cite_ref-Wells_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wells-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cadmium sulfide is a direct <a href="/wiki/Band_gap" title="Band gap">band gap</a> <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> (gap 2.42 <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">eV</a><sup id="cite_ref-Kittel_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kittel-19">[19]</a></sup>). The magnitude of its band gap means that it appears coloured.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiberg.26Holleman_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg.26Holleman-4">[4]</a></sup><br />
As well as this obvious property others properties result:</p>
<ul>
<li>the conductivity increases when irradiated with light<sup id="cite_ref-Kittel_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kittel-19">[19]</a></sup> (leading to uses as a <a href="/wiki/Photoresistor" title="Photoresistor">photoresistor</a>)</li>
<li>when combined with a <a href="/wiki/P-type_semiconductor" class="mw-redirect" title="P-type semiconductor">p-type semiconductor</a> it forms the core component of a <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_cell" class="mw-redirect" title="Photovoltaic cell">photovoltaic</a> (<a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar</a>) cell and a CdS/Cu<sub>2</sub>S solar cell was one of the first efficient cells to be reported (1954)<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li>when <a href="/wiki/Doped" class="mw-redirect" title="Doped">doped</a> with for example Cu<sup>+</sup> ("<a href="/wiki/Activator_(phosphor)" title="Activator (phosphor)">activator</a>") and Al<sup>3+</sup> ("coactivator") CdS luminesces under <a href="/wiki/Electron_beam" class="mw-redirect" title="Electron beam">electron beam</a> excitation (<a href="/wiki/Cathodoluminescence" title="Cathodoluminescence">cathodoluminescence</a>) and is used as <a href="/wiki/Phosphor" title="Phosphor">phosphor</a><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>both <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphs</a> are <a href="/wiki/Piezoelectric" class="mw-redirect" title="Piezoelectric">piezoelectric</a> and the hexagonal is also <a href="/wiki/Pyroelectric" class="mw-redirect" title="Pyroelectric">pyroelectric</a><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Electroluminescence" title="Electroluminescence">electroluminescence</a><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>CdS crystal can act as a <a href="/wiki/Solid_state_laser" class="mw-redirect" title="Solid state laser">solid state laser</a><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li>In thin-film form, CdS can be combined with other layers for use in certain types of solar cells.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> CdS was also one of the first semiconductor materials to be used for <a href="/wiki/Thin-film_transistor" title="Thin-film transistor">thin-film transistors</a> (TFTs).<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> However interest in compound semiconductors for TFTs largely waned after the emergence of <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_silicon" title="Amorphous silicon">amorphous silicon</a> technology in the late 1970s.</li>
</ul>
<p>Thin films of Cadmium Sulfide can be piezoelectric and have been used as transducers which can operate at frequencies in the GHz region.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium_sulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pigment">Pigment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium_sulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pigment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

Yellow cadmium sulfide- pigment</div>

<p>Synthetic <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_pigments" title="Cadmium pigments">cadmium pigments</a> based on cadmium sulfide are valued for their good thermal stability, light and weather fastness, chemical resistance and high opacity.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-7">[7]</a></sup> As a pigment, CdS is known as cadmium yellow.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiberg.26Holleman_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg.26Holleman-4">[4]</a></sup> (CI pigment yellow 37<sup id="cite_ref-Christie_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christie-30">[30]</a></sup>) About 2000 tons are produced annually as of 1982, representing about 25% of the cadmium processed commercially.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> CdS is used as pigment in plastics.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_use_in_art">Historical use in art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium_sulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Historical use in art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The general commercial availability of cadmium sulfide from the 1840s led to its adoption by artists, notably <a href="/wiki/Vincent_Van_Gogh" class="mw-redirect" title="Vincent Van Gogh">Van Gogh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Claude_Monet" title="Claude Monet">Monet</a> (in his London series and other works) and <a href="/wiki/Henri_Matisse" title="Henri Matisse">Matisse</a> (<i>Bathers by a river</i> 1916–1919).<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The presence of cadmium in paints has been used to detect forgeries in paintings alleged to have been produced prior to the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CdS-CdSe_solutions">CdS-CdSe solutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium_sulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: CdS-CdSe solutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CdS and CdSe form solid solutions. Increasing amounts of <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_selenide" title="Cadmium selenide">cadmium selenide</a>, gives pigments verging toward red, for example CI pigment orange 20 and CI pigment red 108.<sup id="cite_ref-Christie_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christie-30">[30]</a></sup><br />
Such solid solutions are components of <a href="/wiki/Photoresistor" title="Photoresistor">photoresistors</a> (light dependent resistors) sensitive to visible and near infrared light.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium_sulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cadmium sulfide is toxic, especially when inhaled as dust, and cadmium compounds general are classified as <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogenic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Problems of <a href="/wiki/Biocompatibility" title="Biocompatibility">biocompatibility</a> have been reported when CdS is used as colors in <a href="/wiki/Tattoo" title="Tattoo">tattoos</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
