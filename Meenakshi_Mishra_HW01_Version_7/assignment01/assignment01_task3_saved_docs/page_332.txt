<p>An <b>electric aircraft</b> is an aircraft powered by <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motors</a>. Electricity may be supplied by a variety of methods including; <a href="/wiki/Electric_battery" class="mw-redirect" title="Electric battery">batteries</a>, ground <a href="/wiki/Power_cable" title="Power cable">power cables</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ultracapacitors" class="mw-redirect" title="Ultracapacitors">ultracapacitors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cells</a> and even <a href="/wiki/Power_beaming" class="mw-redirect" title="Power beaming">power beaming</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Power_Beaming_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Power_Beaming-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Model_aircraft#Electric_power" title="Model aircraft">Electrically powered model aircraft</a> have been flown since the 1970s, with one unconfirmed report as early as 1957.<sup id="cite_ref-Noth_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Noth-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> They have since developed into small battery-powered <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">unmanned aerial vehicles</a> or drones, which in the twenty-first century have become widely used for many purposes.</p>
<p>Although manned flights in a tethered helicopter go back to 1917 and in airships to the previous century, the first manned free flight by an electrically powered aeroplane was not made until 1973 and most manned electric aircraft today are still only experimental demonstrators.<sup id="cite_ref-JAWA74_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAWA74-4">[4]</a></sup> Between 2015 and 2016, <a href="/wiki/Solar_Impulse_2" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar Impulse 2">Solar Impulse 2</a> completed a circumnavigation of the Earth.<sup id="cite_ref-Batrawy_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Batrawy-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All electric aircraft to date have been powered by <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motors</a> driving thrust-generating <a href="/wiki/Propeller" title="Propeller">propellers</a> or lift-generating <a href="/wiki/Rotorcraft" title="Rotorcraft">rotors</a>. Some of the propeller-driven types have been <a href="/wiki/Airship" title="Airship">airships</a>.</p>
<p>Mechanisms for storing and supplying the necessary electricity vary considerably, and each has distinct advantages and disadvantages. Mechanisms used include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Electric_battery" class="mw-redirect" title="Electric battery">Batteries</a> can retain a significant electrical charge, although their weight still limits the range achievable.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Power_cable" title="Power cable">Power cables</a> connect to a ground-based supply.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">Solar cells</a> convert sunlight directly into electricity.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ultracapacitor" class="mw-redirect" title="Ultracapacitor">Ultracapacitors</a> can store a limited amount of energy for short bursts of high-power use</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">Fuel cells</a> are similar to batteries but draw their reactants from an external source.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">Microwave</a> energy has been <a href="/wiki/Power_beaming" class="mw-redirect" title="Power beaming">beamed</a> from a ground-based source.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Batteries">Batteries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Batteries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Electric_battery" class="mw-redirect" title="Electric battery">Batteries</a> are the most common <a href="/wiki/Energy_carrier" title="Energy carrier">Energy carrier</a> component of electric aircraft, due to their relatively high capacity. Batteries were the earliest source of electricity, first powering airships in the nineteenth century. These early batteries were very heavy and it was not until the arrival of technologies such as nickel-cadmium (NiCad) rechargeable types in the second half of the twentieth century, that batteries became a practicable power source. Modern battery types include Lithium-based and a number of other less widely used technologies. Such batteries remain a popular power source today, although they still have limited life between charges and hence limited range.</p>
<p>Batteries are also often used for temporary storage of electricity generated by another source.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_cables">Power cables</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Power cables">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An electrical <a href="/wiki/Power_cable" title="Power cable">power cable</a> may be connected to a ground-based supply, such as an electric generator. At low altitudes this can avoid carrying heavy batteries and was used by the experimental <a href="/wiki/Petr%C3%B3czy-K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n-%C5%BDurovec" title="Petróczy-Kármán-Žurovec">Petróczy-Kármán-Žurovec PKZ-1</a> observation helicopter of 1917. However such a craft must remain tethered to a ground facility, and the higher it flies, the heavier the weight of cable it must lift with it.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_cells">Solar cells</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Solar cells">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">Solar cell</a> converts sunlight directly into electricity, either for direct power or temporary storage. The power output of solar cells is small, even when many are connected together, which limits their use and is also expensive. However their use of freely available sunlight makes them attractive for high-altitude, long-endurance applications.</p>
<p>For endurance flights, keeping the craft in the air all night typically requires a backup storage system, which supplies power during the hours of darkness and recharges during the day.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ultracapacitors">Ultracapacitors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ultracapacitors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Ultracapacitor" class="mw-redirect" title="Ultracapacitor">Ultracapacitor</a> can store a limited amount of energy for short bursts of high-power use, such as when taking off, but due to its relatively small storage ability it is not suitable as a primary power source. Its advantage over a small battery is the ability to charge and discharge much faster with higher peak currents.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel_cells">Fuel cells</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fuel cells">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">Fuel cell</a> reacts two fluids such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> to create electricity. Unlike a battery, the fluids are not stored in the battery but are drawn in from outside. This offers the prospect of much greater range than batteries and experimental examples have flown, but the technology has yet to reach production.</p>
<p>Two types of cell have been considered for aircraft, SPFC (polymer) and AFC (alkaline). SPFC is the lightest and simplest air-breathing technology, but is inefficient and requires precious metal electrodes. AFC provides higher output using cheaper materials, but requires either pure oxygen or additional equipment for carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) treatment.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="aircraft-related discussion requires verification (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="what are SPFC and AFC acronyms for? (January 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microwaves">Microwaves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Microwaves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Power_beaming" class="mw-redirect" title="Power beaming">Power beaming</a> of electromagnetic energy such as <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwaves</a>, like a power cable, requires a ground-based power source. However, compared to a power cable, power beaming carries much less weight penalty as altitude increases. The technology has been demonstrated on small models but awaits practical development.<sup id="cite_ref-Power_Beaming_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Power_Beaming-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pioneers">Pioneers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Pioneers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The use of electricity for aircraft propulsion was first experimented with during the development of the <a href="/wiki/Airship" title="Airship">airship</a> which took place in the latter part of the nineteenth century. In 1883, <a href="/wiki/Gaston_Tissandier" title="Gaston Tissandier">Gaston Tissandier</a> flew the first electrically-powered airship.<sup id="cite_ref-ADS1884_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ADS1884-6">[6]</a></sup> The following year, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Renard" title="Charles Renard">Charles Renard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Constantin_Krebs" title="Arthur Constantin Krebs">Arthur Krebs</a> flew <a href="/wiki/La_France_(airship)" title="La France (airship)">La France</a> with a more powerful motor.<sup id="cite_ref-ADS1884_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ADS1884-6">[6]</a></sup> Even with the lifting capacity of an airship, the heavy accumulators needed to store the electricity severely limited the speed and range of such early airships.</p>
<p>For a tethered device such as an air observation platform, it is possible to run the power up the tether. In an attempt to create a more practical solution than the clumsy balloons then in use, the Austro-Hungarian <a href="/wiki/Petr%C3%B3czy-K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n-%C5%BDurovec" title="Petróczy-Kármán-Žurovec">Petróczy-Kármán-Žurovec</a> PKZ-1 electric-powered helicopter was flown in 1917. It had a specially-designed 190 horsepower (140&#160;kW) continuous-rated electric motor made by <a href="/wiki/Austro-Daimler" title="Austro-Daimler">Austro-Daimler</a> and received its power up a cable from a ground-based DC generator. However electric motors were not yet powerful enough for such applications and the motor burned out after only a few flights.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The problems of power-to-weight ratio for electric flight were formidable even for small models. A claim made in 1909 has since been challenged, while in more modern times success has been achieved at least since 1957.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Microwaves present an alternative way of transmitting electrical power from the ground. In 1964, <a href="/wiki/William_C._Brown" title="William C. Brown">William C. Brown</a> flew a model helicopter that received all of the power needed for flight from a microwave beam.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NiCad_batteries_and_solar_power">NiCad batteries and solar power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: NiCad batteries and solar power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Success in a full-sized aeroplane would not be achieved until <a href="/wiki/Nickel-cadmium_battery" class="mw-redirect" title="Nickel-cadmium battery">Nickel-cadmium</a> (NiCad) batteries were developed, having a much higher storage-to-weight ratio than older technologies. In 1973, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fred_Militky&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fred Militky (page does not exist)">Fred Militky</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heino_Brditschka&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Heino Brditschka (page does not exist)">Heino Brditschka</a> converted a <a href="/wiki/Brditschka_HB-3" title="Brditschka HB-3">Brditschka HB-3</a> <a href="/wiki/Motor_glider" title="Motor glider">motor glider</a> to an electric aircraft, the <a href="/wiki/Militky_MB-E1" class="mw-redirect" title="Militky MB-E1">Militky MB-E1</a>. It flew for just 14 minutes to become the first manned electric aircraft to fly under its own power.<sup id="cite_ref-JAWA74_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAWA74-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Developed almost in parallel with NiCad technology, solar cells were also slowly becoming a practicable power source. Following a successful model test in 1974, the world’s first official flight in a solar-powered, man-carrying aircraft took place on April 29, 1979. The <a href="/wiki/Mauro_Solar_Riser" title="Mauro Solar Riser">Mauro Solar Riser</a> used photovoltaic cells to deliver 350 watts at 30 volts. These charged a small battery, which in turn powered the motor. The battery alone was capable of powering the motor for 3 to 5 minutes, following a 1.5-hour charge, enabling it to reach a gliding altitude.<sup id="cite_ref-EAAMuseum_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EAAMuseum-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under the direction of Freddie To, an architect and member of the <a href="/wiki/Kremer_prize" title="Kremer prize">Kremer prize</a> committee, the <a href="/wiki/Solar-Powered_Aircraft_Developments_Solar_One" title="Solar-Powered Aircraft Developments Solar One">Solar One</a> was designed by David Williams and produced by Solar-Powered Aircraft Developments. A motor-glider type aircraft originally built as a pedal-powered airplane to attempt the Channel crossing, the airplane proved too heavy to be successfully powered by human power and was then converted to solar power,<sup id="cite_ref-astroflight_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-astroflight-12">[12]</a></sup> using an electric motor driven by batteries that were charged before flight by a solar cell array on the wing.<sup id="cite_ref-propdesigner_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-propdesigner-13">[13]</a></sup> The maiden flight of Solar One took place at Lasham Airfield; Hampshire on June 13, 1979.<sup id="cite_ref-flightglobal_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-flightglobal-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human-powered_hybrids">Human-powered hybrids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Human-powered hybrids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following successful <a href="/wiki/Human-powered_flight" class="mw-redirect" title="Human-powered flight">human-powered flight</a>, a relaunched <a href="/wiki/Kremer_prize" title="Kremer prize">Kremer prize</a> allowed the crew to store energy before takeoff.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In the 1980s several such designs stored electricity generated by pedalling, including the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> <a href="/wiki/MIT_Monarch" class="mw-redirect" title="MIT Monarch">Monarch</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Aerovironment" class="mw-redirect" title="Aerovironment">Aerovironment</a> Bionic Bat.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Advancing_technology">Advancing technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Advancing technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The human piloted Solair 1, developed by Günther Rochelt, flew in 1983 with notably improved performance.<sup id="cite_ref-Noth_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Noth-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Khammas_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Khammas-17">[17]</a></sup> It employed 2499 wing-mounted solar cells.<sup id="cite_ref-Noth_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Noth-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The German solar-powered aircraft "Icaré II" was designed and built by the institute of aircraft design (Institut für Flugzeugbau) of the University of Stuttgart in 1996. The leader of the project and often pilot of the aircraft is Rudolf Voit-Nitschmann the head of the institute. The design won the Berblinger prize in 1996, the EAA Special Achievement Award in Oshkosh, the Golden Daidalos Medal of the German Aeroclub and the OSTIV-Prize in France in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-IcareII_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IcareII-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/NASA_Pathfinder" title="NASA Pathfinder">NASA's Pathfinder, Pathfinder Plus</a>, <a href="/wiki/NASA_Centurion" title="NASA Centurion">Centurion</a>, and <a href="/wiki/NASA_Helios" class="mw-redirect" title="NASA Helios">Helios</a> were a series of solar and fuel cell system–powered <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">unmanned aerial vehicles</a> (UAVs) developed by <a href="/wiki/AeroVironment" title="AeroVironment">AeroVironment</a>, Inc. from 1983 until 2003 under <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Research_Aircraft_and_Sensor_Technology" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental Research Aircraft and Sensor Technology">Environmental Research Aircraft and Sensor Technology</a> program.<sup id="cite_ref-heliosfact_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heliosfact-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-goebel12_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goebel12-20">[20]</a></sup> On September 11, 1995, Pathfinder set an unofficial altitude record for solar-powered aircraft of 50,000 feet (15,000&#160;m) during a 12-hour flight from <a href="/wiki/Dryden_Flight_Research_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Dryden Flight Research Center">NASA Dryden</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-heliosfact_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heliosfact-19">[19]</a></sup> After further modifications, the aircraft was moved to the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Missile_Range_Facility" title="Pacific Missile Range Facility">Pacific Missile Range Facility</a> (PMRF) on the Hawaiian island of <a href="/wiki/Kauai" title="Kauai">Kauai</a>. On July 7, 1997, Pathfinder raised the altitude record for solar–powered aircraft to 71,530 feet (21,800&#160;m), which was also the record for propeller–driven aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-heliosfact_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heliosfact-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 6, 1998, Pathfinder Plus raised the national altitude record to 80,201 feet (24,445&#160;m) for solar-powered and propeller-driven aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-heliosfact_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heliosfact-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 14, 2001 Helios set an altitude record of 96,863 feet (29,524&#160;m) – the record for FAI class U (Experimental / New Technologies), and FAI class U-1.d (Remotely controlled UAV: Mass 500&#160;kg to less than 2,500&#160;kg) as well as the altitude record for propeller–driven aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> On June 26, 2003, the Helios Prototype broke up and fell into the Pacific Ocean off Hawaii after the aircraft encountered turbulence, ending the program.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coming_of_age">Coming of age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Coming of age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first commercially available production electric aircraft, the <a href="/wiki/Alisport_Silent_Club" title="Alisport Silent Club">Alisport Silent Club</a> self-launching sailplane, flew in 1997. It is optionally driven by a 13&#160;kW (17&#160;hp) DC electric motor running on 40&#160;kg (88&#160;lb) of batteries that store 1.4 kWh of energy.<sup id="cite_ref-SilentElectric_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SilentElectric-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="claim of 'first' contradicts the next paragraph (December 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The first certificate of airworthiness for an electric powered aircraft was granted to the <a href="/wiki/Lange_Antares" title="Lange Antares">Lange Antares</a> 20E in 2003. An electric, self-launching 20-meter <a href="/wiki/Sailplane" class="mw-redirect" title="Sailplane">sailplane</a> with a 42-kilowatt DC/DC brushless motor and <a href="/wiki/Lithium-ion_battery" title="Lithium-ion battery">lithium-ion batteries</a>, it can climb up to 3,000 meters with fully charged cells.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The first flight was in 2003. In 2011 the aircraft won the 2011 Berblinger competition.<sup id="cite_ref-2011_Berblinger_competition_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2011_Berblinger_competition-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="claim of 'first' contradicts the previous paragraph (December 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 2005, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Cocconi" class="mw-redirect" title="Alan Cocconi">Alan Cocconi</a> of <a href="/wiki/AC_Propulsion" title="AC Propulsion">AC Propulsion</a> flew, with the assistance of several other pilots, an unmanned airplane named "SoLong" for 48 hours non-stop, propelled entirely by solar energy. This was the first such around-the-clock flight, on energy stored in the batteries mounted on the plane.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2007, the non-profit <a href="/wiki/CAFE_Foundation" title="CAFE Foundation">CAFE Foundation</a> held the first Electric Aircraft Symposium in San Francisco.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a>-led FCD (Fuel Cell Demonstrator) project uses a <a href="/wiki/Diamond_HK-36_Super_Dimona" class="mw-redirect" title="Diamond HK-36 Super Dimona">Diamond HK-36 Super Dimona</a> <a href="/wiki/Motor_glider" title="Motor glider">motor glider</a> as a research test bed for a hydrogen <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cell</a> powered light airplane.<sup id="cite_ref-AvWeb04Apr08_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AvWeb04Apr08-29">[29]</a></sup> Successful flights took place in February and March 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-AvWeb04Apr08_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AvWeb04Apr08-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Apr08Times_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Apr08Times-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/QinetiQ_Zephyr" class="mw-redirect" title="QinetiQ Zephyr">QinetiQ Zephyr</a> is a lightweight solar-powered <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">unmanned aerial vehicle</a> (UAV). As of 23 July 2010 it holds the endurance record for an unmanned aerial vehicle of over 2 weeks (336 hours).<sup id="cite_ref-BBC20100723_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC20100723-31">[31]</a></sup> It is of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_fiber-reinforced_polymer" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon fiber-reinforced polymer">carbon fiber-reinforced polymer</a> construction, the 2010 version weighing 50&#160;kg (110&#160;lb)<sup id="cite_ref-BBC20100717_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC20100717-32">[32]</a></sup> (the 2008 version weighed 30&#160;kg (66&#160;lb)) with a <a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">span</a> of 22.5 metres<sup id="cite_ref-BBC20100717_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC20100717-32">[32]</a></sup> (the 2008 version had 18 metres (59 feet)). During the day it uses sunlight to charge lithium-sulphur batteries, which power the aircraft at night.<sup id="cite_ref-QinetiQ_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QinetiQ-33">[33]</a></sup> In July 2010 a Zephyr made a world record UAV endurance flight of 336 hours, 22 minutes and 8 seconds (more than two weeks) and also set an altitude record of 70,742 feet (21,562&#160;m) for FAI class U-1.c (Remotely controlled UAV: Weight 50&#160;kg to less than 500&#160;kg).<sup id="cite_ref-BBC200808_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC200808-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AvWeb29Dec10_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AvWeb29Dec10-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/NASA_Puffin" title="NASA Puffin">NASA Puffin</a> was a concept, proposed in 2010, for an electric-powered, <a href="/wiki/Vertical_takeoff_and_landing" class="mw-redirect" title="Vertical takeoff and landing">vertical takeoff and landing</a> (VTOL), <a href="/wiki/Personal_air_vehicle" title="Personal air vehicle">personal air vehicle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ps20100119_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ps20100119-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first the NASA <a href="/wiki/Green_Flight_Challenge" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Flight Challenge">Green Flight Challenge</a> took place in 2011 and was won by a <a href="/wiki/Pipistrel_Taurus" title="Pipistrel Taurus">Pipistrel Taurus G4</a> on 3 October 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-AVweb27Jul11_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AVweb27Jul11-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AVweb13Aug11_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AVweb13Aug11-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AVweb03Sep11_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AVweb03Sep11-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The European Commission has financed many low <a href="/wiki/Technology_readiness_level" title="Technology readiness level">TRL</a> projects for innovative electric or hybrid propulsion aircraft. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=ENFICA-FC&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="ENFICA-FC (page does not exist)">ENFICA-FC</a> is a project of the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a>, to study and demonstrate an all-electric aircraft with fuel-cells as the main or auxiliary power system. During the three-year project, a fuel-cell based power system was designed and flown in a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rapid_200FC&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rapid 200FC (page does not exist)">Rapid 200FC</a> <a href="/wiki/Ultralight_aircraft" class="mw-redirect" title="Ultralight aircraft">ultralight aircraft</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ENFICA_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ENFICA-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_Impulse_2_flies_round_the_world">Solar Impulse 2 flies round the world</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Solar Impulse 2 flies round the world">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Solar Impulse 2 is powered by four electric motors. Energy from solar cells on the wings and horizontal stabilizer is stored in lithium polymer batteries and used to drive propellers.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012 the first <a href="/wiki/Solar_Impulse" title="Solar Impulse">Solar Impulse</a> made the first intercontinental flight by a solar plane, flying from <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a> in Europe, to <a href="/wiki/Rabat" title="Rabat">Rabat</a> in Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Completed in 2014, Solar Impulse 2 carried more solar cells and more powerful engines, among other improvements. In March 2015, the plane took off on the first stage of a planned round-the-world trip, flying Eastwards from <a href="/wiki/Abu_Dhabi" title="Abu Dhabi">Abu Dhabi</a> in the United Arab Emirates.<sup id="cite_ref-Batrawy_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Batrawy-5">[5]</a></sup> Due to battery damage, the craft halted at Hawaii until April 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> On 23 June 2016 the plane reached Sevilla, Spain on 23rd.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> It has since returned to Abu Dhabi, completing its circumnavigation of the world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_rotorcraft">Electric rotorcraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Electric rotorcraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the Austro-Hungarian <a href="/wiki/Petr%C3%B3czy-K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n-%C5%BDurovec" title="Petróczy-Kármán-Žurovec">Petróczy-Kármán-Žurovec</a> team flew an experimental tethered military observation helicopter in 1917, the use of electric power for rotor-borne flight was not exploited until modern times. Lightweight technologies have enabled the development of small, cheap radio-controlled UAVs, more commonly called drones, for recreational and other purposes. In particular the electric <a href="/wiki/Quadcopter" title="Quadcopter">quadcopter</a> has been manufactured in large numbers.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Solution_F/Chretien_Helicopter" title="Solution F/Chretien Helicopter">Solution F/Chretien Helicopter</a> the world's first man-carrying, free-flying electric helicopter was developed by <a href="/wiki/Pascal_Chretien" title="Pascal Chretien">Pascal Chretien</a>. The concept was taken from the conceptual <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_design" title="Computer-aided design">computer-aided design</a> model on September 10, 2010 to the first testing at 30% power on March 1, 2011—less than six months. The aircraft first flew on August 12, 2011. All development was conducted in Venelles, France.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>The electric Sikorsky S-300 of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_Firefly&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Project Firefly (page does not exist)">Project Firefly</a> was a project to flight test an electric rotorcraft, but the project was put on hold due to battery limitations.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The world's first large-scale all-electric <a href="/wiki/Tilt-rotor" class="mw-redirect" title="Tilt-rotor">tilt-rotor</a> was the <a href="/wiki/AgustaWestland_Project_Zero" title="AgustaWestland Project Zero">AgustaWestland Project Zero</a> <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">unmanned aerial vehicle</a> technology demonstrator, which performed unmanned tethered fights on ground power in June 2011, less than six months after the company gave the official go-ahead.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2016, <a href="/wiki/Martine_Rothblatt" title="Martine Rothblatt">Martine Rothblatt</a> and Tier1 Engineering successfully tested an electric-powered helicopter. The five minute flight reached an altitude of 400 feet with a peak speed of 80 knots. The <a href="/wiki/Robinson_R44" title="Robinson R44">Robinson R44</a> helicopter was modified with two three-phase permanent magnet synchronous motors, weighing 100 lb, plus 11 <a href="/wiki/Brammo,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Brammo, Inc.">Brammo</a> <a href="/wiki/Lithium_polymer_batteries" class="mw-redirect" title="Lithium polymer batteries">Lithium polymer batteries</a> weighing 1100 lb and a digital cockpit display.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> It later flew for 20 minutes in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
