<p><b>Blood pressure</b> (<b>BP</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> of circulating <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> on the walls of <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">blood vessels</a>. When used without further specification, "blood pressure" usually refers to the <a href="/wiki/Arterial" class="mw-redirect" title="Arterial">arterial</a> pressure in the <a href="/wiki/Systemic_circulation" class="mw-redirect" title="Systemic circulation">systemic circulation</a>. Blood pressure is usually expressed in terms of the <a href="/wiki/Systole" title="Systole">systolic</a> (maximum during one heart beat) pressure over <a href="/wiki/Diastolic" class="mw-redirect" title="Diastolic">diastolic</a> (minimum in between two heart beats) pressure and is measured in millimeters of mercury (<a href="/wiki/Millimeter_of_mercury" title="Millimeter of mercury">mmHg</a>), above the surrounding atmospheric pressure (considered to be zero for convenience).</p>
<p>It is one of the <a href="/wiki/Vital_signs" title="Vital signs">vital signs</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_rate" title="Respiratory rate">respiratory rate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heart_rate" title="Heart rate">heart rate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_saturation" title="Oxygen saturation">oxygen saturation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Body_temperature" class="mw-redirect" title="Body temperature">body temperature</a>. Normal resting blood pressure in an adult is approximately 120&#160;mmHg systolic, and 80&#160;mmHg diastolic, abbreviated "120/80&#160;mmHg".</p>
<p>Traditionally, blood pressure is measured non-invasively using a mercury manometer because these gauges are dependent upon only gravity, thus inherently more accurate than alternative types of pressure gauges. In research, the values obtained by an experienced knowledgeable physician using a mercury manometer and <a href="/wiki/Stethoscope" title="Stethoscope">stethoscope</a> listening for the <a href="/wiki/Korotkoff_sounds" title="Korotkoff sounds">Korotkoff sounds</a> are typically within 10 mmHg of the pressures measured via higher sophistication internal measurements of central aortic pressures at heart level. Other methods, which have become more dominant (for cost, time, convenience and concerns about potential mercury toxicity issues) are inherently less accurate.</p>
<p>Blood pressure is determined, moment by moment, by the balance between <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_output" title="Cardiac output">heart output</a> versus <a href="/wiki/Total_peripheral_resistance" class="mw-redirect" title="Total peripheral resistance">total peripheral resistance</a> and varies depending on situation, emotional state, activity, and relative health/disease states. It is <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis#The_arterial_blood_pressure_homeostat" title="Homeostasis">regulated</a> by the brain via both the <a href="/wiki/Nervous_system" title="Nervous system">nervous</a> and <a href="/wiki/Endocrine_system" title="Endocrine system">endocrine</a> systems.</p>
<p>Blood pressure that is low <a href="/wiki/Pathologically" class="mw-redirect" title="Pathologically">due to a disease state</a> is called <a href="/wiki/Hypotension" title="Hypotension">hypotension</a>, and pressure that is consistently high is <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a>. Both have many causes which can range from mild to severe. Both may be of sudden onset or of long duration. Long term hypertension is a risk factor for many diseases, including <a href="/wiki/Heart_disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Heart disease">heart disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kidney_failure" title="Kidney failure">kidney failure</a>. Long term hypertension is more common than long term hypotension in Western countries. Long term hypertension often goes undetected because of infrequent monitoring and the absence of symptoms.</p>

<p>The table presented here shows the classification of blood pressure adopted by the American Heart Association for adults who are 18 years and older.<sup id="cite_ref-Heartorg_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heartorg-1">[1]</a></sup> It assumes the values are a result of averaging resting blood pressure readings measured at two or more visits to the doctor.<sup id="cite_ref-Chobanian2003_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chobanian2003-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NHLBI2008_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHLBI2008-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/UK" class="mw-redirect" title="UK">UK</a>, clinic blood pressures are usually categorized into three groups; low (90/60 or lower), normal (between 90/60 and 139/89), and high (140/90 or higher).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Blood pressure fluctuates from minute to minute and normally shows a circadian rhythm over a 24-hour period, with highest readings in the early morning and evenings and lowest readings at night.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Loss of the normal fall in blood pressure at night is associated with a greater future risk of cardiovascular disease and there is evidence that night-time blood pressure is a stronger predictor of cardiovascular events than day-time blood pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-HansenLi2010_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HansenLi2010-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Various factors, such as age and sex, influence a person's blood pressure and variations in it. In children, the normal ranges are lower than for adults and depend on height.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Reference blood pressure values have been developed for children in different countries, based on the distribution of blood pressure in children of these countries.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> As adults age, systolic pressure tends to rise and diastolic tends to fall.<sup id="cite_ref-Pickering2005p145a_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pickering2005p145a-13">[13]</a></sup> In the elderly, systolic blood pressure tends to be above the normal adult range,<sup id="cite_ref-Pickering2005p144_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pickering2005p144-14">[14]</a></sup> thought to be largely because of reduced flexibility of the arteries. Also, an individual's blood pressure varies with exercise, emotional reactions, sleep, digestion and time of day (<a href="/wiki/Circadian_rhythm" title="Circadian rhythm">circadian rhythm</a>).</p>
<p>Differences between left and right arm blood pressure measurements tend to be random and average to nearly zero if enough measurements are taken. However, in a small percentage of cases there is a consistent difference greater than 10&#160;mmHg which may need further investigation, e.g. for <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">obstructive arterial disease</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The risk of cardiovascular disease increases progressively above 115/75&#160;mmHg.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In the past, <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a> was only diagnosed if secondary signs of high arterial pressure were present, along with a prolonged high systolic pressure reading over several visits. Regarding hypotension, in practice blood pressure is considered too low only if noticeable <a href="/wiki/Hypotension#Signs_and_symptoms" title="Hypotension">symptoms</a> are present.<sup id="cite_ref-Mayo2009causes_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mayo2009causes-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Clinical trials demonstrate that people who maintain arterial pressures at the low end of these pressure ranges have much better long term cardiovascular health. The principal medical debate concerns the aggressiveness and relative value of methods used to lower pressures into this range for those who do not maintain such pressure on their own. Elevations, more commonly seen in older people, though often considered normal, are associated with increased <a href="/wiki/Morbidity" class="mw-redirect" title="Morbidity">morbidity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">mortality</a>.</p>

<p>The up and down fluctuation of the <a href="/wiki/Arterial" class="mw-redirect" title="Arterial">arterial</a> pressure results from the pulsatile nature of the <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_output" title="Cardiac output">cardiac output</a>, i.e. the heartbeat. Pulse pressure is determined by the interaction of the <a href="/wiki/Stroke_volume" title="Stroke volume">stroke volume</a> of the heart, the compliance (ability to expand) of the arterial system—largely attributable to the <a href="/wiki/Aorta" title="Aorta">aorta</a> and large elastic arteries—and the <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">resistance</a> to flow in the <a href="/wiki/Arterial_tree" title="Arterial tree">arterial tree</a>. By expanding under pressure, the aorta absorbs some of the force of the blood surge from the heart during a heartbeat. In this way, the pulse pressure is reduced from what it would be if the aorta were not compliant.<sup id="cite_ref-KlabundePulse2007_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KlabundePulse2007-20">[20]</a></sup> The loss of arterial compliance that occurs with aging explains the elevated pulse pressures found in elderly patients.</p>

<p>Blood pressure generally refers to the arterial pressure in the <a href="/wiki/Systemic_circulation" class="mw-redirect" title="Systemic circulation">systemic circulation</a>. However, measurement of pressures in the venous system and the <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_circulation" title="Pulmonary circulation">pulmonary vessels</a> plays an important role in <a href="/wiki/Intensive_care_medicine" title="Intensive care medicine">intensive care medicine</a> but requires invasive measurement of pressure using a <a href="/wiki/Catheter" title="Catheter">catheter</a>.</p>
<p>Venous pressure is the vascular pressure in a <a href="/wiki/Vein" title="Vein">vein</a> or in the <a href="/wiki/Atrium_(anatomy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Atrium (anatomy)">atria of the heart</a>. It is much less than arterial pressure, with common values of 5&#160;mmHg in the <a href="/wiki/Right_atrium" class="mw-redirect" title="Right atrium">right atrium</a> and 8&#160;mmHg in the left atrium.</p>
<p>Variants of venous pressure include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Central_venous_pressure" title="Central venous pressure">Central venous pressure</a>, which is a good approximation of right atrial pressure,<sup id="cite_ref-urlCentral_Venous_Catheter_Physiology_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urlCentral_Venous_Catheter_Physiology-22">[22]</a></sup> which is a major determinant of right ventricular end diastolic volume. (However, there can be exceptions in some cases.)<sup id="cite_ref-pmid12533747_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid12533747-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Jugular_venous_pressure" title="Jugular venous pressure">jugular venous pressure</a> (JVP) is the indirectly observed pressure over the venous system. It can be useful in the differentiation of different forms of <a href="/wiki/Heart_disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Heart disease">heart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lung_disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Lung disease">lung disease</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Portal_venous_pressure" title="Portal venous pressure">portal venous pressure</a> is the blood pressure in the <a href="/wiki/Portal_vein" title="Portal vein">portal vein</a>. It is normally 5–10&#160;mmHg<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pulmonary_pressure">Pulmonary pressure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pulmonary pressure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_artery_pressure" class="mw-redirect" title="Pulmonary artery pressure">Pulmonary artery pressure</a></div>
<p>Normally, the pressure in the <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_artery" title="Pulmonary artery">pulmonary artery</a> is about 15&#160;mmHg at rest.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Increased blood pressure in the capillaries of the lung cause <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_hypertension" title="Pulmonary hypertension">pulmonary hypertension</a>, leading to interstitial <a href="/wiki/Edema" title="Edema">edema</a> if the pressure increases to above 20&#160;mmHg, and to <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_edema" title="Pulmonary edema">pulmonary edema</a> at pressures above 25&#160;mmHg.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disorders">Disorders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Disorders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Disorders of blood pressure control include: <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">high blood pressure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hypotension" title="Hypotension">low blood pressure</a>, and blood pressure that shows excessive or maladaptive fluctuation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High">High</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: High">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">Hypertension</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Main_complications_of_persistent_high_blood_pressure.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Main_complications_of_persistent_high_blood_pressure.svg/300px-Main_complications_of_persistent_high_blood_pressure.svg.png" width="300" height="277" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Main_complications_of_persistent_high_blood_pressure.svg/450px-Main_complications_of_persistent_high_blood_pressure.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Main_complications_of_persistent_high_blood_pressure.svg/600px-Main_complications_of_persistent_high_blood_pressure.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1164" data-file-height="1076" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Main_complications_of_persistent_high_blood_pressure.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Overview of main complications of persistent high blood pressure.</div>
</div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Arterial_hypertension" class="mw-redirect" title="Arterial hypertension">Arterial hypertension</a> can be an indicator of other problems and may have long-term adverse effects. Sometimes it can be an acute problem, for example <a href="/wiki/Hypertensive_emergency" title="Hypertensive emergency">hypertensive emergency</a>.</p>
<p>Levels of arterial pressure put mechanical stress on the arterial walls. Higher pressures increase heart workload and progression of unhealthy tissue growth (<a href="/wiki/Atheroma" title="Atheroma">atheroma</a>) that develops within the walls of arteries. The higher the pressure, the more stress that is present and the more <a href="/wiki/Atheroma" title="Atheroma">atheroma</a> tend to progress and the <a href="/wiki/Myocardium" class="mw-redirect" title="Myocardium">heart muscle</a> tends to thicken, enlarge and become weaker over time.</p>
<p>Persistent <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a> is one of the risk factors for <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">strokes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">heart attacks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heart_failure" title="Heart failure">heart failure</a> and arterial aneurysms, and is the leading cause of <a href="/wiki/Chronic_kidney_failure" class="mw-redirect" title="Chronic kidney failure">chronic kidney failure</a>. Even moderate elevation of arterial pressure leads to shortened <a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">life expectancy</a>. At severely high pressures, mean arterial pressures 50% or more above average, a person can expect to live no more than a few years unless appropriately treated.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the past, most attention was paid to <a href="/wiki/Diastolic" class="mw-redirect" title="Diastolic">diastolic</a> pressure; but nowadays it is recognised that both high <a href="/wiki/Systole_(medicine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Systole (medicine)">systolic</a> pressure and high <a href="/wiki/Pulse_pressure" title="Pulse pressure">pulse pressure</a> (the numerical difference between systolic and diastolic pressures) are also risk factors. In some cases, it appears that a decrease in excessive diastolic pressure can actually increase risk, due probably to the increased difference between systolic and diastolic pressures (see the article on <a href="/wiki/Pulse_pressure" title="Pulse pressure">pulse pressure</a>). If systolic blood pressure is elevated (&gt;140&#160;mmHg) with a normal diastolic blood pressure (&lt;90&#160;mmHg), it is called "isolated systolic hypertension" and may present a health concern.<sup id="cite_ref-urlIsolated_systolic_hypertension:_A_health_concern.3F_.E2.80.93_MayoClinic.com_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urlIsolated_systolic_hypertension:_A_health_concern.3F_.E2.80.93_MayoClinic.com-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-urlThe_Cleveland_Clinic_Center_for_Continuing_Education_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urlThe_Cleveland_Clinic_Center_for_Continuing_Education-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>For those with <a href="/wiki/Heart_valve" title="Heart valve">heart valve</a> regurgitation, a change in its severity may be associated with a change in diastolic pressure. In a study of people with heart valve regurgitation that compared measurements 2 weeks apart for each person, there was an increased severity of <a href="/wiki/Aortic_insufficiency" title="Aortic insufficiency">aortic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mitral_regurgitation" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitral regurgitation">mitral regurgitation</a> when diastolic blood pressure increased, whereas when diastolic blood pressure decreased, there was a decreased severity.<sup id="cite_ref-Gottdiener2002_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gottdiener2002-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Low">Low</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Low">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hypotension" title="Hypotension">Hypotension</a></div>
<p>Blood pressure that is too low is known as <a href="/wiki/Hypotension" title="Hypotension">hypotension</a>. Hypotension is a medical concern if it causes signs or symptoms, such as dizziness, fainting, or in extreme cases, <a href="/wiki/Shock_(circulatory)" title="Shock (circulatory)">shock</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NHLBI2008_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHLBI2008-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>When arterial pressure and blood <a href="/wiki/Rate_of_fluid_flow" class="mw-redirect" title="Rate of fluid flow">flow</a> decrease beyond a certain point, the <a href="/wiki/Perfusion" title="Perfusion">perfusion</a> of the brain becomes critically decreased (i.e., the blood supply is not sufficient), causing lightheadedness, dizziness, weakness or fainting.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sometimes the arterial pressure drops significantly when a patient stands up from sitting. This is known as <a href="/wiki/Orthostatic_hypotension" title="Orthostatic hypotension">orthostatic hypotension</a> (postural hypotension); gravity reduces the rate of blood return from the body veins below the heart back to the heart, thus reducing stroke volume and cardiac output.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>When people are healthy, the veins below their heart quickly constrict and the heart rate increases to minimize and compensate for the gravity effect. This is carried out involuntarily by the autonomic nervous system. The system usually requires a few seconds to fully adjust and if the compensations are too slow or inadequate, the individual will suffer reduced blood flow to the brain, dizziness and potential blackout. Increases in G-loading, such as routinely experienced by aerobatic or combat pilots '<a href="/wiki/G-force" title="G-force">pulling Gs</a>', greatly increases this effect. Repositioning the body perpendicular to gravity largely eliminates the problem.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Other causes of low arterial pressure include:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sepsis" title="Sepsis">Sepsis</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hemorrhage" class="mw-redirect" title="Hemorrhage">Hemorrhage</a> – blood loss</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Toxins" class="mw-redirect" title="Toxins">Toxins</a> including toxic doses of blood pressure medicine</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">Hormonal</a> abnormalities, such as <a href="/wiki/Addison%27s_disease" title="Addison's disease">Addison's disease</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eating_disorder" title="Eating disorder">Eating disorders</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Anorexia_nervosa" title="Anorexia nervosa">anorexia nervosa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bulimia" class="mw-redirect" title="Bulimia">bulimia</a></li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Shock_(circulatory)" title="Shock (circulatory)">Shock</a> is a complex condition which leads to critically decreased <a href="/wiki/Perfusion" title="Perfusion">perfusion</a>. The usual mechanisms are loss of blood volume, pooling of blood within the veins reducing adequate return to the heart and/or low effective heart pumping. Low arterial pressure, especially low pulse pressure, is a sign of shock and contributes to and reflects decreased perfusion.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>If there is a significant difference in the pressure from one arm to the other, that may indicate a narrowing (for example, due to <a href="/wiki/Aortic_coarctation" class="mw-redirect" title="Aortic coarctation">aortic coarctation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aortic_dissection" title="Aortic dissection">aortic dissection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thrombosis" title="Thrombosis">thrombosis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Embolism" title="Embolism">embolism</a>) of an <a href="/wiki/Artery" title="Artery">artery</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fluctuating_blood_pressure">Fluctuating blood pressure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Fluctuating blood pressure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Normal fluctuation in blood pressure is adaptive and necessary. Fluctuations in pressure that are significantly greater than the norm are associated with greater <a href="/wiki/White_matter_hyperintensity" class="mw-redirect" title="White matter hyperintensity">white matter hyperintensity</a>, a finding consistent with reduced local cerebral blood flow<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and a heightened risk of <a href="/wiki/Cerebrovascular" class="mw-redirect" title="Cerebrovascular">cerebrovascular</a> disease.<sup id="cite_ref-ncbi.nlm.nih.gov_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncbi.nlm.nih.gov-33">[33]</a></sup> Within both high and low blood pressure groups, a greater degree of fluctuation was found to correlate with an increase in cerebrovascular disease compared to those with less variability, suggesting the consideration of the clinical management of blood pressure fluctuations, even among <a href="/wiki/Normotensive" class="mw-redirect" title="Normotensive">normotensive</a> older adults.<sup id="cite_ref-ncbi.nlm.nih.gov_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncbi.nlm.nih.gov-33">[33]</a></sup> Older individuals and those who had received blood pressure medications were more likely to exhibit larger fluctuations in pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-ncbi.nlm.nih.gov_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncbi.nlm.nih.gov-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physiology">Physiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Physiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Systolevs_Diastole.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Systolevs_Diastole.png/220px-Systolevs_Diastole.png" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Systolevs_Diastole.png/330px-Systolevs_Diastole.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Systolevs_Diastole.png/440px-Systolevs_Diastole.png 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="889" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Systolevs_Diastole.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Systole on the left and diastole on the right</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During each heartbeat, blood pressure varies between a maximum (systolic) and a minimum (diastolic) pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The blood pressure in the circulation is principally due to the pumping action of the heart.<sup id="cite_ref-Caro_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Caro-35">[35]</a></sup> Differences in mean blood pressure are responsible for blood flow from one location to another in the circulation. The rate of mean blood flow depends on both blood pressure and the resistance to flow presented by the blood vessels. Mean blood pressure decreases as the <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">circulating blood</a> moves away from the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a> through arteries and <a href="/wiki/Capillaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Capillaries">capillaries</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscous</a> losses of energy. Mean blood pressure drops over the whole circulation, although most of the fall occurs along the small arteries and <a href="/wiki/Arterioles" class="mw-redirect" title="Arterioles">arterioles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Klabunde2005p93-4_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Klabunde2005p93-4-36">[36]</a></sup> Gravity affects blood pressure via <a href="/wiki/Fluid_statics" class="mw-redirect" title="Fluid statics">hydrostatic</a> forces (e.g., during standing), and valves in veins, <a href="/wiki/Breathing" title="Breathing">breathing</a>, and pumping from contraction of skeletal muscles also influence blood pressure in veins.<sup id="cite_ref-Caro_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Caro-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hemodynamics">Hemodynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Hemodynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hemodynamics" title="Hemodynamics">Hemodynamics</a></div>
<p>Most influences on blood pressure can be understood in terms of their effect on cardiac output and resistance (the determinants of mean arterial pressure).<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some factors are:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Blood_volume" title="Blood volume">Blood volume</a>: the greater the blood volume, the higher the cardiac output. There is some relationship between dietary salt intake and increased blood volume, potentially resulting in higher arterial pressure, though this varies with the individual and is highly dependent on autonomic nervous system response and the <a href="/wiki/Renin%E2%80%93angiotensin_system" title="Renin–angiotensin system">renin–angiotensin system</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
<li>Cardiac output: the pumping action of the heart is ultimately responsible for blood pressure. Increases or decreases in cardiac output can result in increases or decreases respectively in blood pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
<li>Systemic vascular resistance: the higher the resistance to blood flow, the higher the arterial pressure upstream needs to be to maintain flow. In simple terms, resistance is related to vessel radius by the <a href="/wiki/Hagen%E2%80%93Poiseuille_equation" title="Hagen–Poiseuille equation">Hagen-Poiseuille's equation</a> (resistance∝1/radius<sup>4</sup>, so the smaller the radius, the very much higher the resistance). Other physical factors that affect resistance include: vessel length (the longer the vessel, the higher the resistance), blood viscosity (the higher the viscosity, the higher the resistance)<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> and the presence of an arterial <a href="/wiki/Stenosis" title="Stenosis">stenosis</a> (a narrow stenosis increases resistance to flow, however this increase in resistance rarely if ever increases systemic blood pressure, it decreases downstream flow).<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Substances called <a href="/wiki/Vasoconstrictor" class="mw-redirect" title="Vasoconstrictor">vasoconstrictors</a> can reduce the calibre of blood vessels, thereby increasing blood pressure. <a href="/wiki/Vasodilator" class="mw-redirect" title="Vasodilator">Vasodilators</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Nitroglycerin" title="Nitroglycerin">nitroglycerin</a>) increase the calibre of blood vessels, thereby decreasing arterial pressure.</li>
</ul>
<p>In practice, each individual's autonomic nervous system and other systems regulating blood pressure respond to and regulate all these factors so that, although the above issues are important, they rarely act in isolation and the actual arterial pressure response of a given individual can vary widely in the short and long-term.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation">Regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">Homeostasis</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Endogenous" class="mw-redirect" title="Endogenous">endogenous</a> regulation of arterial pressure is not completely understood, but the following mechanisms of regulating arterial pressure have been well-characterized:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Baroreceptor_reflex" class="mw-redirect" title="Baroreceptor reflex">Baroreceptor reflex</a>: <a href="/wiki/Baroreceptor" title="Baroreceptor">Baroreceptors</a> in the <a href="/wiki/High_pressure_receptor_zones" title="High pressure receptor zones">high pressure receptor zones</a> detect changes in arterial pressure. These baroreceptors send signals ultimately to the <a href="/wiki/Medulla_oblongata" title="Medulla oblongata">medulla of the brain stem</a>, specifically to the <a href="/wiki/Rostral_ventrolateral_medulla" title="Rostral ventrolateral medulla">rostral ventrolateral medulla</a> (RVLM). The medulla, by way of the <a href="/wiki/Autonomic_nervous_system" title="Autonomic nervous system">autonomic nervous system</a>, adjusts the mean arterial pressure by altering both the force and speed of the heart's contractions, as well as the systemic vascular resistance. The most important arterial baroreceptors are located in the left and right <a href="/wiki/Carotid_sinus" title="Carotid sinus">carotid sinuses</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Aortic_arch" title="Aortic arch">aortic arch</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-KlabundeArtBar2007_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KlabundeArtBar2007-44">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Renin-angiotensin_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Renin-angiotensin system">Renin-angiotensin system</a> (RAS): This system is generally known for its long-term adjustment of arterial pressure. This system allows the <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidney</a> to compensate for loss in <a href="/wiki/Blood_volume" title="Blood volume">blood volume</a> or drops in arterial pressure by activating an endogenous <a href="/wiki/Vasoconstrictor" class="mw-redirect" title="Vasoconstrictor">vasoconstrictor</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Angiotensin_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Angiotensin II">angiotensin II</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aldosterone" title="Aldosterone">Aldosterone</a> release: This <a href="/wiki/Steroid_hormone" title="Steroid hormone">steroid hormone</a> is released from the <a href="/wiki/Adrenal_cortex" title="Adrenal cortex">adrenal cortex</a> in response to angiotensin II or high serum <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> levels. Aldosterone stimulates <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> retention and potassium excretion by the kidneys. Since sodium is the main ion that determines the amount of fluid in the blood vessels by <a href="/wiki/Osmosis" title="Osmosis">osmosis</a>, aldosterone will increase fluid retention, and indirectly, arterial pressure.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Baroreceptor" title="Baroreceptor">Baroreceptors</a> in <a href="/wiki/Low_pressure_receptor_zones" title="Low pressure receptor zones">low pressure receptor zones</a> (mainly in the <a href="/wiki/Venae_cavae" title="Venae cavae">venae cavae</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_veins" class="mw-redirect" title="Pulmonary veins">pulmonary veins</a>, and in the <a href="/wiki/Atrium_(heart)" title="Atrium (heart)">atria</a>) result in feedback by regulating the secretion of <a href="/wiki/Antidiuretic_hormone" class="mw-redirect" title="Antidiuretic hormone">antidiuretic hormone</a> (ADH/Vasopressin), <a href="/wiki/Renin" title="Renin">renin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aldosterone" title="Aldosterone">aldosterone</a>. The resultant increase in <a href="/wiki/Blood_volume" title="Blood volume">blood volume</a> results in an increased <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_output" title="Cardiac output">cardiac output</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Frank%E2%80%93Starling_law_of_the_heart" class="mw-redirect" title="Frank–Starling law of the heart">Frank–Starling law of the heart</a>, in turn increasing arterial blood pressure.</li>
</ul>
<p>These different mechanisms are not necessarily independent of each other, as indicated by the link between the RAS and aldosterone release. When blood pressure falls many physiological cascades commence in order to return the blood pressure to a more appropriate level.</p>
<ol>
<li>The blood pressure fall is detected by a decrease in blood flow and thus a decrease in <a href="/wiki/Glomerular_filtration_rate" class="mw-redirect" title="Glomerular filtration rate">Glomerular filtration rate</a> (GFR).</li>
<li>Decrease in GFR is sensed as a decrease in Na<sup>+</sup> levels by the <a href="/wiki/Macula_densa" title="Macula densa">macula densa</a>.</li>
<li>The macula densa cause an increase in Na<sup>+</sup> reabsorption, which causes water to follow in via <a href="/wiki/Osmosis" title="Osmosis">osmosis</a> and leads to an ultimate increase in <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">plasma</a> volume. Further, the macula densa releases adenosine which causes constriction of the afferent arterioles.</li>
<li>At the same time, the <a href="/wiki/Juxtaglomerular_cells" class="mw-redirect" title="Juxtaglomerular cells">juxtaglomerular cells</a> sense the decrease in blood pressure and release <a href="/wiki/Renin" title="Renin">renin</a>.</li>
<li>Renin converts <a href="/wiki/Angiotensinogen" class="mw-redirect" title="Angiotensinogen">angiotensinogen</a> (inactive form) to <a href="/wiki/Angiotensin_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Angiotensin I">angiotensin I</a> (active form).</li>
<li>Angiotensin I flows in the bloodstream until it reaches the capillaries of the lungs where <a href="/wiki/Angiotensin_converting_enzyme" class="mw-redirect" title="Angiotensin converting enzyme">angiotensin converting enzyme</a> (ACE) acts on it to convert it into <a href="/wiki/Angiotensin_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Angiotensin II">angiotensin II</a>.</li>
<li>Angiotensin II is a vasoconstrictor which will increase bloodflow to the heart and subsequently the preload, ultimately increasing the <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_output" title="Cardiac output">cardiac output</a>.</li>
<li>Angiotensin II also causes an increase in the release of <a href="/wiki/Aldosterone" title="Aldosterone">aldosterone</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Adrenal_gland" title="Adrenal gland">adrenal glands</a>.</li>
<li>Aldosterone further increases the Na<sup>+</sup> and H<sub>2</sub>O reabsorption in the <a href="/wiki/Distal_convoluted_tubule" title="Distal convoluted tubule">distal convoluted tubule</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Nephron" title="Nephron">nephron</a>.</li>
</ol>
<p>Currently, the RAS is targeted pharmacologically by <a href="/wiki/ACE_inhibitor" title="ACE inhibitor">ACE inhibitors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Angiotensin_II_receptor_antagonist" title="Angiotensin II receptor antagonist">angiotensin II receptor antagonists</a>. The aldosterone system is directly targeted by <a href="/wiki/Spironolactone" title="Spironolactone">spironolactone</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Aldosterone_antagonist" class="mw-redirect" title="Aldosterone antagonist">aldosterone antagonist</a>. The fluid retention may be targeted by <a href="/wiki/Diuretic" title="Diuretic">diuretics</a>; the antihypertensive effect of diuretics is due to its effect on blood volume. Generally, the baroreceptor reflex is not targeted in <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a> because if blocked, individuals may suffer from <a href="/wiki/Orthostatic_hypotension" title="Orthostatic hypotension">orthostatic hypotension</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fainting" class="mw-redirect" title="Fainting">fainting</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement">Measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Blood_Pressure_-_Take_Another_Person.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Blood_Pressure_-_Take_Another_Person.png/220px-Blood_Pressure_-_Take_Another_Person.png" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Blood_Pressure_-_Take_Another_Person.png/330px-Blood_Pressure_-_Take_Another_Person.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Blood_Pressure_-_Take_Another_Person.png/440px-Blood_Pressure_-_Take_Another_Person.png 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Blood_Pressure_-_Take_Another_Person.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Taking another persons blood pressure with a sphygmomanometer</div>
</div>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure_measurement" title="Blood pressure measurement">Blood pressure measurement</a></div>
<p>Arterial pressure is most commonly measured via a <a href="/wiki/Sphygmomanometer" title="Sphygmomanometer">sphygmomanometer</a>, which historically used the height of a column of mercury to reflect the circulating pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-Booth1977_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Booth1977-45">[45]</a></sup> Blood pressure values are generally reported in <a href="/wiki/Millimetre_of_mercury" class="mw-redirect" title="Millimetre of mercury">millimetres of mercury</a> (mmHg), though aneroid and electronic devices do not contain <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>.</p>
<p>For each heartbeat, blood pressure varies between systolic and diastolic pressures. Systolic pressure is peak pressure in the arteries, which occurs near the end of the <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_cycle" title="Cardiac cycle">cardiac cycle</a> when the <a href="/wiki/Ventricle_(heart)" title="Ventricle (heart)">ventricles</a> are contracting. Diastolic pressure is minimum pressure in the arteries, which occurs near the beginning of the cardiac cycle when the ventricles are filled with blood. An example of normal measured values for a resting, healthy adult human is 120&#160;mmHg <a href="/wiki/Systole_(medicine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Systole (medicine)">systolic</a> and 80&#160;mmHg <a href="/wiki/Diastolic" class="mw-redirect" title="Diastolic">diastolic</a> (written as 120/80&#160;mmHg, and spoken as "one-twenty over eighty").</p>
<p>Systolic and diastolic arterial blood pressures are not static but undergo natural variations from one heartbeat to another and throughout the day (in a <a href="/wiki/Circadian" class="mw-redirect" title="Circadian">circadian</a> rhythm). They also change in response to <a href="/wiki/Stress_(medicine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stress (medicine)">stress</a>, nutritional factors, <a href="/wiki/Medication" class="mw-redirect" title="Medication">drugs</a>, disease, exercise, and <a href="/wiki/Orthostatic_hypotension" title="Orthostatic hypotension">momentarily from standing up</a>. Sometimes the variations are large. <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">Hypertension</a> refers to arterial pressure being abnormally high, as opposed to <a href="/wiki/Hypotension" title="Hypotension">hypotension</a>, when it is abnormally low. Along with <a href="/wiki/Body_temperature" class="mw-redirect" title="Body temperature">body temperature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_rate" title="Respiratory rate">respiratory rate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pulse_rate" class="mw-redirect" title="Pulse rate">pulse rate</a>, blood pressure is one of the four main vital signs routinely monitored by medical professionals and healthcare providers.<sup id="cite_ref-OHSU_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OHSU-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Measuring pressure <a href="/wiki/Invasive_blood_pressure" class="mw-redirect" title="Invasive blood pressure">invasively</a>, by penetrating the arterial wall to take the measurement, is much less common and usually restricted to a hospital setting.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fetal_blood_pressure">Fetal blood pressure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Fetal blood pressure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Fetal_circulation#Blood_pressure" title="Fetal circulation">Fetal circulation §&#160;Blood pressure</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a>, it is the fetal heart and not the mother's heart that builds up the fetal blood pressure to drive blood through the fetal circulation. The blood pressure in the fetal aorta is approximately 30&#160;mmHg at 20 weeks of gestation, and increases to approximately 45&#160;mmHg at 40 weeks of gestation.<sup id="cite_ref-Struijk_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Struijk-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>The average blood pressure for full-term infants:<sup id="cite_ref-SharonSmithMurray_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SharonSmithMurray-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Systolic 65–95&#160;mmHg</li>
<li>Diastolic 30–60&#160;mmHg</li>
</ul>
