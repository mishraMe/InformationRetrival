<p><b>Blythe</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="/θ/ 'th' in 'thigh'">θ</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a city in <a href="/wiki/Riverside_County,_California" title="Riverside County, California">Riverside County</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, United States, in the <a href="/wiki/Palo_Verde_Valley" title="Palo Verde Valley">Palo Verde Valley</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Lower_Colorado_River_Valley" title="Lower Colorado River Valley">Lower Colorado River Valley</a> region, an agricultural area and part of the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Desert" title="Colorado Desert">Colorado Desert</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_River" title="Colorado River">Colorado River</a>. Blythe was named after <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_H._Blythe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Thomas H. Blythe (page does not exist)">Thomas H. Blythe</a>, a San Francisco financier, who established primary water rights to the Colorado River in the region in 1877. The city was incorporated on July 21, 1916. The population was 20,817 at the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 census</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blythe,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early or mid-1870s, William Calloway (known as Oliver Calloway in some sources), an engineer and a former captain of the <a href="/wiki/1st_California_Infantry_Regiment" title="1st California Infantry Regiment">1st California Infantry Regiment</a>, explored an area across the Colorado River from <a href="/wiki/Ehrenberg,_Arizona" title="Ehrenberg, Arizona">Ehrenberg, Arizona</a> and found its potential for development. Calloway made preliminary surveys and filed land claims under the <a href="/wiki/Swamp_Land_Act_of_1850" title="Swamp Land Act of 1850">Swamp Land Act of 1850</a>. He interested the wealthier Thomas Henry Blythe (real last name Williams), who was born in <a href="/wiki/Mold,_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Mold, Wales">Mold, Wales</a>, to undertake development and settlement of an "empire" located next to the Colorado. On July 17, 1877, Blythe filed his first claim for Colorado River water on what was to become the "Blythe Intake".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-San_Diego_History_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-San_Diego_History-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Blythe appointed another man named George Irish as manager to assist Calloway in building an irrigation system. Calloway died in a <a href="/wiki/Chemehuevi" title="Chemehuevi">Chemehuevi</a> attack in March 28, 1880, and was replaced by C.C. Miller, the father of <a href="/wiki/Frank_Augustus_Miller" title="Frank Augustus Miller">Frank Augustus Miller</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Desert_Magazine_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Desert_Magazine-12">[12]</a></sup> Thomas Blythe died on April 4, 1883; his only revisit to the valley was in November 1882.<sup id="cite_ref-Desert_Magazine_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Desert_Magazine-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PVID_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PVID-13">[13]</a></sup> After his death, the work in the valley halted and Blythe's estate subsequently went into litigation between his illegitimate daughter Florence and other claimants. The <a href="/wiki/California_Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="California Supreme Court">California Supreme Court</a> up to 1895, and the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Supreme Court">U.S. Supreme Court</a> towards the 1900s, ruled that Florence was the holder of the estate, after several years of preceding rulings in favor of her and appeals against her in these courts.<sup id="cite_ref-San_Diego_History_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-San_Diego_History-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Desert_Magazine_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Desert_Magazine-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Boessenecker1998_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boessenecker1998-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Frank Murphy and Ed Williams, who were involved on the cattle industry in southeastern Arizona, came to the area in 1904 and were convinced it was well-suited for cattle and farming. With the Hobson brothers from <a href="/wiki/Ventura_County,_California" title="Ventura County, California">Ventura County, California</a>, they bought Blythe's estate and formed the Palo Verde Land and Water Company.<sup id="cite_ref-PVID_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PVID-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kleinsorge1941_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kleinsorge1941-15">[15]</a></sup> During 1911-12, W.F. Holt, who helped develop nearby <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Valley" title="Imperial Valley">Imperial Valley</a>, was the company's general manager.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Myrick2001_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Myrick2001-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 8, 1916, the California Southern Railroad reached Blythe from the desert station of <a href="/wiki/Rice,_CA" class="mw-redirect" title="Rice, CA">Rice, California</a>, then known as Blythe Junction. It was later renamed to honor G.W. Rice, an engineer and superintendent of the railroad. The dramatic growth in the valley following this event attracted national attention. Production totals increased annually from virtually nothing to near $8,000,000 in few years, primarily from cotton and cotton seed shipped to the ports.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The lower cotton prices in 1920 ended this prosperous time. The <a href="/wiki/Atchison,_Topeka,_and_Santa_Fe_Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Atchison, Topeka, and Santa Fe Railway">Atchison, Topeka, and Santa Fe Railway</a> began leasing the line in 1921 and acquired it in the end of 1942.<sup id="cite_ref-Myrick2001_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Myrick2001-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Robertson1986_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robertson1986-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first automobile bridge over the Colorado River between Blythe and Ehrenberg was constructed in 1928 to replace a <a href="/wiki/Cable_ferry" title="Cable ferry">cable ferry</a> service.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The bridge's successor was built in the early 1960s and it was expanded to four lanes and a pedestrian walkway in early 1974.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1972, <a href="/wiki/Interstate_10_in_California" title="Interstate 10 in California">Interstate 10</a> was built through the city, replacing <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_60#California" title="U.S. Route 60">US 60</a> and the previously decommissioned <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_70#History" title="U.S. Route 70">US 70</a> in Hobsonway as the main thoroughfare.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_climate">Geography and climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blythe,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography and climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Blythe is located near the <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>/<a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> border in the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Desert" title="Colorado Desert">Colorado Desert</a> section of the <a href="/wiki/Sonoran_Desert" title="Sonoran Desert">Sonoran Desert</a>, at the junction of <a href="/wiki/Interstate_10_in_California" title="Interstate 10 in California">Interstate 10</a> and <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_95_in_California" title="U.S. Route 95 in California">US 95</a>. According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the city has a total area of 27.0 square miles (70&#160;km<sup>2</sup>), of which 26.2 square miles (68&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) is land and 0.8 square miles (2.1&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) (2.90%) is water.</p>
<p>Nearby communities include <a href="/wiki/Lost_Lake_Resort,_California" title="Lost Lake Resort, California">Lost Lake</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vidal,_California" title="Vidal, California">Vidal</a> to the north, <a href="/wiki/Ripley,_California" title="Ripley, California">Ripley</a> to the south, <a href="/wiki/Desert_Center,_California" title="Desert Center, California">Desert Center</a> to the west, and <a href="/wiki/Ehrenberg,_Arizona" title="Ehrenberg, Arizona">Ehrenberg, Arizona</a>, to the east. Major cities in the region include <a href="/wiki/Yuma,_Arizona" title="Yuma, Arizona">Yuma</a> (85 miles), <a href="/wiki/Indio,_California" title="Indio, California">Indio</a> (95 miles), <a href="/wiki/Phoenix,_Arizona" title="Phoenix, Arizona">Phoenix</a> (150 miles), <a href="/wiki/San_Bernardino,_California" title="San Bernardino, California">San Bernardino</a> (170 miles), <a href="/wiki/Riverside,_California" title="Riverside, California">Riverside</a> (172 miles), and <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas,_Nevada" class="mw-redirect" title="Las Vegas, Nevada">Las Vegas</a> (200 miles). Blythe is within 4 hours via car of 10% of the United States’ population.</p>
<p>Blythe has a <a href="/wiki/Desert_climate" title="Desert climate">desert climate</a> with very hot summers and mild winters. There are an average of 178.4 days with highs of 90&#160;°F (32&#160;°C) or higher. There are an average of 18.9 days with lows of 32&#160;°F (0&#160;°C) or lower. Until 2016, the record high temperature was 122&#160;°F (50&#160;°C) on July 7, 1920, and June 24, 1929. But on June 20, 2016, that long-standing record was shattered when Blythe reached 124&#160;°F (51&#160;°C). The record low temperature was 5&#160;°F (−15&#160;°C) on January 6, 1913. There are an average of 16 days with measurable precipitation. The wettest year was 1951 with 8.71 inches (22.1&#160;cm) and the driest year was 1956 with .18 inches (0.46&#160;cm). The most rainfall in one month was 6.00 inches (15.2&#160;cm) in August 1951, which included the 24-hour record rainfall of 3.06 inches (7.8&#160;cm) on August 27.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the airport, there are an average of 176.0 days with highs of 90&#160;°F (32&#160;°C) or higher. There are an average of 5.4 days with lows of 32&#160;°F (0&#160;°C) or lower. The record high temperature was 124&#160;°F (51&#160;°C) on June 20, 2016. The record low temperature was 20&#160;°F (−7&#160;°C) on January 8, 1971. There an average of 17 days with measurable precipitation. The wettest year was 1992 with 9.16 inches (23.3&#160;cm) and the driest year was 1953 with .59 inches (1.5&#160;cm). The most rainfall in one month was 5.92 inches (15.0&#160;cm) in August 1951, which included the 24-hour record rainfall of 3.00 inches (7.6&#160;cm) on August 26.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>

<p>Also notable is the geological history of Blythe – despite California's reputation for <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a>, according to geologists Blythe has not had an earthquake centered in the city for over 500,000 years.<sup id="cite_ref-Palo_Verde_Valley_District_Library_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Palo_Verde_Valley_District_Library-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_features">Local features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blythe,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Local features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tourism is a major component of the local economy. Blythe is a stopover city with full services for travelers between any of the nearby regions, in particular the major cities of <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phoenix,_Arizona" title="Phoenix, Arizona">Phoenix</a>, since it is approximately midway between those two metropolitan areas. The winter months bring visitors avoiding the colder climates of the north, when the population of the area within <span style="white-space:nowrap">50&#160;miles&#160;(80&#160;km)</span> of Blythe has been known to exceed 500,000.<sup id="cite_ref-Palo_Verde_Valley_District_Library_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Palo_Verde_Valley_District_Library-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Con-Way" class="mw-redirect" title="Con-Way">Con-Way</a> freight has a large facility here. It is used mainly at night for cross dock operations, as there is seldom a need to make deliveries here.</p>
<p>Blythe also contains 24 churches, one library, two newspapers <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://paloverdevalleytimes.com/">Palo Verde Valley Times</a> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://thedesertinde.com">The Desert Independent</a>, two museums, two radio stations, three banks, a three-screen movie theater (now closed), one funeral home and an <span style="white-space:nowrap">18,500&#160;sq&#160;ft&#160;(1,720&#160;m<sup>2</sup>)</span>. recreation center. The area is popular with campers and hikers and has six parks, seven campgrounds, seven <a href="/wiki/R.V._park" class="mw-redirect" title="R.V. park">R.V. parks</a>, 3 boat ramps onto the Colorado River, and an 18-hole public golf course.</p>
<p>Blythe hosts the <b><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.blythebluegrass.com/">Blythe Blue Grass Festival</a></b> annually the third weekend of January.</p>
<p>Dove hunting is popular in Blythe. The hunting season starts at every September 1.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010">2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blythe,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2010">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 United States Census</a><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> reported that Blythe had a population of 20,817. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 771.8 people per square mile (298.0/km<sup>2</sup>). The racial makeup of Blythe was 12,396 (59.5%) <a href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a> (28.3% Non-Hispanic White),<sup id="cite_ref-quickfacts.census.gov_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-quickfacts.census.gov-30">[30]</a></sup> 3,126 (15.0%) <a href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 243 (1.2%) <a href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 319 (1.5%) <a href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 32 (0.2%) <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 4,045 (19.4%) from <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 656 (3.2%) from two or more races. <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 11,068 persons (53.2%).</p>
<p>The Census reported that 12,972 people (62.3% of the population) lived in households, 82 (0.4%) lived in non-institutionalized group quarters, and 7,763 (37.3%) were institutionalized.</p>
<p>There were 4,513 households, out of which 1,972 (43.7%) had children under the age of 18 living in them, 1,995 (44.2%) were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">opposite-sex married couples</a> living together, 855 (18.9%) had a female householder with no husband present, 344 (7.6%) had a male householder with no wife present. There were 396 (8.8%) <a href="/wiki/POSSLQ" title="POSSLQ">unmarried opposite-sex partnerships</a>, and 24 (0.5%) <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_partnerships" class="mw-redirect" title="Same-sex partnerships">same-sex married couples or partnerships</a>. 1,071 households (23.7%) were made up of individuals and 367 (8.1%) had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.87. There were 3,194 <a href="/wiki/Family_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Family (U.S. Census)">families</a> (70.8% of all households); the average family size was 3.41.</p>
<p>The population was spread out with 4,157 people (20.0%) under the age of 18, 1,770 people (8.5%) aged 18 to 24, 7,332 people (35.2%) aged 25 to 44, 5,764 people (27.7%) aged 45 to 64, and 1,794 people (8.6%) who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 38.0 years. For every 100 females there were 218.1 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 268.1 males.</p>
<p>There were 5,473 housing units at an average density of 202.9 per square mile (78.3/km<sup>2</sup>), of which 2,358 (52.2%) were owner-occupied, and 2,155 (47.8%) were occupied by renters. The homeowner vacancy rate was 4.0%; the rental vacancy rate was 10.3%. 6,913 people (33.2% of the population) lived in owner-occupied housing units and 6,059 people (29.1%) lived in rental housing units.</p>
<p>According to the 2010 United States Census, Blythe had a median household income of $48,327, with 16.1% of the population living below the federal poverty line.<sup id="cite_ref-quickfacts.census.gov_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-quickfacts.census.gov-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000">2000</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blythe,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2000">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup id="cite_ref-GR2_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GR2-31">[31]</a></sup> of 2000, there were 12,155 people, 4,103 households, and 2,974 families residing in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 501.5 people per square mile (193.6/km<sup>2</sup>). There were 4,891 housing units at an average density of 201.8 per square mile (77.9/km<sup>2</sup>). The racial makeup of the city was 55.4% <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">White</a>, 8.3% <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">Black</a> or <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">African American</a>, 1.4% <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">Native American</a>, 1.4% <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">Asian</a>, 0.2% <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 28.8% from <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 4.5% from two or more races. 45.8% of the population were <a href="/wiki/Hispanics_in_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Hispanics in the United States">Hispanic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">Latino</a> of any race.</p>
<p>There were 4,103 households out of which 41.4% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 50.0% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 16.6% had a female householder with no husband present, and 27.5% were non-families. 22.9% of all households were made up of individuals and 7.1% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.91 and the average family size was 3.45.</p>
<p>In the city, the population was spread out with 33.9% under the age of 18, 8.7% from 18 to 24, 28.4% from 25 to 44, 19.0% from 45 to 64, and 9.9% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 31 years. For every 100 females there were 99.2 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 97.0 males.</p>
<p>The median <a href="/wiki/Income" title="Income">income</a> for a household in the city was $35,324, and the median income for a family was $40,783. Males had a median income of $32,342 versus $26,671 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the city was $14,424. About 19.0% of families and 20.9% of the population were below the <a href="/wiki/Poverty_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 24.9% of those under age 18 and 21.6% of those age 65 or over.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blythe,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/California_State_Legislature" title="California State Legislature">California State Legislature</a>, Blythe is in the 40th <a href="/wiki/California_State_Senate" title="California State Senate">Senate</a> District represented by <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democrat</a> <a href="/wiki/Denise_Moreno_Ducheny" title="Denise Moreno Ducheny">Denise Moreno Ducheny</a> and, in <a href="/wiki/California%27s_56th_State_Assembly_district" title="California's 56th State Assembly district">the 56th Assembly District</a>, represented by <a href="/wiki/California_Democratic_Party" title="California Democratic Party">Democrat</a> <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Garcia_(politician)" title="Eduardo Garcia (politician)">Eduardo Garcia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a>, Blythe is in <a href="/wiki/California%27s_36th_congressional_district" title="California's 36th congressional district">California's 36th congressional district</a>, represented by <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democrat</a> <a href="/wiki/Raul_Ruiz_(politician)" title="Raul Ruiz (politician)">Raul Ruiz</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blythe,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Blythe_Heat" title="Blythe Heat">Blythe Heat</a> is a winter professional minor league baseball team of the <a href="/wiki/Arizona_Winter_League" title="Arizona Winter League">Arizona Winter League</a>, and also a member of the <a href="/wiki/Arizona_Summer_League" title="Arizona Summer League">Arizona Summer League</a>. They play on Alexander Field in Todd Park facing the Palo Verde College. Some games of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Havasu_Heat&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lake Havasu Heat (page does not exist)">Lake Havasu Heat</a> (folded in 2009) of the semi-pro <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Southwest_Baseball_League" title="Pacific Southwest Baseball League">Pacific Southwest Baseball League</a> played in Blythe during the summer months.</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blythe,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Blythe has its own police department and volunteer fire department. The <a href="/wiki/Riverside_County_Sheriff%27s_Department" title="Riverside County Sheriff's Department">Riverside County Sheriff's Department</a> also has a regional station in Blythe.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blythe,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Blythe area public elementary and secondary schools comprise the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.pvusd-bly.k12.ca.us/home/">Palo Verde Unified School District</a>, which contains 3 elementary schools and 1 high school, and continuation/adult education school. Palo Verde Community College District is part of the <a href="/wiki/California_Community_College" class="mw-redirect" title="California Community College">California Community College</a> system and includes <a href="/wiki/Palo_Verde_Community_College" class="mw-redirect" title="Palo Verde Community College">Palo Verde Community College</a>, a campus in Blythe, a center in Needles and an extension for Adult Basic education and non-credit classes located on Spring Street in downtown Blythe. As of 2012, there is one private non-profit school, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.desertlearningcenter.org">The Desert Learning Center</a>, for grades K-8.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blythe,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Interstate_10_in_California" title="Interstate 10 in California">Interstate 10</a> crosses Blythe on an east-west direction. <a href="/wiki/California_State_Route_78" title="California State Route 78">State Route 78</a>'s northern terminus is west of Blythe. <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_95_(California)" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Route 95 (California)">U.S. Route 95</a> crosses Blythe in the eastern side. Lovekin Boulevard and Midland Road serves the ghost town of <a href="/wiki/Midland,_CA" class="mw-redirect" title="Midland, CA">Midland</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Blythe_Airport" title="Blythe Airport">Blythe Airport</a> (BLH) is just west of the town on <a href="/wiki/Interstate_10_in_California" title="Interstate 10 in California">Interstate 10</a> and has a 6,500-foot (2,000&#160;m) runway.</p>
<p>Blythe was served in rail by the <a href="/wiki/Arizona_and_California_Railroad" title="Arizona and California Railroad">Arizona and California Railroad</a> but currently has no rail service since an embargo in late 2007 and abandonment in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Blythe is served by Palo Verde Valley Transit Agency.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Greyhound_Lines" title="Greyhound Lines">Greyhound</a> stops in Blythe.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Healthcare">Healthcare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blythe,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Healthcare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Health facilities in Blythe include <a href="/wiki/Palo_Verde_Hospital" title="Palo Verde Hospital">Palo Verde Hospital</a>, a General Acute Care <a href="/wiki/Hospital" title="Hospital">Hospital</a> with 55 total beds and 24-hour standby emergency services, 23 physicians/surgeons, 2 dentists, 2 optometrists, 1 chiropractor, and 1 podiatrist.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
