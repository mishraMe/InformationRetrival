			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Solar luminosity</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

<p>The <b>solar luminosity</b>, <i>L</i><sub>☉</sub>, is a unit of <a href="/wiki/Radiant_flux" title="Radiant flux">radiant flux</a> (<a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">power</a> emitted in the form of <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a>) conventionally used by <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomers</a> to measure the <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminosity</a> of <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>. It is defined in terms of the Sun's output. One solar luminosity is <span class="nowrap"><span style="display:none" class="sortkey">7026382800000000000♠</span>3.828<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>26</sup>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">W</a></span>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This does not include the <a href="/wiki/Solar_neutrino" title="Solar neutrino">solar neutrino</a> luminosity, which would add 0.023&#160;<i>L</i><sub>☉</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The Sun is a weakly <a href="/wiki/Variable_star" title="Variable star">variable star</a>, and its luminosity therefore <a href="/wiki/Solar_variation" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar variation">fluctuates</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The major fluctuation is the eleven-year <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">solar cycle</a> (sunspot cycle) that causes a periodic variation of about ±0.1%. Other variations over the last 200–300 years are thought to be much smaller than this.<sup id="cite_ref-Noed_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Noed-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Determination">Determination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_luminosity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Determination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Solar luminosity is related to <a href="/wiki/Irradiance" title="Irradiance">solar irradiance</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Solar_constant" title="Solar constant">solar constant</a>). Solar irradiance is responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Orbital_forcing" title="Orbital forcing">orbital forcing</a> that causes the <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">Milankovitch cycles</a>, which determine Earthly glacial cycles. The mean irradiance at the top of the Earth's atmosphere is sometimes known as the <a href="/wiki/Solar_constant" title="Solar constant">solar constant</a>, <i>I</i><sub>☉</sub>. Irradiance is defined as power per unit area, so the solar luminosity (total power emitted by the Sun) is the irradiance received at the Earth (solar constant) multiplied by the area of the sphere whose radius is the mean distance between the Earth and the Sun:</p>

<p>where <i>A</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">unit distance</a> (the value of the <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">astronomical unit</a> in <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metres</a>) and <i>k</i> is a constant (whose value is very close to one) that reflects the fact that the mean distance from the Earth to the Sun is not exactly one <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">astronomical unit</a>.</p>
