<p>The <b>solar mass</b> (<b><i>M</i><sub>☉</sub></b>) is a standard <a href="/wiki/Mass#Units_of_mass" title="Mass">unit of mass</a> in <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, equal to approximately 1.99 × 10<sup>30</sup> <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kilograms</a>. It is used to indicate the masses of other <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Star_cluster" title="Star cluster">clusters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nebula" title="Nebula">nebulae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a>. It is equal to the mass of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, about two <a href="/wiki/Names_of_large_numbers" title="Names of large numbers">nonillion</a> (two quintillion in the <a href="/wiki/Long_scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Long scale">long scale</a>) <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kilograms</a>:</p>
<dl>
<dd><span class="texhtml"><i>M</i><sub>☉</sub> = <span class="nowrap"><span style="display:none" class="sortkey">7030198855000000000♠</span>(1.988<span style="margin-left:.25em;">55</span><span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.000<span style="margin-left:.25em;">25</span>)<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>30</sup>&#160;kg</span></span><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>The above mass is about <span class="nowrap"><span style="display:none" class="sortkey">7005332946000000000♠</span>332<span style="margin-left:.25em;">946</span></span> times the <a href="/wiki/Earth_mass" title="Earth mass">mass of Earth</a> (<i>M</i><sub>⊕</sub>), or <span class="nowrap"><span style="display:none" class="sortkey">7003104800000000000♠</span>1048</span> times the mass of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> (<i>M</i><sub>J</sub>).</p>
<p>Because Earth follows an <a href="/wiki/Elliptical_orbit" class="mw-redirect" title="Elliptical orbit">elliptical orbit</a> around the Sun, its solar mass can be computed from the equation for the <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">orbital period</a> of a small body orbiting a central mass.<sup id="cite_ref-harwit1998_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harwit1998-3">[3]</a></sup> Based upon the length of the year, the distance from Earth to the Sun (an <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">astronomical unit</a> or AU), and the <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_constant" title="Gravitational constant">gravitational constant</a> (<span class="texhtml"><i>G</i></span>), the mass of the Sun is given by:</p>

<p>The value of the gravitational constant was first derived from measurements that were made by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cavendish" title="Henry Cavendish">Henry Cavendish</a> in 1798 with a <a href="/wiki/Torsion_balance" class="mw-redirect" title="Torsion balance">torsion balance</a>. The value he obtained differs by only 1% from the modern value.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Parallax#Diurnal_parallax" title="Parallax">diurnal parallax</a> of the Sun was accurately measured during the transits of Venus in 1761 and 1769,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> yielding a value of <span class="nowrap"><span style="display:none" class="sortkey">6995436332312998583♠</span>9″</span> (9 <a href="/wiki/Minute_and_second_of_arc" title="Minute and second of arc">arcseconds</a>, compared to the present 1976 value of <span class="nowrap"><span style="display:none" class="sortkey">6995426352326410207♠</span>8.794<span style="margin-left:.25em;">148</span>″</span>). From the value of the diurnal parallax, one can determine the distance to the Sun from the geometry of Earth.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first person to estimate the mass of the Sun was <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a>. In his work <i><a href="/wiki/Philosophiae_Naturalis_Principia_Mathematica" class="mw-redirect" title="Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica">Principia</a></i>, he estimated that the ratio of the mass of Earth to the Sun was about 1/28&#160;700. Later he determined that his value was based upon a faulty value for the solar parallax, which he had used to estimate the distance to the Sun (1 AU). He corrected his estimated ratio to 1/169&#160;282 in the third edition of the <i>Principia</i>. The current value for the solar parallax is smaller still, yielding an estimated mass ratio of 1/332&#160;946.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a unit of measurement, the solar mass came into use before the AU and the gravitational constant were precisely measured. This is because the relative mass of another planet in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> or the combined mass of two <a href="/wiki/Binary_star#Use_in_astrophysics" title="Binary star">binary stars</a> can be calculated in units of Solar mass directly from the orbital radius and orbital period of the planet or stars using Kepler's third law, provided that orbital radius is measured in astronomical units and orbital period is measured in years.</p>
<p>The mass of the Sun has decreased since the time it formed. This has occurred through two processes in nearly equal amounts. First, in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_core" title="Solar core">Sun's core</a>, hydrogen is converted into helium through <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a>, in particular the <a href="/wiki/Proton%E2%80%93proton_chain_reaction" title="Proton–proton chain reaction">p–p chain</a>, and this reaction converts some mass into energy in the form of <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma ray</a> photons. Most of this energy eventually <a href="/wiki/Solar_luminosity" title="Solar luminosity">radiates away</a> from the Sun. Second, high-energy protons and electrons in the atmosphere of the Sun are ejected directly into outer space as a <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.</p>
<p>The original mass of the Sun at the time it reached the <a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">main sequence</a> remains uncertain. The early Sun had much higher mass-loss rates than at present, and it may have lost anywhere from 1–7% of its natal mass over the course of its main-sequence lifetime.<sup id="cite_ref-apj583_2_1024_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-apj583_2_1024-8">[8]</a></sup> The Sun gains a very small amount of mass through the impact of <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comets</a>. However, as the Sun already contains 99.86% of the Solar System's total mass, these impacts cannot offset the mass lost by radiation and ejection.</p>
