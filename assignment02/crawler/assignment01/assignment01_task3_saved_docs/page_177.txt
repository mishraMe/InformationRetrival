<p>A <b>hot water storage tank</b> (also called a <b>hot water tank</b>, <b>thermal storage tank</b>, <b>hot water thermal storage unit</b>, <b>heat storage tank</b> and <b>hot water cylinder</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Water_tank" title="Water tank">water tank</a> used for storing hot water for space heating or domestic use.</p>
<p>Water is a convenient heat storage medium because it has a high <a href="/wiki/Specific_heat_capacity" class="mw-redirect" title="Specific heat capacity">specific heat capacity</a>. This means, compared to other substances, it can store more heat per unit of weight (and volume).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Water is non-toxic and in many countries available free of charge or at low cost.</p>
<p>An efficiently insulated tank can retain stored heat for days, reducing fuel costs.<sup id="cite_ref-sysdhw_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sysdhw-1">[1]</a></sup> Hot water tanks may have a built-in gas or oil burner system, electric <a href="/wiki/Immersion_heater" class="mw-redirect" title="Immersion heater">immersion heaters</a>, an external heat exchanger such as a <a href="/wiki/Central_heating" title="Central heating">central heating system</a>, or heated water from another energy source such as a <a href="/wiki/Wood-burning_stove" title="Wood-burning stove">wood-burning stove</a>, <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a> system, <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_collector" title="Solar thermal collector">solar collectors</a>, a biomass heat source, or an air, water or earth source <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">heat pump</a> or any combination of the above. The most typical, in the domestic context, is a fossil-fuel burner supplemented by electric immersion elements, whether grid powered or using renewable generation such as wind power as an independent installation or a district heating scheme.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Largely due to the move towards smaller households in developed countries, along with high density housing and high levels of private home ownership, the trend has been increasingly away from maintaining any hot water storage tanks, opting instead for integrated central heating systems supplied by combination boilers that can supply mains pressure hot water on demand.</p>
<p>Water heaters for washing, bathing, or laundry have thermostat controls to regulate the temperature, in the range of 40 to 60&#160;°C (104 to 140&#160;°F), and are connected to the domestic cold water supply.</p>
<p>Where the local water supply has a <a href="/wiki/Hard_water" title="Hard water">high content of dissolved minerals</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>, heating the water causes the minerals to precipitate in the tank (scaling) and a tank may develop leaks due to corrosion after only a few years, a problem exacerbated by dissolved <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> in the water which accelerates <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> of both tank and fittings.</p>
<p>Typically hot water storage tanks are wrapped in <a href="/wiki/Heat_insulation" class="mw-redirect" title="Heat insulation">heat insulation</a> to reduce energy consumption, speed up the heating process, and maintain the desired operating temperature.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Insulation">Insulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_water_storage_tank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Insulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In general terms the more <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">thermal insulation</a> the better, since it reduces standby heat loss. Water heaters are available with various insulation ratings but it is possible to add layers of extra insulation in the form of a blanket or 'jacket' on the outside of an un-insulated, or poorly insulated, water heater to reduce heat loss, to increase energy efficiency and, in extreme conditions, the heater itself might be wholly enclosed in a specially constructed insulated space.</p>
<p>The most commonly available type of water heater blanket is <a href="/wiki/Fiberglass" title="Fiberglass">fiberglass</a> insulation with a <a href="/wiki/Vinyl" class="mw-redirect" title="Vinyl">vinyl</a> film on the outside, the insulating blanket being wrapped around the tank fixed in place with tape or straps.</p>
<p>It is important that the blanket be the right size for the tank, not block air flow or cover safety and drainage valves, the controls, or block airflow through an exhaust vent, if present. In extremely humid locations, adding insulation to an already well-insulated tank may cause <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a> leading to <a href="/wiki/Rust" title="Rust">rust</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mold" title="Mold">mold</a>, or other operational problems so some air flow must be maintained, usually by <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> caused by waste heat, but in particularly humid conditions such ventilation may be fan-assisted.</p>
<p>Most modern water heaters have applied <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">polyurethane</a> foam (PUF) insulation. Where access to the inner tank is a priority (in cases of particularly aggressive minerals or oxygen levels in the local water supply) the PUF can be applied in encapsulated form, allowing the removal of insulation layer for regular integrity checks and if required, repairs to the water tank proper.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_hot_water_storage_tank">Solar hot water storage tank</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_water_storage_tank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Solar hot water storage tank">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Solar_water_heating" title="Solar water heating">solar water heating</a> system, a solar hot water storage tank stores heat from <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_collector" title="Solar thermal collector">solar thermal collectors</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The tank has a built-in <a href="/wiki/Heat-exchanger" class="mw-redirect" title="Heat-exchanger">heat-exchanger</a> to heat domestic cold water. In relatively mild climates, such as the Mediterranean, the (heavily insulated but metal-wrapped) storage tanks are often roof-mounted. All such tanks share the same problems as artificially-heated tanks including limestone deposit and corrosion, and suffer similar reductions in overall efficiency unless scrupulously maintained.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_tank_leakage">Water tank leakage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_water_storage_tank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Water tank leakage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Water heater tanks may be made of <a href="/wiki/Vitreous_enamel" title="Vitreous enamel">vitreous enamel</a>-lined <a href="/wiki/Carbon_steel" title="Carbon steel">carbon steel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>.</p>
<p>While copper and stainless steel domestic hot water tanks are more commonplace in Europe, carbon steel tanks are more common in the United States, where typically the periodic check is neglected, the tank develops a leak whereupon the entire appliance is replaced.<sup id="cite_ref-WHRKills_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHRKills-4">[4]</a></sup> Even when neglected, carbon steel tanks tend to last for a few years more than their manufacturer's warranty, which is typically 3 to 12 years in the US.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Vitreous-lined tanks are much lower in initial cost, and often include one or more <a href="/wiki/Sacrificial_anode" class="mw-redirect" title="Sacrificial anode">sacrificial anode</a> rods designed to protect the tank from perforation caused by <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a><sup id="cite_ref-WHRAnode_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHRAnode-5">[5]</a></sup> made necessary since <a href="/wiki/Water_chlorination" title="Water chlorination">chlorinated</a> water is very corrosive to carbon steel. As it is very nearly impossible to apply any protective coating perfectly (without microscopic cracks or pinhole defects in the protective layer)<sup id="cite_ref-WHRChoose_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHRChoose-6">[6]</a></sup> manufacturers may recommend a periodic check of any sacrificial anode, replacing it when necessary.</p>
<p>Some manufacturers offer an extended warranty kit that includes a replacement anode rod. Because conventional hot water storage tanks can be expected to leak every 5 to 15 years, high-quality installations will include, and most US building/plumbing codes now require, a shallow metal or plastic pan to collect the inevitable seepage when it occurs.</p>
<p>In such installations the pan should be connected to a drain, or plumbed to discharge directly outdoors. (No equivalent requirement exists in most countries requiring stainless steel or copper tanks.) When this is not possible, or uneconomic, an alternative precaution is to install a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_alarm&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Water alarm (page does not exist)">water leakage alarm</a> coupled to an <a href="/wiki/Safety_shutoff_valve" title="Safety shutoff valve">automatic water shutoff</a> valve.</p>
<p>While apparently obvious, the need to install heater tanks in spaces with adequate physical access for inspection and replacement of the long anode rod, and for eventual replacement of the entire tank,<sup id="cite_ref-WHRHoudini_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHRHoudini-7">[7]</a></sup>) is sometimes neglected with the result that an existing tank has been 'walled off' or 'built in' and must be cut up before being removed. In some cases limited demolition is required to install a replacement.</p>
<p>An accepted rule of thumb among plumbers and heating specialists is that any leak from the bottom of the tank will require total replacement of the unit because this type of leak cannot be repaired. However, if the leak is coming from in or around the pipes or taps on the storage tank, then repair is economically feasible.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hot_water_storage_tank_with_closed_water_circuit">Hot water storage tank with closed water circuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_water_storage_tank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hot water storage tank with closed water circuit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This method stores heat in a tank by using external heat-exchangers (coils) that can be directly tapped or used to power other (external) heat-exchangers.</p>
<p>The chief benefit is that by avoiding drawing-off domestic hot water directly the tank is not continually fed with cold water, which in 'hard' water areas reduces the deposit of limescale to whatever is dissolved in the original charge of water plus relatively trivial amounts added to replace losses due to seepage.</p>
<p>An added benefit is reduced oxygen levels in such a closed system, which allows for some relaxation in the requirements for materials used in the hot water storage tank and the closed water circuits, external heat exchangers, and associated pipework.</p>
<p>While an external heat exchanger system used for domestic hot water will inevitably suffer to some degree from mineral deposits, the use of <a href="/wiki/Descaling_agent" title="Descaling agent">descaling agent</a> from time to time extends the life of such a system to the point where it is cheaper than exchanging the hot water storage tank before its useful life is exceeded for want of proper maintenance.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stratified_hot_water_storage_tank_with_closed_water_circuit">Stratified hot water storage tank with closed water circuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_water_storage_tank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Stratified hot water storage tank with closed water circuit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl>
<dd><i>For an illustration - see reference</i><sup id="cite_ref-solvarme_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-solvarme-9">[9]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Another method to store heat in a hot water storage tank has many names: <b>Stratified hot water storage tank with closed water circuit</b>, <b>stratified thermal storage</b>, <b><a href="/wiki/Thermocline" title="Thermocline">thermocline</a> tank</b> and <b>water stratified tank storage</b> but in all cases the significant difference is that pains are taken to maintain the vertical <a href="/wiki/Stratification_(water)" title="Stratification (water)">stratification</a> of the water column, in other words to keep the hot water at the top of the tank while the water at the bottom is at a distinctly lower temperature.</p>
<p>This is desirable in places with a wide climatic range where summer cooling is as important as heating in winter, and entails one or more of the following measures:</p>
<ul>
<li>Different heating and cooling loops must send the heated or cooled water in with as low a velocity as possible. (This necessarily entails heating and cooling loops having velocity controlled pumps and tube ports with the maximum feasible diameter.)</li>
<li>For cooling applications, cool water is sent out from the bottom and warm (return) water is fed in at the top.</li>
<li>Heating applications get hot water out at the top and return cool water to the bottom.</li>
<li>"Stratification-enhancing" devices within the hot water storage tank<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> (but if the water inlet velocity is as low as possible this might not be needed).</li>
<li>A more advanced <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_control_system&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Heat control system (page does not exist)">heat control system</a><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> is required.</li>
</ul>
<p>When a stratified hot water storage tank has closed water circuits, the water temperatures can be up to 90 to 95&#160;°C at the top and 20 to 40&#160;°C at the bottom. Calm, undisturbed water is a relatively poor <a href="/wiki/Heat_conductor" class="mw-redirect" title="Heat conductor">heat conductor</a> when compared to <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">bricks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>.</p>
<p>(Illustrated by a still lake, where the surface water can be comfortably warm for swimming but deeper layers be so cold as to represent a danger to swimmers, the same effect as gives rise to notices in London's city docks warning 'Danger Cold Deep Water).</p>
<p>Accordingly, an arbitrary volume of hot water can be stored, as long as the stratification is kept intact. In this case there must not be vertical metal plates or tubes as they would conduct heat through the water layers, defeating the purpose of stratification. When effectively employed this technique can maintain water as high as 95&#160;°C (i.e. just below boiling) yielding a higher <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a>, and this energy can be stored a long time provided the hot water remains undiluted.</p>
<p>Depending on the purpose of the installations, water exchanges tapping different levels allow water temperatures appropriate to the required use to be selected.<sup id="cite_ref-solvarme_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-solvarme-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In many <a href="/wiki/Solar_heating_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar heating system">solar heating systems</a> the energy parameters can be read as a function of time, from the 'dwell' time necessary to transform daylight into heat, at its peak the maximum hot water temperature near the top of the tank.<sup id="cite_ref-sysdhw_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sysdhw-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dual_element_electric">Dual element electric</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_water_storage_tank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dual element electric">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When flow starts from the uppermost outlet, cold water enters the tank at the bottom. This drop in temperature causes the thermostat to switch on the electric heating element at the bottom of the tank. When the water at the top of the tank is drawn off the hot water at the top is displaced by relatively cooler water, the top thermostat turns the top element on. When the flow stops, the elements stay on until their settings are met.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>While it is common to have the top and bottom thermostats set differently in order to save energy, the fact that hot water rises means the thermostat controlling the upper element should feed the hottest supply, while the lower element the warmest.</p>
<p>If the thermostats in such a system are reversed - warm feed from the top, hot from the center - it may not only affect the energy efficiency of the system, feeding scalding water to a domestic hot water outlet may be dangerous, or if directed to warm-feed washers damage them beyond repair.</p>
