<p><b>Solar radius</b> is a unit of <a href="/wiki/Distance" title="Distance">distance</a> used to express the size of <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> in <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>. The solar <a href="/wiki/Radius" title="Radius">radius</a> is usually defined as the radius to the layer in the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">photosphere</a> where the <a href="/wiki/Optical_depth" title="Optical depth">optical depth</a> equals 2/3:</p>

<p>The solar radius is approximately 695,700 kilometres (432,288 miles), which is about 10 times the average radius of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>, 110 times the radius of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, and 1/215th of an <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">astronomical unit</a>, the distance of the Earth from the Sun. It varies slightly from pole to equator due to its <a href="/wiki/Stellar_rotation" title="Stellar rotation">rotation</a>, which induces an oblateness in the order of 10 parts per million. <i>(See <a href="/wiki/1_gigametre" class="mw-redirect" title="1 gigametre">1 gigametre</a> for similar distances.)</i><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The unmanned <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">SOHO</a> spacecraft was used to measure the radius of the Sun by timing transits of Mercury across the surface during 2003 and 2006. The result was a measured radius of 696,342&#160;±&#160;65 kilometres (432,687&#160;±&#160;40 miles).<sup id="cite_ref-arxiv1203_4898_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arxiv1203_4898-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Haberreiter, Schmutz &amp; Kosovichev (2008)<sup id="cite_ref-Hab2008_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hab2008-3">[3]</a></sup> determined the radius corresponding to the solar photosphere to be 695,660&#160;±&#160;140 kilometres (432,263&#160;±&#160;87 miles). This new value is consistent with helioseismic estimates; the same study showed that previous estimates using inflection point methods had been overestimated by approximately 300&#160;km.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nominal_solar_radius">Nominal solar radius</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_radius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nominal solar radius">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2015, the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a> passed Resolution B3, which defined a set of nominal conversion constants for stellar and planetary <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>. Resolution B3 defined the <i>nominal solar radius</i> (symbol <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" >
  <semantics>
    <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
      <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
        <msubsup>
          <mi>R</mi>
          <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">

          </mrow>
          <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
            <mi>N</mi>
          </mrow>
        </msubsup>
      </mstyle>
    </mrow>
    <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle R_{\odot }^{N}}</annotation>
  </semantics>
</math></span><img src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9db70e1e155bbf32599dea568910e9f6b8d350f" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" aria-hidden="true" style="vertical-align: -1.005ex; width:3.473ex; height:3.176ex;" alt="{\displaystyle R_{\odot }^{N}}" /></span>) to be equal to <i>exactly</i> 695,700&#160;km.<sup id="cite_ref-IAU2015resB3_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IAU2015resB3-4">[4]</a></sup> The nominal values were adopted to help astronomers avoid confusion when quoting stellar radii in units of the Sun's radius, even when future observations will likely refine the Sun's actual photospheric radius (which is currently only known to about the +-100-200&#160;km accuracy).</p>
