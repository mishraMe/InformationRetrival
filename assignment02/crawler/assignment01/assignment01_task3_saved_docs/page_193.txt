			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Green Energy Act 2009</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>The <b>Ontario Green Energy Act</b> (GEA), formally the <b>Green Energy and Green Economy Act, 2009</b>, introduced in the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_legislature" class="mw-redirect" title="Ontario legislature">Ontario legislature</a> on February 23, 2009, is intended to expand <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> production, encourage <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">energy conservation</a> and create <a href="/wiki/Green_job" title="Green job">green jobs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Among many clauses, the GEA is best known for creating a number of <a href="/wiki/Feed-in_tariff" title="Feed-in tariff">feed-in tariff</a> rates for different types of energy sources. Notable among these is the <b>microFIT</b> program for small non-commercial systems under 10 <a href="/wiki/Kilowatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Kilowatt">kilowatts</a>, and <b>FIT</b>, the larger commercial version which covers a number of project types with sizes into the <a href="/wiki/Megawatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Megawatt">megawatts</a>.</p>
<p>The GEA has been highly controversial within Ontario for the high initial tariff rates, up to 80.2 cents/kWh for small systems under microFIT. It has also been controversial outside Ontario due to its "made in Ontario" clauses which demand a certain amount of Ontario labour and manufacturing input in order to receive the tariff rates. Changes to the program and rates, some of them applied retroactively, had added confusion and complaints about the way the program is managed. The GEA was a major issue during the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_general_election,_2011" title="Ontario general election, 2011">2011 provincial elections</a>, with the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Conservative_Party_of_Ontario" title="Progressive Conservative Party of Ontario">PC party</a> threatening to cancel it outright if elected, while the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Liberal_Party" title="Ontario Liberal Party">Liberals</a> supporting it as a primary plank of their re-election platform.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Energy_Act_2009&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Previous_efforts">Previous efforts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Energy_Act_2009&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Previous efforts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Prior to the introduction of the GEA, Ontario had enacted a number of different programs to introduce renewable energy or promote conservation. These included the Energy Conservation Leadership Act, the Energy Efficiency Act and of particular note, November 2006's Renewable Energy Standard Offer Program. The Standard Offer, also known as SOP or RESOP for short, introduced a number of fixed 20-year <a href="/wiki/Feed-in_tariff" title="Feed-in tariff">feed-in tariffs</a> for <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">hydro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_photovoltaics" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar photovoltaics">solar</a> (PV) and <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> projects. RESOP tariffs were relatively low, 42 cents/kWh for PV and 11 cents/kWh for other forms of energy.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> At the time, RESOP was named North America's first true feed-in tariff program.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In practice, it was found that the RESOP program had significant administrative overhead that eroded the value of the program. In order to connect a generation project, the provider had to not only meet expected requirements for the equipment, but also had to apply to various agencies and levels of government for permission to connect to the grid. This process was not streamlined, and often required hundreds of pages of documentation to be submitted to each organization, in the proper sequence. Certain areas of the Ontario distribution grid were also placed off-limits for development, due to load considerations. Even then, there were stakeholders at the municipal level that could block development at any time as part of local building codes.</p>
<p>Due to the presence of fixed costs, including the administrative overhead, RESOP favoured large projects which could distribute these costs. It proved particularly successful for wind power developments, with almost 64% of the RESOP developments being wind, 28% biomass, and the rest a mix of hydro and a tiny amount of solar.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Towards_the_GEA">Towards the GEA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Energy_Act_2009&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Towards the GEA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>RESOP included a built-in two-year review process that started in 2008. During this period, the world-wide industry was also exploring a number of different ways to implement incentive programs. The main contenders were the feed-in tariff system, like RESOP, and emissions-trading systems like the UK's Renewable Obligation or New Jersey's <a href="/wiki/Solar_Renewable_Energy_Certificate" title="Solar Renewable Energy Certificate">Solar Renewable Energy Certificate</a>. In 2008, <a href="/wiki/Ernst_%26_Young" title="Ernst &amp; Young">Ernst &amp; Young</a> published <i>Renewable energy country attractiveness indices for the first quarter of 2008</i>, which demonstrated that Germany's FIT program was far more successful, delivering more power at lower costs. An earlier report from <a href="/wiki/UC_Berkeley" class="mw-redirect" title="UC Berkeley">UC Berkeley</a> demonstrated that job creation with renewables was far higher than fossil fuels, another argument in favour of the German-style program, which was then considered a great success.<sup id="cite_ref-rec_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rec-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the success of FIT programs, and in response to RESOP issues, a number of stakeholders suggested an expansion of the program with higher rates and various changes to the connection process to simplify the workload. In particular, a number of proposals suggested adding additional classes for very small systems that would have minimal impact on the grid that could be given an express application process and pre-authorized access to connect. These systems would also be given much higher tariffs, in order to offset basic implementation details, like metering, that are often a fixed cost no matter the project size.<sup id="cite_ref-rec_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rec-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Energy_Act_2009&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The GEA effort was led by <a href="/wiki/George_Smitherman" title="George Smitherman">George Smitherman</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Energy_(Ontario)" title="Ministry of Energy (Ontario)">Minister of Energy and Infrastructure</a>. It was claimed that the bill will help the government ensure the province’s future by:</p>
<ul>
<li>Supporting and expanding economic investment, thus building a stronger, greener economy with an estimated 50,000+ direct and indirect jobs over the next three years</li>
<li>Expanding Ontario’s use of clean and renewable sources of energy such as wind, solar, biomass and biogas</li>
<li>Better protecting the environment, combating climate change and creating a healthier future for generations to come.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Energy_Act_2009&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Among the key features<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> of the Act are the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Different levels of tariff<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> are set for electricity from various renewable sources (solar photovoltaic, biogas, <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Landfill_gas" title="Landfill gas">landfill gas</a>, on-shore and off-shore wind and water power) that is "fed in" to the electrical grid; these are known as "<a href="/wiki/Feed-in_tariff" title="Feed-in tariff">Feed in Tariffs</a>", and are based on similar tariffs implemented in Europe</li>
<li>In order to qualify for the program, minimum levels of Ontario content are required in materials and labour<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Local electrical distribution companies (LDCs) were obligated to accept small generators into their systems, and given a set of standard regulations for systems under 10&#160;kW (microFIT systems), and a variety of other sizes, depending on the technology involved</li>
<li>Smaller systems, and in particular microFIT systems, were guaranteed a simpler application procedure and faster turn-around time.</li>
<li>The contract for payment of tariffs is for 20 years (40 years in the case of hydro generators), and is with the Ontario Power Authority, providing small generators with protection against changes in government policies</li>
</ul>
<p>Several non-profit organizations have been active in supporting the implementation of the Green Energy Act with customized wikis to assist consumers and service providers, including SWITCH and OurPower (see Further Reading).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="MicroFIT">MicroFIT</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Energy_Act_2009&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: MicroFIT">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">For the econometrics software package, see <a href="/wiki/Microfit" title="Microfit">Microfit</a>.</div>
<p><b>MicroFIT</b><sup id="cite_ref-microfit_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-microfit-10">[10]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> <a href="/wiki/Microgeneration" title="Microgeneration">microgeneration</a> program (less than 10 <a href="/wiki/Kilowatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Kilowatt">kW</a>) in the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Ontario" class="mw-redirect" title="Province of Ontario">Province of Ontario</a>, launched in October 2009 following the Green Energy Act 2009, alongside <a href="/wiki/Feed-in_tariff" title="Feed-in tariff">feed-in tariff</a> (FIT) to provide incentives for landowners to generate <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_Canada" title="Wind power in Canada">wind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_Canada" title="Solar power in Canada">solar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity_in_Canada" title="Hydroelectricity in Canada">hydroelectric</a> or other clean energy to sell to the <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">electrical grid</a>. Most applications for microFIT generation have been for solar energy.</p>
<p>By December 16, 2009, microFIT issued its first 700 contracts.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">Benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Energy_Act_2009&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Solar_photovoltaic" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar photovoltaic">solar photovoltaic</a> companies have become involved in microFIT installations; the solar panels are also suitable for schools and places of worship.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The projects benefit home, farm or business owners by revenue generation, reduce <a href="/wiki/Canada_and_the_Kyoto_Protocol" title="Canada and the Kyoto Protocol">greenhouse gas emissions</a>, and approved applicants are guaranteed to receive revenue for twenty years.<sup id="cite_ref-white_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-white-13">[13]</a></sup> The Green Energy Act aims to create 50,000 new <a href="/wiki/Green-collar_worker" title="Green-collar worker">green-collar</a> jobs by 2012.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Problems_and_challenges">Problems and challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Energy_Act_2009&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Problems and challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Installation costs often exceed $50,000 <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Dollar" class="mw-redirect" title="Canadian Dollar">CAN</a>, and only about 9,000<sup id="cite_ref-white_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-white-13">[13]</a></sup> projects have connected to the grid by late 2011 out of over 42,000 applicants. Concerns over <a href="/wiki/Islanding" title="Islanding">islanding</a>, lack of capacity and other problems have resulted in delays for many homeowners in installation, as connection points in some places have not been implemented.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Cuts to pay rates generated further controversy.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_electricity_price">Effect on electricity price</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Energy_Act_2009&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Effect on electricity price">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The signing of the GEA has corresponded with a dramatic increase in the Provincial Adjustment(now called the Global Adjustment). This is the cost added to the market price of electricity in Ontario.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> However, a June 2012 report by Bridgepoint Group Ltd. claimed most of this cost increase has been coincidental, rather than attributable to the fixed price for renewable energy.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Likewise, the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Energy_Board" title="Ontario Energy Board">Ontario Energy Board</a> claimed in April 2012 that the highest generator of the increased cost of electricity is guaranteed payments to nuclear generators, followed, by gas, coal, and hydro generation.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In March 2011, the Ontario Government claimed that the combined cost of new renewable and conservation measures had been shown to account for only about 3% of total electricity cost in Ontario, although it was acknowledged that amount will rise as more green energy comes on line in future years.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Energy_Act_2009&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the Green Energy Act promised to create 50,000 jobs, the Liberals admitted in 2013 that the act had only created 31,000 jobs. Critics charge that even confirming this number is problematic because the large majority are “indirect” as opposed to “direct” employment. However, a 2011 report by Jim McCarter, Ontario's Auditor General, found that a large majority of these jobs were in construction and would only exist for no more than three years. In addition, McCarter's report also cited <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabriel_Calzada_Alvarez&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gabriel Calzada Alvarez (page does not exist)">controversial reports</a> from other countries which indicate "that for each job created through renewable energy programs, about two to four jobs are often lost in other sectors of the economy because of higher electricity prices" which has led critics to charge that the Green Energy Act has actually resulted in a net-loss of employment.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 2013 study by the conservative Fraser Institute stated that "wind power tends to be produced at times when it is least needed," and pointed out that:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">Eighty percent of Ontario’s generation of electricity from wind power occurs at times and seasons so far out of phase with demand that the entire output is surplus and is exported at a substantial loss...The Auditor-General of Ontario estimates that the province has already lost close to $2-billion on such exports.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>The price of exports is determined by allowing electricity producers to bid on export offers at the price at which they are willing to produce it.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The quality of the "green" manufacturing jobs created in Ontario has also been questioned. Writing in the <i><a href="/wiki/National_Post" title="National Post">National Post</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/John_Ivison" title="John Ivison">John Ivison</a> noted in 2011 that Eclipsall Energy Corp, a newly created company in Scarborough, Ontario, that was touted by Premier Dalton McGuinty, only pays its workers 20% above minimum wage and that its manufacturing facility simply "assemble[s] glass and solar cells imported from Asia" (which allows it to qualify for the local content rules).<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_developments">Recent developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Energy_Act_2009&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Recent developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In May 2013, Ontario Energy Minister <a href="/wiki/Bob_Chiarelli" title="Bob Chiarelli">Bob Chiarelli</a> announced that the made-in-Ontario content requirements for wind and solar projects would be scrapped, after the <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organization" title="World Trade Organization">World Trade Organization</a> (WTO) ruled that they were a violation of WTO regulations.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2013, Chiarelli announced that the province's future Feed-in-Tariff contacts will now be limited to smaller projects (less than 500&#160;kW) and that a cap of 900 MW of additional capacity will be set for deployment by 2018. For larger projects, a competitive bidding system will be created, although projects that have already been contracted will not be affected by these changes.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2013, Chiarelli announced that the remaining local content requirements which had already been reduced, would be removed entirely some time in 2014 in order to comply with the WTO decision. As a result, concerns have been expressed that this will lead to the loss of "green" manufacturing jobs in Ontario.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In February 2014, Jeff Garrah, CEO of Kingston’s Economic Development Commission (KEDCO), cited the removal of the local content requirements as the reason for the bankruptcy of Centennial Global Technology Inc., which in November 2013 had been described as Canada's "leading national solar panel distributor." It was reported that the removal of the local content rules had effectively "pull[ed] the plug on local production." Describing the Green Energy Act as "an ongoing soap opera," Garrah stated that municipalities across Ontario have become dismayed with how its solar and wind power provisions have been implemented.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
