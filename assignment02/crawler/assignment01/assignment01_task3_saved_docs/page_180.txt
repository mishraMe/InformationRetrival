<p><b>Austin Energy</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Publicly_owned_utility" class="mw-redirect" title="Publicly owned utility">publicly owned utility</a> providing <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electrical power</a> to the city of <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Texas" title="Austin, Texas">Austin, Texas</a> and surrounding areas. Established in 1895, the utility is a department of the City of Austin and returns its profits to the city's <a href="/wiki/General_fund" class="mw-redirect" title="General fund">general fund</a> to finance other city services. Austin Energy is the United States' 8th largest public utility, serving more than 440,000 customers and more than one million residents (as of 2015) within a service area of approximately 437 square miles (1,130&#160;km<sup>2</sup>), including Austin, <a href="/wiki/Travis_County,_Texas" title="Travis County, Texas">Travis County</a> and a small portion of <a href="/wiki/Williamson_County,_Texas" title="Williamson County, Texas">Williamson County</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_generation">Energy generation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Austin_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Energy generation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Austin Energy’s total generation capacity is more than 3,000 megawatts (MW), provided by a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-assets_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-assets-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Generation_assets">Generation assets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Austin_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Generation assets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Austin Energy owns and operates two <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>-fired power plants in the Austin area: the Decker Creek Power Station and the Sand Hill Energy Center. The utility also owns 50% of units 1 and 2 at the <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>-fired Fayette Power Project in <a href="/wiki/La_Grange,_Texas" title="La Grange, Texas">La Grange</a> and 16% of the <a href="/wiki/South_Texas_Nuclear_Generating_Station" title="South Texas Nuclear Generating Station">South Texas Nuclear Project</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bay_City,_Texas" title="Bay City, Texas">Bay City</a> (near <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-assets_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-assets-2">[2]</a></sup> The STNP was the subject of a binding citizen referendum (November 3, 1981) to sell Austin's part in the project. STNP went online in 1986. No council has sold Austin's STNP telling citizens that "no one wanted our 16 percent".</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewable_energy">Renewable energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Austin_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Renewable energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As of July 2014, <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> represents roughly 23% of Austin Energy's generation portfolio, including solar, wind, landfill methane and biomass projects.<sup id="cite_ref-renewables_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-renewables-3">[3]</a></sup> The utility's 2014 generation plan indicated that it aims to produce 50% of power from renewable sources and 75% from carbon-free sources by 2025.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_conservation">Energy conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Austin_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Energy conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Austin Energy operates an <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">energy efficiency</a> program for customers, including a free "energy audit" that helps to identify ways users can reduce power consumption. The utility offers various subsidies and rebates for efficiency improvements, including <a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">HVAC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Building_insulation" title="Building insulation">insulation</a>, efficient <a href="/wiki/Lighting" title="Lighting">lighting</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" class="mw-redirect" title="Photovoltaic">photovoltaic</a> panels.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1992 Austin Energy developed the nation's first local <a href="/wiki/Green_Building" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Building">Green Building</a> program.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It shares the distinction of being the largest and best established green building program in the country along with Built Green Colorado in <a href="/wiki/Denver,_Colorado" class="mw-redirect" title="Denver, Colorado">Denver</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_vehicle_program">Electric vehicle program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Austin_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Electric vehicle program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Austin Energy’s <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://austinenergy.com/wps/portal/ae/programs/plug-in-austin/">Plug-In EVerywhere</a> network, powered by 100% renewable energy, expanded to a total of 186 public charging stations to help drive a two-year, 300% Austin EV growth rate. Austin Energy led a 10-county, regional effort to develop a community plan that supports the adoption of EVs and successfully deployed the first-of-its-kind EV home charging <a href="/wiki/Demand_response" title="Demand response">Demand Response</a> program.</p>
