<p>In <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, <b>luminosity</b> is the total amount of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> emitted by a <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxy</a>, or other <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">astronomical object</a> per unit time.<sup id="cite_ref-Hopkins1980_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins1980-1">[1]</a></sup> It is related to the brightness, which is the luminosity of an object in a given spectral region.<sup id="cite_ref-Hopkins1980_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins1980-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/SI" class="mw-redirect" title="SI">SI</a> units luminosity is measured in <a href="/wiki/Joules" class="mw-redirect" title="Joules">joules</a> per second or <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watts</a>. Values for luminosity are often given in the terms of the luminosity of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, which has a total power output of <span class="nowrap"><span style="display:none" class="sortkey">7026384600000000000♠</span>3.846<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>26</sup>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">W</a></span>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The symbol for <a href="/wiki/Solar_luminosity" title="Solar luminosity">solar luminosity</a> is L<sub>⊙</sub>. Luminosity can also be given in terms of magnitude. The <a href="/wiki/Absolute_magnitude#Bolometric_magnitude" title="Absolute magnitude">absolute bolometric magnitude</a> (M<sub>bol</sub>) of an object is a logarithmic measure of its total energy emission.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measuring_luminosity">Measuring luminosity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luminosity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Measuring luminosity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hertzsprung-Russel_StarData.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Hertzsprung-Russel_StarData.png/230px-Hertzsprung-Russel_StarData.png" width="230" height="243" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Hertzsprung-Russel_StarData.png/345px-Hertzsprung-Russel_StarData.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Hertzsprung-Russel_StarData.png/460px-Hertzsprung-Russel_StarData.png 2x" data-file-width="567" data-file-height="600" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hertzsprung-Russel_StarData.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Hertzsprung%E2%80%93Russell_diagram" title="Hertzsprung–Russell diagram">Hertzsprung–Russell diagram</a> identifying stellar luminosity as a function of temperature for many stars in our solar neighborhood.</div>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, <i>luminosity</i> is the amount of <a href="/wiki/Radiant_energy" title="Radiant energy">electromagnetic energy</a> a body radiates per unit of time.<sup id="cite_ref-AUSTRALIA2004_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AUSTRALIA2004-3">[3]</a></sup> It is most frequently measured in two forms: <i>visual</i> (visible light only) and <i>bolometric</i> (total radiant energy),<sup id="cite_ref-COSMOS1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-COSMOS1-4">[4]</a></sup> although luminosities at other wavelengths are increasingly being used as instruments become available to measure them. A <a href="/wiki/Bolometer" title="Bolometer">bolometer</a> is the instrument used to measure <a href="/wiki/Radiant_energy" title="Radiant energy">radiant energy</a> over a wide band by <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation)" title="Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)">absorption</a> and measurement of heating. When not qualified, the term "luminosity" means bolometric luminosity, which is measured either in the <a href="/wiki/SI" class="mw-redirect" title="SI">SI</a> units, <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watts</a>, or in terms of <a href="/wiki/Solar_luminosity" title="Solar luminosity">solar luminosities</a>. A star also radiates <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrinos</a>, which carry off some energy (about 2% in the case of our Sun), contributing to the star's total luminosity.<sup id="cite_ref-BAHCALL1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BAHCALL1-5">[5]</a></sup> While bolometers do exist, they cannot be used to measure even the apparent brightness of a star because they are insufficiently sensitive across the electromagnetic spectrum and because most wavelengths do not reach the surface of the Earth. In practice bolometric magnitudes are measured by taking measurements at certain wavelengths and constructing a model of the total spectrum that is most likely to match those measurements. In some cases, the process of estimation is extreme, with luminosities being calculated when less than 1% of the energy output is observed, for example with a hot <a href="/wiki/Wolf-Rayet_star" class="mw-redirect" title="Wolf-Rayet star">Wolf-Rayet star</a> observed only in the infra-red.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stellar_luminosity">Stellar luminosity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luminosity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Stellar luminosity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A star's luminosity can be determined from two stellar characteristics: size and <a href="/wiki/Effective_temperature" title="Effective temperature">effective temperature</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AUSTRALIA2004_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AUSTRALIA2004-3">[3]</a></sup> The former is typically represented in terms of solar <a href="/wiki/Radius" title="Radius">radii</a>, R<sub>⊙</sub>, while the latter is represented in <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvins</a>, but in most cases neither can be measured directly. To determine a star's radius, two other metrics are needed: the star's <a href="/wiki/Angular_diameter" title="Angular diameter">angular diameter</a> and its distance from Earth, often calculated using <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">parallax</a>. Both can be measured with great accuracy in certain cases, with cool supergiants often having large angular diameters, and some cool evolved stars having <a href="/wiki/Astrophysical_maser" title="Astrophysical maser">masers</a> in their atmospheres that can be used to measure the parallax using <a href="/wiki/VLBI" class="mw-redirect" title="VLBI">VLBI</a>. However, for most stars the angular diameter or parallax, or both, are far below our ability to measure with any certainty. Since the effective temperature is merely a number that represents the temperature of a black body that would reproduce the luminosity, it obviously cannot be measured directly, but it can be estimated from the spectrum.</p>
<p>An alternate way to measure stellar luminosity is to measure the star's apparent brightness and distance. A third component needed to derive the luminosity is the degree of <a href="/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)" title="Extinction (astronomy)">interstellar extinction</a> that is present, a condition that usually arises because of gas and dust present in the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a> (ISM), the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Circumstellar_dust" title="Circumstellar dust">circumstellar matter</a>. Consequently, one of astronomy's central challenges in determining a star's luminosity is to derive accurate measurements for each of these components, without which an accurate luminosity figure remains elusive.<sup id="cite_ref-KARTTUNEN1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KARTTUNEN1-6">[6]</a></sup> Extinction can only be measured directly if the actual and observed luminosities are both known, but it can be estimated from the observed colour of a star, using models of the expected level of reddening from the interstellar medium.</p>
<p>In the current system of <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">stellar classification</a>, stars are grouped according to temperature, with the massive, very young and energetic <a href="/wiki/O-type_main_sequence_star" class="mw-redirect" title="O-type main sequence star">Class O</a> stars boasting temperatures in excess of 30,000&#160;<a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a> while the less massive, typically older <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification#Class_M" title="Stellar classification">Class M</a> stars exhibit temperatures less than 3,500&#160;K. Because luminosity is proportional to temperature to the fourth power, the large variation in stellar temperatures produces an even vaster variation in stellar luminosity.<sup id="cite_ref-LEDREW1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LEDREW1-7">[7]</a></sup> Because the luminosity depends on a high power of the stellar mass, high mass luminous stars have much shorter lifetimes. The most luminous stars are always young stars, no more than a few million years for the most extreme. In the <a href="/wiki/Hertzsprung%E2%80%93Russell_diagram" title="Hertzsprung–Russell diagram">Hertzsprung–Russell diagram</a>, the x-axis represents temperature or spectral type while the y-axis represents luminosity or magnitude. The vast majority of stars are found along the <a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">main sequence</a> with blue Class 0 stars found at the top left of the chart while red Class M stars fall to the bottom right. Certain stars like <a href="/wiki/Deneb" title="Deneb">Deneb</a> and <a href="/wiki/Betelgeuse" title="Betelgeuse">Betelgeuse</a> are found above and to the right of the main sequence, more luminous or cooler than their equivalents on the main sequence. Increased luminosity at the same temperature, or alternatively cooler temperature at the same luminosity, indicates that these stars are larger than those on the main sequence and they are called giants or supergiants.</p>
<p>Blue and white supergiants are high luminosity stars somewhat cooler than the most luminous main sequence stars. A star like Deneb, for example, has a luminosity around 200,000 L<sub>⊙</sub>, a spectral type of A2, and an effective temperature around 8,500&#160;K, meaning it has a radius around 203 R<sub>⊙</sub>. For comparison, the red supergiant Betelgeuse has a luminosity around 100,000 L<sub>⊙</sub>, a spectral type of M2, and a temperature around 3,500&#160;K, meaning its radius is about 1,000 R<sub>⊙</sub>. Red supergiants are the largest type of star, but the most luminous are much smaller and hotter, with temperatures up to 50,000&#160;K and more and luminosities of several million L<sub>⊙</sub>, meaning their radii are just a few tens of R<sub>⊙</sub>. An example is <a href="/wiki/R136a1" title="R136a1">R136a1</a>, over 50,000&#160;K and shining at over 8,000,000 L<sub>⊙</sub> (mostly in the UV), it is only 35 R<sub>⊙</sub>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio_luminosity">Radio luminosity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luminosity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Radio luminosity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The luminosity of a <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_radio_source" title="Astronomical radio source">radio source</a> is measured in <span class="texhtml">W Hz<sup>−1</sup></span>, to avoid having to specify a <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(signal_processing)" title="Bandwidth (signal processing)">bandwidth</a> over which it is measured. The observed strength, or <a href="/wiki/Flux_density" class="mw-redirect" title="Flux density">flux density</a>, of a radio source is measured in <a href="/wiki/Jansky" title="Jansky">Jansky</a> where <span class="texhtml">1 Jy = 10<sup>−26</sup> W m<sup>−2</sup> Hz<sup>−1</sup></span>.</p>
<p>For example, consider a 10W transmitter at a distance of 1 million metres, radiating over a bandwidth of 1&#160;MHz. By the time that power has reached the observer, the power is spread over the surface of a sphere with area <span class="texhtml">4<span class="texhtml">π</span><i>r</i><sup>2</sup></span> or about <span class="texhtml">1.26×10<sup>13</sup> m<sup>2</sup></span>, so its flux density is <span class="texhtml">10 / 10<sup>6</sup> / 1.26×10<sup>13</sup> W m<sup>−2</sup> Hz<sup>−1</sup> = 10<sup>8</sup> Jy</span>.</p>
<p>More generally, for sources at cosmological distances, a <a href="/wiki/K-correction" class="mw-redirect" title="K-correction">k-correction</a> must be made for the spectral index α of the source, and a relativistic correction must be made for the fact that the frequency scale in the emitted <a href="/wiki/Rest_frame" title="Rest frame">rest frame</a> is different from that in the observer's <a href="/wiki/Rest_frame" title="Rest frame">rest frame</a>. <p>Luminosity is an intrinsic measurable property of a star independent of distance. The concept of magnitude, on the other hand, incorporates distance. First conceived by the Greek astronomer <a href="/wiki/Hipparchus" title="Hipparchus">Hipparchus</a> in the second century BC, the original concept of magnitude grouped stars into six discrete categories depending on how bright they appeared. The brightest first magnitude stars were twice as bright as the next brightest stars, which were second magnitude; second was twice as bright as third, third twice as bright as fourth and so on down to the faintest stars, which Hipparchus categorized as sixth magnitude.<sup id="cite_ref-KNOWLE2001_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KNOWLE2001-8">[8]</a></sup> The system was but a simple delineation of stellar brightness into six distinct groups and made no allowance for the variations in brightness within a group. With the invention of the <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescope</a> at the beginning of the seventeenth century, researchers soon realized that there were subtle variations among stars and millions fainter than the sixth magnitude—hence the need for a more sophisticated system to describe a continuous range of values beyond what the naked eye could see.<sup id="cite_ref-KNOWLE2001_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KNOWLE2001-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ASTRONOTES1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASTRONOTES1-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1856 <a href="/wiki/Norman_Pogson" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Pogson">Norman Pogson</a>, noticing that <a href="/wiki/Photometry_(astronomy)" title="Photometry (astronomy)">photometric</a> measurements had established first magnitude stars as being about 100 times brighter than sixth magnitude stars, formalized the Hipparchus system by creating a <a href="/wiki/Logarithmic_scale" title="Logarithmic scale">logarithmic scale</a>, with every interval of one magnitude equating to a variation in brightness of 100<sup>1/5</sup> or roughly 2.512 times. Consequently, a first magnitude star is about 2.5 times brighter than a second magnitude star, 2.5<sup>2</sup> brighter than a third magnitude star, 2.5<sup>3</sup> brighter than a fourth magnitude star, et cetera. Based on this continuous scale, any star with a magnitude between 5.5 and 6.5 is now considered to be sixth magnitude, a star with a magnitude between 4.5 and 5.5 is fifth magnitude and so on. With this new mathematical rigor, a first magnitude star should then have a magnitude in the range 0.5 to 1.5, thus excluding the <a href="/wiki/List_of_brightest_stars" title="List of brightest stars">nine brightest stars</a> with magnitudes lower than 0.5, as well as the four brightest with negative values. It is customary therefore to extend the definition of a first magnitude star to any star with a magnitude less than 0.5, as can be seen in accompanying table.<sup id="cite_ref-KNOWLE2001_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KNOWLE2001-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Pogson logarithmic scale is used to measure both apparent and absolute magnitudes, the latter corresponding to the brightness of a star or other <a href="/wiki/Celestial_body" class="mw-redirect" title="Celestial body">celestial body</a> as seen if it would be located at an interstellar distance of 10 <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsecs</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitude</a> is a measure of the diminishing flux of light as a result of distance according to the <a href="/wiki/Inverse-square_law" title="Inverse-square law">inverse-square law</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HAWAII2003_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HAWAII2003-11">[11]</a></sup> In addition to this brightness decrease from increased distance, there is an extra decrease of brightness due to extinction from intervening interstellar dust.<sup id="cite_ref-ASTRONOTES1_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASTRONOTES1-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>By measuring the width of certain absorption lines in the <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">stellar spectrum</a>, it is often possible to assign a certain luminosity class to a star without knowing its distance. Thus a fair measure of its absolute magnitude can be determined without knowing its distance nor the interstellar extinction, allowing astronomers to estimate a star's distance and extinction without <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">parallax</a> calculations. Since the <a href="/wiki/Stellar_parallax" title="Stellar parallax">stellar parallax</a> is usually too small to be measured for many distant stars, this is a common method of determining such distances.</p>
<p>To conceptualize the range of magnitudes in our own <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">galaxy</a>, the smallest star to be identified has about 8% of the Sun’s mass and glows feebly at absolute magnitude +19. Compared to the Sun, which has an absolute of +4.8, this faint star is 14 magnitudes or 400,000 times dimmer than our Sun. Our galaxy's most massive stars begin their lives with masses of roughly 100 times solar, radiating at upwards of absolute magnitude –8, over 160,000 times the solar luminosity. The total range of stellar luminosities, then, occupies a range of 27 magnitudes, or a factor of 60 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-LEDREW1_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LEDREW1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In measuring star brightnesses, absolute magnitude, apparent magnitude, and distance are interrelated parameters—if two are known, the third can be determined. Since the Sun's luminosity is the standard, comparing these parameters with the Sun's apparent magnitude and distance is the easiest way to remember how to convert between them.</p>
