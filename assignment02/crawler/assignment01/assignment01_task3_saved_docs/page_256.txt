			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Sodium–sulfur battery</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

<p>A <b>sodium–sulfur battery</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Molten_salt_battery" title="Molten salt battery">molten-salt</a> <a href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">battery</a> constructed from liquid <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> (Na) and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> (S).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bland_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bland-2">[2]</a></sup> This type of battery has a high <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a>, high efficiency of charge/discharge (89–92%) and <a href="/wiki/Charge_cycle" title="Charge cycle">long cycle life</a>, and is fabricated from inexpensive materials. The operating temperatures of 300 to 350&#160;°C and the highly <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosive</a> nature of the <a href="/wiki/Polysulfide" title="Polysulfide">sodium polysulfides</a>, primarily make them suitable for stationary energy storage applications. The cell becomes more economical with increasing size.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium%E2%80%93sulfur_battery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Typical batteries have a solid <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolyte</a> membrane between the <a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">anode</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cathode" title="Cathode">cathode</a>, compared with liquid-metal batteries where the anode, the cathode and the membrane are liquids.<sup id="cite_ref-Bland_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bland-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_cell" title="Electrochemical cell">cell</a> is usually made in a cylindrical configuration. The entire cell is enclosed by a steel casing that is protected, usually by <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a>, from corrosion on the inside. This outside container serves as the positive electrode, while the liquid sodium serves as the negative electrode. The container is sealed at the top with an airtight <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">alumina</a> lid. An essential part of the cell is the presence of a BASE (<a href="/wiki/Beta-alumina_solid_electrolyte" title="Beta-alumina solid electrolyte">beta-alumina solid electrolyte</a>) membrane, which selectively conducts Na<sup>+</sup>. In commercial applications the cells are arranged in blocks for better heat conservation and are encased in a vacuum-insulated box.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation">Operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium%E2%80%93sulfur_battery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the discharge phase, <a href="/wiki/Molten" class="mw-redirect" title="Molten">molten</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">elemental</a> sodium at the core serves as the <a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">anode</a>, meaning that the <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">Na</a> donates electrons to the external circuit. The sodium is separated by a <a href="/wiki/Beta-alumina_solid_electrolyte" title="Beta-alumina solid electrolyte">beta-alumina solid electrolyte</a> (BASE) cylinder from the container of molten sulfur, which is fabricated from an <a href="/wiki/Inert" title="Inert">inert</a> metal serving as the <a href="/wiki/Cathode" title="Cathode">cathode</a>. The sulfur is absorbed in a <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> sponge. BASE is a good conductor of sodium <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a>, but a poor conductor of electrons, and thus avoids self-discharge. When sodium gives off an <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>, the Na<sup>+</sup> ion migrates to the sulfur container. The electron drives an electric current through the molten sodium to the contact, through the electrical load and back to the sulfur container. Here, another electron reacts with sulfur to form S<sub>n</sub><sup>2−</sup>, sodium <a href="/wiki/Polysulfide" title="Polysulfide">polysulfide</a>. The discharge process can be represented as follows:</p>
<dl>
<dd>2 Na + 4 S → Na<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> (E<sub>cell</sub> ~ 2 V)</dd>
</dl>
<p>As the cell discharges, the sodium level drops. During the charging phase the reverse process takes place. Once running, the heat produced by charging and discharging cycles is sufficient to maintain operating temperatures and usually no external source is required.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium%E2%80%93sulfur_battery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pure <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> presents a hazard, because it spontaneously burns in contact with air and moisture, thus the system must be protected from water and oxidizing atmospheres.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2011_Tsukuba_Plant_fire_incident">2011 Tsukuba Plant fire incident</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium%E2%80%93sulfur_battery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2011 Tsukuba Plant fire incident">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On September 21, 2011, <a href="/wiki/NGK_Insulators" title="NGK Insulators">NGK</a>-manufactured NaS batteries for storing electricity installed at the <a href="/wiki/Tsukuba,_Ibaraki" title="Tsukuba, Ibaraki">Tsukuba, Japan</a>, plant caught fire. Following the incident, NGK temporarily suspended production of NaS batteries.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium%E2%80%93sulfur_battery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium%E2%80%93sulfur_battery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford Motor Company</a> pioneered the <a href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">battery</a> in the 1960s to power early-model <a href="/wiki/Electric_cars" class="mw-redirect" title="Electric cars">electric cars</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2009<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sodium%E2%80%93sulfur_battery&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, a lower temperature, solid electrode version was under development in <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a> by Ceramatec. They use a <a href="/wiki/NASICON" title="NASICON">NASICON</a> membrane to allow operation at 90&#160;°C with all components remaining solid.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><span>In 2014 researchers identified a liquid sodium-cesium alloy that operates at 150&#160;°C and produces 420 milliampere-hours per gram. The material fully coated ("wetted") the electrolyte. After 100 charge/discharge cycles, a test battery maintained about 97% of its initial storage capacity. The lower operating temperature allowed the use of a less-expensive polymer external casing instead of steel, offsetting some of the increased cost associated with using cesium.</span><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium%E2%80%93sulfur_battery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The NaS battery was one of four battery types selected as candidates for intensive research by <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_International_Trade_and_Industry" title="Ministry of International Trade and Industry">MITI</a> as part of the "Moonlight Project" in 1980. This project sought to develop a durable utility power storage device meeting the criteria shown below in a 10-year project.</p>
<ul>
<li>1,000&#160;kW class</li>
<li>8 hour charge/8 hour discharge at rated load</li>
<li>Efficiency of 70% or better</li>
<li>Lifetime of 1,500 cycles or better</li>
</ul>
<p>The other three were improved <a href="/wiki/Lead%E2%80%93acid_battery" title="Lead–acid battery">lead–acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vanadium_redox_battery" title="Vanadium redox battery">redox flow (vanadium type)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zinc%E2%80%93bromine_battery" title="Zinc–bromine battery">zinc-bromide batteries</a>.</p>
<p>A consortium formed by TEPCO (<a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Electric_Power" class="mw-redirect" title="Tokyo Electric Power">Tokyo Electric Power</a> Co.) and NGK (<a href="/wiki/NGK_Insulators" title="NGK Insulators">NGK Insulators</a> Ltd.) declared their interest in researching the NaS battery in 1983, and became the primary drivers behind the development of this type ever since. TEPCO chose the NaS battery because all its component elements (sodium, sulfur and ceramics) are abundant in Japan. The first large-scale field testing took place at TEPCO's Tsunashima substation between 1993 and 1996, using 3 x 2&#160;MW, 6.6 kV battery banks. Based on the findings from this trial, improved battery modules were developed and were made commercially available in 2000. The commercial NaS battery bank offers:<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Capacity&#160;: 25–250&#160;kW per bank</li>
<li>Efficiency of 87%</li>
<li>Lifetime of 2,500 cycles at 100% depth of discharge (DOD), or 4,500 cycles at 80% DOD</li>
</ul>
<p>As of 2008, sodium–sulfur batteries were manufactured by one group, the NGK/TEPCO consortium, which produced 90 MW of storage capacity each year.<sup id="cite_ref-Yomogita_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yomogita-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>A demonstration project used NaS battery at Japan Wind Development Co.’s Miura Wind Park in Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Japan Wind Development opened a 51 MW wind farm that incorporates a 34 MW sodium sulfur battery system at Futamata in Aomori Prefecture in May 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-Yomogita_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yomogita-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2007, 165 MW of capacity were installed in Japan. NGK announced in 2008 a plan to expand its NaS factory output from 90 MW a year to 150 MW a year.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010 <a href="/wiki/Xcel_Energy" title="Xcel Energy">Xcel Energy</a> announced that it would test a wind farm energy storage battery based on twenty 50&#160;kW sodium–sulfur batteries. The 80 tonne, 2 semi-trailer sized battery is expected to have 7.2 MW·h of capacity at a charge and discharge rate of 1 MW.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2011, <a href="/wiki/Sumitomo_Electric_Industries" title="Sumitomo Electric Industries">Sumitomo Electric Industries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_University" title="Kyoto University">Kyoto University</a> announced that they had developed a low temperature molten sodium ion battery that can output power at under 100&#160;°C. The batteries have double the energy density of Li-ion and considerably lower cost. Sumitomo Electric Industry CEO Masayoshi Matsumoto indicated that the company planned to begin production in 2015. Initial applications are envisaged to be buildings and buses.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges">Challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium%E2%80%93sulfur_battery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Corrosion of the insulators was found to be a problem in the harsh chemical environment as they gradually became conductive and the self-discharge rate increased. <a href="/wiki/Dendrite_(metal)" title="Dendrite (metal)">Dendritic</a>-sodium growth can also be a problem.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium%E2%80%93sulfur_battery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grid_and_standalone_systems">Grid and standalone systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium%E2%80%93sulfur_battery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Grid and standalone systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Grid_energy_storage" title="Grid energy storage">Grid energy storage</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Stand_Alone_Power_System" class="mw-redirect" title="Stand Alone Power System">Stand Alone Power System</a></div>
<p>NaS batteries can be deployed to support the electric grid, or for stand-alone renewable power<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> applications. In 2010, <a href="/wiki/Presidio,_Texas" title="Presidio, Texas">Presidio, Texas</a> built the world's largest sodium–sulfur battery, which can provide 4 MW of power for up to eight hours when the city's lone line to the <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">Texas power grid</a> goes down.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Under some market conditions, NaS batteries provide value via energy <a href="/wiki/Arbitrage" title="Arbitrage">arbitrage</a> (charging battery when electricity is abundant/cheap, and discharging into the grid when electricity is more valuable) and <a href="/wiki/Voltage_regulation" title="Voltage regulation">voltage regulation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> NaS batteries are a possible energy storage technology to support renewable energy generation, specifically <a href="/wiki/Wind_farm" title="Wind farm">wind farms</a> and solar generation plants. In the case of a wind farm, the battery would store energy during times of high wind but low power demand. This stored energy could then be discharged from the batteries during <a href="/wiki/Peak_load" class="mw-redirect" title="Peak load">peak load</a> periods. In addition to this power shifting, sodium sulfur batteries could be used to assist in stabilizing the power output of the wind farm during wind fluctuations. These types of batteries present an option for energy storage in locations where other storage options are not feasible. For example, <a href="/wiki/Pumped-storage_hydroelectricity" title="Pumped-storage hydroelectricity">pumped-storage hydroelectricity</a> facilities require significant space and water resources, while compressed air energy storage (<a href="/wiki/Compressed_air_energy_storage" title="Compressed air energy storage">CAES</a>) requires some type of geologic feature such as a salt cave.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Space">Space</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium%E2%80%93sulfur_battery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Space">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because of its high energy density, the NaS battery has been proposed for space applications.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Sodium sulfur cells can be made space-qualified: in fact a test sodium sulfur cell flew on the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>. The NaS flight experiment demonstrated a battery with a specific energy of 150 W·h/kg (3 x nickel–hydrogen battery energy density), operating at 350&#160;°C. It was launched on the <a href="/wiki/STS-87" title="STS-87">STS-87</a> mission in November 1997, and demonstrated 10 days of experimental operation.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport_and_heavy_machinery">Transport and heavy machinery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium%E2%80%93sulfur_battery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Transport and heavy machinery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first large-scale use of sodium–sulfur batteries was in the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Ecostar" title="Ford Ecostar">Ford "Ecostar"</a> demonstration vehicle,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> an <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric vehicle</a> prototype in 1991. The high operating temperature of sodium sulfur batteries presented difficulties for electric vehicle use, however. The Ecostar never went into production.</p>
